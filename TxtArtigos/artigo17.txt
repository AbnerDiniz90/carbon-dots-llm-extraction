### TITLE ###

A novel ultrasensitive and non-enzymatic “turn-on-off” fluorescence nanosensor for direct determination of glucose in the serum: As an alternative approach to the other optical and electrochemical methods


### AUTHOR GROUP ###

Gholamreza Dehghan
Masoomeh Shaghaghi
Pari Alizadeh


### ABSTRACT ###

A new, simple, rapid, highly sensitive and selective and non-enzymatic fluorometric method for direct determination of glucose in real samples was developed. The method was based on the inhibition of fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) process between terbium (III)-1, 10-phenanthroline (Tb-phen) complex and silver nanoparticles (AgNPs). Upon the addition of glucose, the quenched FRET-based fluorescence of Tb-phen complex was gradually recovered by glucose via its strong adsorption on the surface of AgNPs and removal of Tb-phen complex from AgNPs surface. Therefore the fluorescence of Tb-phen complex switched to “turn-on” state. Under the optimum conditions, a linear relationship was obtained between the enhanced fluorescence intensity and glucose concentration in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M with the detection limit of 1.94 × 10−8 M. The proposed sensing system was successfully applied to determine glucose in the spiked normal and diabetic patient serum samples after deproteinization with acetonitrile. Analytical recoveries from treated serum samples were in the range of 99.97–104.80% and 92.14–105.43%, respectively. The common interfering species, such as ascorbic acid, fructose and galactose did not cause interior interference due to unique emission properties of Tb-phen complex probe. Also the interaction of the Tb-phen complex with AgNPs, which led to the fluorescence intensity quenching of the complex, was further examined by FTIR technique. In short, as compared to most of the existing methods, the newly proposed method, provides some advantages and makes it promising for the direct rapid screening of glucose residues of real samples in clinical diagnosis of diabetes, as an alternative approach to the other exiting optical and electrochemical methods.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Glucose, a primary source of energy in our body and the important carbohydrate existing in the serum, is usually used as a clinical indicator of diabetes mellitus based on its level in blood and plays a critical role in the metabolic process by providing energy to the cells and evaluating a person's health conditions [1,2]. The blood glucose level is an important parameter in clinical medicine, as whether it is too high or too low apart from the normal concentration level (3.3–6.1 mM), it causes harmful effect to human health. On the other hand, the presence of glucose in urine can reflect serious kidney diseases [2–4]. The accurate detection of glucose concentration is vital in various fields such as biology, biochemistry, biotechnology, clinical chemistry, textile industry, food analysis and environmental monitoring [1,4–8]. On the basis of the statistics of World Health Organization (WHO), about 422 million people suffered from diabetes in the year 2014 and the number is estimated to be almost double in 2030 [9–11]. Millions of diabetics need to test their blood glucose levels daily; making glucose the most commonly tested analyte for an effective treatment [9,12–14].
Since the glucose sensor was first proposed in 1962, numerous approaches have been introduced to develop suitable techniques for precisely detection the glucose level with high sensitivity, high reliability, good selectivity, good stability, simplicity and low cost [1,4,5,11,12,15,16]. Most of the approaches to glucose sensing can be categorized into two types: optical methods and electrochemical methods. Both of these methods are mostly enzyme-based approaches, which require glucose-oxidase (GOx), an enzyme that can catalyze the oxidation of glucose to gluconolactone. However, the enzyme-based methods despite several benefits such as good selectivity and high sensitivity, due to the nature of enzymes, suffer from a number of limitations including: (1) the storage, fabrication and use of enzymes is difficult as they are easily denatured in the environment or digested by protease (lake of stability overtime); (2) the working conditions of enzymes that are significantly affected by many factors such as temperature, humidity, ionic strength, pH and toxic chemicals, are typically rigorous; (3) the operations of enzymes such as labeling and immobilization are troublesome; (4) the cost of enzymes remains a concern [1,4,5,7,11,17–22].
To overcome these problems, especially with advances in nanoscience and nanotechnology, the non-enzymatic glucose sensors with high functionality in recent years have been developed and are proposed as an alternative for GOx-based sensors.
The electrochemical and optical biosensors utilizing nanomaterials recently attracted considerable attention in the area of sensing [1,3,14,23]. Glucose biosensors based on nanomaterials, such as carbon nanotubes (CNT), metal nanoparticles (MNPs) including various metal alloys, metal oxides and metal sulphides, magnetic nanoparticles, bimetallic nanocomposites, metal nanorods and quantum dots (QDs), have been extensively studied in recent years [5,6,8,9,12,13,15,16,19–44]. Although, most of these sensors are based on electrochemical methods, which possess some intrinsic drawbacks that has been limited their corresponding applications, especially for routine glucose assay [1,6,7,9,18,23,29,37].
Recently, the molecular fluorescence approaches have been attracted increasing attention to the determination of biological samples. These techniques provide high sensitivity and favorable selectivity for biomolecules sensing and being rapid and simple and these methods are less sensitive to matrix effects [1,2,9,13,27,28,40,41,43,45–55]. Fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) is a powerful and sensitive spectroscopic technique in which the excitation energy of a donor fluorophor is transferred to the acceptor molecule. Energy conservation requires that the energy gap between the ground state and excited states of participating donor and acceptor molecules are nearly the same. This in turn implies that the fluorescence emission spectrum of donor must overlap with the absorption spectrum of acceptor and they should be within a minimal spatial range for donor to transfer its excitation energy to acceptor [27,28,40,47,56–60].
In recent years, the coordination complexes of lanthanide ions, especially Tb3+ ion, as luminescent probes with high quantum yields (sensitized luminescence), have been used to study and determination of various substances has attracted much attention. These complexes have luminescence characteristics that are great utility in analysis, e.g. narrow emission bonds, a large Stokes' shift and long luminescence decay times, and hence avoid potential background fluorescent emission interferences from the biological matrix, so selectivity and detection limits are improved. Also they have strong binding with biological molecules and rare earth elements have more physiological activities and lower toxicities after coordinating with a ligand [61–67].
Noble metals such as silver (Ag) and gold (Au) nanoparticles (NPs) have been widely investigated in various fields of chemistry for several years due to their unique optical and electronic properties. When these NPs dispersed in an aqueous medium, exhibit strong surface plasmon resonance (SPR) absorption from the visible to near-infrared region that is not observed in the spectrum of the bulk metal, so they can be employed as either the acceptor or donor in the FRET system. This SPR absorption is extremely sensitive to their size, shape, composition, distance, and the surrounding media [12,58,68–74]. When the fluorophore molecules interact with the MNPs, their fluorescence will be strongly quenched through FRET from fluorophores to MNPs and this fluorescence quenching by MNPs can be utilized for turn-on fluorescence sensing of analytes. Upon the addition of a target analyte, the fluorophore molecules are removed from the surface of the MNPs and as a result, the fluorescence intensity of the fluorophores increases with an increase in the concentration of the analyte. By considering advantage of the strong quenching ability of MNPs, many efficient fluorescence sensors have been developed for the assay of biological molecules and ions [2,4,13,40,43,44].
Several of fluorescent sensors for glucose have been fabricated including QDs, organic dyes, nanoclusters, upcoverting nanoparticles, nanocomposites and so on [27,28,40]. However these currently used fluorescent sensors have some defects such as photo-bleaching, toxicity, chemical instability, interference of protein fluorescence from biological samples and relatively broad emission band [2,9,13,41–44]. Thus, it is very important to develop a rapid, selective sensitive fluorescence method with high resistance to photobleaching and low toxicity to determine glucose in blood. As useful alternative, in this study, we have tried to use AgNPs as an acceptor and Tb(III)-1,10-phenanthroline (Tb-phen) complex as a donor luminescent probe with above described unique emission property and design a new FRET system with high efficiency, free of the above mentioned limitations. The Tb-phen-AgNPs system was prepared via Tb-phen non-covalent adsorption onto the surface of AgNPs which considering a good overlap between the fluorescence spectrum of Tb-phen complex and the absorption spectrum of AgNPs, induces fluorescence quenching of Tb-phen complex as a result of FRET between them.
Compared with the previously reported glucose detection methods, this non-enzymatic method based on FRET pairs with special characteristics, had the advantages of a lower detection limit, wider linear range in a very short analysis time (within minutes), simple detection procedure, low cost and relatively free from the common interfering species interference. The sensing system was successfully applied to the determination of glucose in human serum with satisfactory results, which did not require any complicated sample pretreatments.


### MATERIALS AND METHODS ###



### MATERIALS AND REAGENTS ###

All reagents and solvents were analytical grades and used without further purification. Doubly distilled water was used throughout this work.
A 1.0 × 10−2 M terbium (III) solution was prepared by dissolving the appropriate amount of terbium (III) chloride hexahydrate (TbCl3·6H2O) (Acros Organics, USA) in doubly distilled water and stored in a polyethylene containers to avoid memory effects of terbium adsorbed on glass vessels.
A 1.0 × 10−2 M stock solution of 1, 10-phenanthroline (phen) (Fluka, Switzerland) was prepared by dissolving the appropriate amount of this compound in 10 mL ethanol and was diluted to the mark in 100 mL volumetric flask with doubly distilled water. A 4.0 × 10−3 M stock solution of glucose was prepared by dissolving an appropriate amount of glucose (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany) in distilled water and stored in a dark place at 4 °C. A 0.1 M solution of sodium borohydride (NaBH4), was prepared by dissolving a desired amount of NaBH4 (Reagents Chemicals, Korea) in doubly distilled water in ice bath. A 2.0 × 10−3 M stock solution of silver nitrate (AgNO3) and a 1% w/v solution of trisodium citrate (C6H5Na3O7·2H2O) were prepared by dissolving the appropriate amount of AgNO3 and C6H5Na3O7·2H2O (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany).
A 5.0 × 10−2 M Tris-(hydroxymethyl) aminomethane-hydrochloric acid (Tris-HCl) buffer solution was prepared by dissolving a desired amount of Tris-base (Merck, Germany) in 90 mL of water, adjusting the pH to 7.0 with HCl and making up the volume to 100 mL with distilled water.
Also aqueous solutions of L-cycteine, L-tyrosine, glycine, ascorbic acid, fructose, NaCl, KCl, CaCl2·2H2O, CuCl2·2H2O, FeCl2·6H2O, AlCl3·6H2O, ZnCl2, MnCl2·4H2O and MgCl2 (all were obtained from Merck, Darmstadt, Germany) were prepared. Acetonitrile (HPLC grade), was purchased from Scharlau (Barcelona, Spain).
Healthy human plasma, used as a real sample, was taken from Iranian Blood Transfusion Organization (IBTO) and patient human plasma was prepared from Aras hospital (Pars Abad, Iran).
Working standard solution was prepared daily by successive dilution of the stock standard with double distilled water.


### INSTRUMENTATION ###

Fluorescence spectra and intensity measurements were performed using a Shimadzu RF-540 spectrofluorometer (Kyoto, Japan) equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp, using 1.0 cm quarts cell. The excitation wavelength was set at 300 nm and the fluorescence was measured using the peak height at 545 nm. All measurements were performed at 25 ± 0.1 °C using a thermostated cell holder and thermostatically controlled water bath (Rikakika, Japan).
UV–Vis absorption spectra were obtained by a UV–visible spectrophotometer (T60, PG Instruments Ltd., Leicestershire, UK) using a 1.0 cm cell. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) measurements were carried out at 298 K on a Nicolet-6700 FTIR spectrometer equipped with the attenuated total reflection (ATR) accessory, a DTGS KBr detector and a KBr beam splitter. The resolution of all measurements was 4 cm−1 and 64 scans. The FT-IR spectra of free phen (1.0 × 10−4 M), Tb-phen complex (the molar ratio of Tb3+ to phen was maintained at 2:1) and the Tb-phen complex in the presence of different concentrations of AgNPs (5.4 × 10−13 M, 4.5 × 10−12 M and 3.6 × 10−11 M) were recorded in the range of 1000–4000 cm−1. Morphology of the AgNPs was examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) MIRA3 FEG-SEM (TescanBrno, Czech Republic).


### SYNTHESIS AND CHARACTERIZATION OF AGNPS ###

AgNPs were prepared by a chemical reduction method described in the literature by controlling AgNO3 and NaBH4 concentration [68–70,75,76]. Briefly, 2 mL of 1% w/v sodium citrate aqueous solution (as stabilizer) was added to an aqueous solution containing 50 mL of 2.0 × 10−3 M (optimum amount) AgNO3 with vigorous stirring. After 10 min, 10 μL of 0.1 M (optimum amount) freshly prepared ice-cold NaBH4 solution (as reducing agent) was added to the solution. The colloid was stirred for another 30 min, the color of the solution changed from pale yellow to deep brown during the time, and aged for 2 days at room temperature before being used. The absorption spectrum of AgNPs (Fig. 1B) showed a plasmonic peak around 412 nm. The size and morphology of the particles were determined using SEM image (data are presented in support information). The particles were spherical and had an average size of 20 ± 3 nm. The concentration of AgNP solution was calculated by UV–visible spectroscopy using the molar extinction coefficient [εmax = 7.6 × 109 (M−1 Cm−1)] [70] and was obtained 1.8 × 10−10 M.


### PREPARATION OF TB-PHEN-AGNPS SYSTEM ###

For investigation of fluorescence quenching of Tb-phen complex in the presence of AgNPs, the analytical procedure which was used to determine the effective and optimum concentration of AgNPs as follow: to 4 mL vials, 80 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M Tb3+ solution, 800 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M Tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.0) solution, 40 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M phen solution and aliquots of working AgNPs solution were added. The mixture was then diluted to the mark with distilled water. The final AgNPs concentration was in the range of 4.5 × 10−14-4.5 × 10−11 M. The solutions were thermostated at 25 ± 0.1 °C and the fluorescence intensity was measured at 545 nm using an excitation wavelength of 300 nm against a blank solution. Both emission and excitation slits were set at 10 nm. The quenched fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen by AgNPs was represented as ΔIf (%) = (I0 − If) / I0 × 100 in which If and I0 were the intensities of the systems with and without AgNPs.


### PROCEDURE FOR THE DETERMINATION OF GLUCOSE ###

For determination of glucose, the analytical procedure which was used to construct the calibration graph was as follow: to 80 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M Tb3+ solution, 800 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M Tris-HCl buffer solution (pH 7.0), 40 μL of 1.0 × 10−2 M phen solution was added to 700 μL of synthesized AgNPs with concentration of 1.8 × 10−10 M and an appropriate amount of a standard solution of glucose was added into the mixture. The final volume of mixture was adjusted to 4 mL with deionized water. The final glucose concentration was in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M. The solutions were thermostated at 25 ± 0.1 °C and the fluorescence intensity was measured at 545 nm using an excitation wavelength of 300 nm against a blank solution (If). A similar solution without adding glucose was also prepared and its fluorescence intensity was recorded at the same wavelengths (I0). The enhanced fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen-AgNPs system by glucose was represented as ΔIf (%) = (If − I0) / I0 × 100.


### SAMPLE PREPARATION AND CONSTRUCTING THE CALIBRATION GRAPH IN SERUM SAMPLE ###

The proposed method was applied to the determination of glucose in a spiked human serum provided by healthy subjects and patient human serum and was used after deproteinization with acetonitrile. An aliquot of 0.5 mL serum, previously spiked with convenient amounts of glucose standard solution for final concentrations in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M, was placed in a centrifuge tube and each sample was deproteinized with 1.5 mL acetonitrile, by vortexing for 2 min and centrifuged for 5 min at 13,000 rpm to remove proteins and its supernatant was diluted to 5.0 mL with deionized water in a volumetric flask. Then 0.05 mL of this solution was subjected to the analysis according to the Section 2.5. The fluorescence intensity was measured against a blank solution (unspiked serum). The concentration of glucose in patient serum was obtained from this calibration graph after deproteinization. For recovery studies, known concentrations of glucose standard solution were added to healthy and patient plasma samples before pretreatment step and total glucose concentrations were then determined.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### INTERACTION STUDIES OF TB-PHEN COMPLEX BY AGNPS USING FLUORESCENCE QUENCHING AND FTIR TECHNIQUES ###

Fluorescence quenching is the decrease of the quantum yield of fluorescence from a fluorophore induced by a variety of molecular interactions with a quencher molecule, such as excited state reactions, energy transfer, molecular rearrangements, ground state complex formation and collisional quenching. One of the most common photophysical processes that cause fluorescence quenching, is FRET. In order for FRET to occur, the spectral overlap between the absorption spectrum of the acceptor and emission spectrum of the donor is necessary [2,13,54,61].
It is well known that with the addition of Tb3+ ions to aqueous solutions of phen (pH 7.0) results in the appearance of the narrower and stronger emission bands at the range of 450–600 nm, characteristic of Tb3+ ion fluorescence which is due to the complex formation of phen with Tb3+ ion and intramolecular energy transfer process. As shown in Fig. 1A (a), after the excitation at 300 nm, the Tb-phen complex emitted the characteristic fluorescence of Tb3+ ion with the emission peaks of 490 and 545 nm, which correspond to 5D4 → 7F6 and 5D4 → 7F5 transitions of Tb3+ ion, respectively [62,63]. As mentioned previously, appreciable overlap between the absorption spectrum of acceptor and emission spectrum of donor is one of the necessary conditions for FRET. Fig. 2 clearly displays the Tb-phen complex emission spectrum (λem = 545 nm) overlaps partially with the extinction spectrum of 20 ± 3 nm AgNPs (λmax = 412 nm). Since AgNPs are capped with negatively charged citrate ions, probably the electrostatic interaction between the positively charged Tb-phen complex and AgNPs leads to form large clusters, due to charge neutralization, resulting in the formation of a Tb-phen-AgNPs assembly. As a result of such noncovalent adsorption of Tb-phen complex onto AgNPs surfaces, the energy transfer can easily occur to AgNPs, which leads to efficient quenching of Tb-phen complex fluorescence [58,71,77]. Therefore the interaction of Tb-phen complex with AgNPs for investigation their possible analytical applicability, was studied in detail. Fig. 3 shows the fluorescence emission spectra of the Tb-phen complex in the presence of different concentrations of AgNPs (4.5 × 10−14–4.5 × 10−11 M) at pH 7.0. It can be seen that Tb-phen complex has a strong fluorescence emission bands at 490 and 545 nm, and there was no fluorescence emission for AgNPs at the measured range. The fluorescence emission intensity of Tb-phen complex decreased remarkably and regularly with the increased concentration of AgNPs, without changing the maximum and the shape of the peaks. With increasing of AgNPs concentration, the quenching efficiency becomes greater and greater so that at 4.5 × 10−11 M concentration, complete quenching occurs and the quenching efficiency is almost 100% (curve “m” in Fig. 3). These results indicated that the Tb-phen complex can interact with AgNPs and the fluorescence quenching effect was due to the FRET phenomenon [2,9]. The fluorescence quenching data are analyzed by the classical Stern–Volmer equation [54,55]: (1) F 0 / F = 1 + K sv Q where F0 and F are the fluorescence intensities in the absence or presence of quencher, respectively, Ksv is the Stern–Volmer quenching constant (M−1) and [Q] is the molar concentration of quencher. Hence Eq. (1) was applied to determine Ksv by linear regression of F0/F versus [Q]. The Stern–Volmer plot of the quenching of Tb-phen complex fluorescence by AgNPs was shown in Fig. 4 . The data showed that within the concentration range of 4.5 × 10−13–4.5 × 10−11 M of AgNPs, there is a good linear relationship and the calculated Ksv value from the linear part of this plot was 1.41 × 1010 L mol−1. Such a large value of Ksv suggests the existence of a strong interaction between Tb-phen complex and AgNPs because of effective spectral overlap between the plasmon absorption band of AgNPs with the high extinction coefficient and the fluorescence spectrum of Tb-phen complex with narrow and intense emission bands. Additionally, it is considered that in the absence of Tb-phen complex, AgNPs were in a dispersed state, but in the presence of Tb-phen complex the AgNPs came into aggregated form which indicated the interaction between Tb-phen complex and AgNPs.
Additional evidence for the interaction of Tb-phen complex with AgNPs was obtained from FTIR spectroscopic results. The FTIR spectra of free phen, Tb-phen complex and Tb-phen-AgNPs system at 298 K was studied (data is provided in support information). The addition of Tb3+ to phen resulted in shifts of CC and CN vibration bands in phen structure. In the presence of AgNPs, in addition to shift of both vibrational peaks of phen, the intensity of the vibrational absorption bands (CC and CN) were also increased. These results could be contributed to the interaction of Tb-phen complex with AgNPs [78–80].


### FACTORS AFFECTING THE FLUORESCENCE INTENSITY QUENCHING OF THE TB-PHEN-AGNPS SYSTEM ###

The effect of several factors such as pH value, concentrations of Tb-phen complex, concentrations of AgNPs and buffer solution, the order of the addition of the various components and time on fluorescence intensity quenching of Tb-phen complex by AgNPs were investigated. The effect of Tb-phen complex concentration at various molar ratios (Tb/phen), varying from 1:1 to 4:1, on the fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen-AgNPs system was studied. It is well known that the optimal concentration of fluorophore molecule is necessary to saturate the surface of AgNPs, where no free fluorophore molecule remain in solution and all of the molecules are adsorbed on AgNPs surface [58,73]. The obtained results showed that the fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen complex is quite quenched at optimum molar ratio of 2:1. This indicates that all of the Tb-phen complex molecules at this molar ratio are adsorbed on AgNPs surface. However, at the high molar ratios (3:1 and 4:1), the fluorescence intensity is not quenched completely. This can be ascribed to the presence of free Tb-phen complex molecules in the solution more than that required for the saturation of AgNPs. Thus, the molar ratio of 2:1, was chosen as the optimum concentration for complex. The effect of pH on the quenching efficiency [ΔIf (%)] of the Tb-phen-AgNPs system was investigated in the pH range from 2 to 11 using HCl or NaOH solutions. The results indicated that the quenched fluorescence intensity of the system reached a maximum when pH was 7.0. At higher pH values (pH > 7.0) the intensity decreased due to the precipitation of terbium hydroxide and AgNPs. Results showed that Tris–HCl (2.0 × 10−3 M, pH 7.0), was the most suitable buffer.
The experimental results showed that the order of the addition of the various components has small effects on the fluorescence intensity of the system. Considering the stability of the system along with the fluorescence quenching intensity enhancement, the following order was chosen for further study: Tb solution, Tris–HCl buffer, phen solution and AgNPs standard solution. Under optimal condition, the effect of experiment time on the fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen-AgNPs system was studied. After addition of all reagents, desired complex solution vortexed for 5 min and in a different time period (every 5 min), fluorescence intensity was measured. In the first few minutes, the ΔIf (%) reached a maximum and remained basically stable for over 3 h without having any significant effects on the results.


### INTERACTION BETWEEN GLUCOSE AND TB-PHEN -AGNPS SYSTEM ###

In order to explore analytical applicability of Tb-phen-AgNPs system, the effect of glucose on the fluorescence of this system was examined. As an example, the fluorescence emission spectra of Tb-phen (a), Tb-phen-AgNPs-glucose (b) and Tb-phen-AgNPs (c) at pH 7.0 under the same experimental conditions are shown in Fig. 5 . From spectrum (a) in Fig. 5, it can be seen that the Tb-phen complex solution exhibits a two strong fluorescence signal at 490 and 545 nm (characteristic peaks of Tb3+ ion), due to intramolecular energy transfer from phen to Tb3+. As mentioned above, when Tb+-phen complex was mixed with AgNPs, it is noncovalently adsorbed onto the surface of the AgNPs and therefore FRET occurs between them, which results in the quenching of Tb-phen complex fluorescence (Fig. 5c). However, upon the addition of 150 × 10−8 M glucose to a mixture solution of Tb-phen-AgNPs, the fluorescence of Tb-phen complex was restored (Fig. 5b), suggesting that complex molecules were released from the surfaces of AgNPs because glucose molecules bound to AgNPs probably via AgO bond instead of Tb-phen-Ag.
It is well known that the metal nanoparticles (MNPs) (especially silver and gold nanoparticles) with diameter range between 12 and 100 nm, can interact with fluorophore molecules without affecting the structure of the fluorophore, causing quenching or enhancement of fluorescence intensity of it. The strength of the quenching/enhancement is influenced by the size and shape of MNPs, the orientation of the fluorophore dipole moments relative to the NPs, the radiative decay rate and quantum yield of the fluorophore [81–83]. In general the energy transfer and electron transfer processes are the two major deactivation mechanisms for fluorescence quenching of excited fluorophore molecules on the MNPs surfaces. In fact, direct binding of a fluorophore to the metal surface in general results the quenching of excited states. The electron transfer mechanism is predominant for particle sizes of ˂5 nm and for MNPs larger than 5 nm, the energy transfer dominates the quenching mechanism. When the fluorophore molecule (as a donor) is placed in the vicinity of a conductive MNPs (AgNPs and AuNPs) surface (as an acceptor), Forster resonance energy transfer (FRET) between donor and acceptor take places and this effectively interaction of the fluorophore molecule by MNPs, leads to fluorescence quenching. The probability FRET depends on the overlap of emission band of the fluorophore molecules with absorption spectra of the MNPs [81–84].
In this work, in the presence of AgNPs, fluorescence quenching in Tb-phen complex was observed. The binding of AgNPs to Tb-phen complex have caused fluorescence quenching. This quenching in the fluorescence intensity is mainly due to energy transfer from the Tb-phen complex to the AgNPs (the size of AgNPs used in the experiment was bigger than 5 nm). When a donor like Tb-phen complex was placed in close vicinity of a conductive AgNPs, FRET takes place between them, resulting in fluorescence quenching. Long range dipole-dipole interaction between the excited donor (Tb-phen complex) and ground state acceptor molecules (AgNPs), probably is the dominant mechanism responsible for the energy transfer [83,85]. Moreover, it is considered that in the presence of glucose, probably the aggregate of AgNPs becomes larger, indicating the significant interaction between glucose and AgNPs. Based on these findings, Tb-phen-AgNPs system was used for sensitive and selective fluorescence assay of glucose. As shown in Fig. 4, the quenching efficiency was gradually increased upon increasing the concentration of AgNPs (4.5 × 10−14–4.5 × 10−11 M) indicating that the more acceptors are presented. On the other hand the experimental results showed that at higher concentrations of the AgNPs, the fluorescence enhancement efficiency starts to decrease. This can be attributed to the fact that in the presence of excess of AgNPs, Tb-phen complex molecules could not be replaced completely by glucose molecules on the surface of AgNPs. Therefore, 3.15 × 10−11 M AgNPs, was chosen for subsequent studies.


### ANALYTICAL FIGURES OF MERIT ###

By using the optimized conditions described above, FRET-based fluorescence response to glucose concentration has been investigated and a spectrofluorometric method was developed for the determination of glucose. As presented in Fig. 6 (A), with increasing the concentration of glucose, the fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen-AgNPs system increased gradually (from bottom to top) and the fluorescence recovery efficiency was proportional to the concentration of glucose (Fig. 6B). The calibration graphs (n = 9) were found to be linear in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M for glucose and its equation was ∆If (%) = 0.05 C + 15.229 (R2 = 0.998), where ΔIf (%) is the enhanced fluorescence intensity of Tb-phen-AgNPs by glucose and C is the concentration of glucose expressed in M. The limit of detection was calculated as 3Sb/m (where Sb is standard deviation of the blank and m is slope of the calibration graph) was found to be 1.94 × 10−8 M. In order to study the precision of the proposed method, series of five solutions of 30, 50 and 700 × 10−8 M of glucose were measured on the same day. By applying the IUPAC definition, the relative standard deviation (RSD) for five analyses was 0.90–2.4%.
The analytical characteristics of the proposed method are compared with some published methods for the determination of glucose in Table 1 . As can be seen, our proposed method has comparable or better detection limit than the previously studied methods. Most of the existing methods require, time-consuming/complicated sample pretreatment and professional operators, which limits the application of these methods for on-site and real-time determination of glucose. However, this proposed non-enzymatic method is very simple, rapid, low cost, high stability, precise, sensitive and selective for screening of glucose and was easy to perform. Furthermore, special emission characteristics of Tb-phen complex as a luminescent probe, such as narrow and strong emission bands, is another most advantage of the proposed non-enzymatic biosensor over those reported previously which caused this method successfully applied to the determination of glucose in serum samples after filtering, without needing a separation technique in analysis (solvent extraction, liquid extraction and solid phase extraction), due to high sensitivity and selectivity obtained with this system.


### STUDY OF INTERFERENCES ###

Selectivity is a very important parameter to evaluate the performance of a new fluorescence sensor, especially for a sensor with potential applications in biomedical samples, so that a highly selective response to the target over other potentially coexisting species is necessary. Therefore, to evaluate the practical applicability of the proposed method, the selectivity experiments for our nanosensor were carried out to various common coexisting substances. In order to study potential interference, the influence of (typical) potentially interfering species was studied by addition of increasing concentration of them to solution of 7.0 × 10−6 M of glucose until variation >10% in analytical intensity was achieved. The tolerance levels of various interferents are summarized in Table 2 . From Table 2, it can be seen that most substances and various cations have no remarkable interference effect on the system and the fluorescence enhancement by glucose was much larger than these substances. Hence selectivity achieved by the proposed method is good and it is possible to determine glucose in serum samples. As mentioned above, this feature is largely attributed to the special emission characteristics of Tb-phen complex, as a luminescent probe.


### APPLICATION ###

To further examine the applicability of the method as a rapid, simple, reliable and sensitive approach, Tb-phen-AgNPs system as the turn-on fluorescence system, was applied to the determination of glucose in serum samples using the standard addition method described in Section 2.6. For this purpose, fluorescence spectrum of Tb-phen-AgNPs system with that of the Tb-phen-AgNPs system in the presence of unspiked serum sample (blank) was studied. Comparing these two spectra showed that blank have no effect on the fluorescence intensity of the Tb-phen-AgNPs and therefore emission peak of Tb-phen complex at 545 nm can be used for determination of glucose. The experimental results revealed that, there is a good linear correlation between glucose concentration and fluorescence intensity in serum sample in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M (R2 = 0.997). Accuracy and precision of the method were checked by the added-found procedure and statistical treatment of data of glucose determination in the matrix. Table 3 shows the analysis results of glucose in the spiked serum sample. The recoveries found for serum sample and for five replicates were in the range of 99.97%–104.80% and the RSD was in the range of 0.06–1.5%. Statistical analysis of the assay results showed satisfactory precision of the proposed method with no significant difference between certified and experimental results. These findings confirmed that the developed method was easy to perform, and afforded good precision, accuracy and recovery when applied to serum sample without needing a separation technique in analysis due to high sensitivity and selectivity obtained with this system.
Also, in order to further investigate the feasibility of this new biosensing system for glucose detection in biological samples, human serum samples of diabetic patients were prepared and glucose levels in the serum samples of these patients was measured using calibration graph described was measured. The obtained results are given in Table 4 . The determined results were compared with those provided by the hospital. Also, accuracy of the method was checked by the above described procedure (Section 2.6) by addition (spiking) of a certain amount of glucose (7.0 × 10−6 M and 50× 10−6 M) to the serum of diabetic patients. The statistical treatment of data and the obtained results were shown in Table 4. As is shown, the RSD was in the range of 0.85–2.3% (for five replicates) and recoveries found for serum samples and for five replicates were in the range of 92.14%–105.43%. These data demonstrated the great potential for practical application of the biosensor for analysis of glucose on real clinical samples.


### CONCLUSION ###

A new ultrasensitive turn-on spectrofluorometric method was developed for the direct determination of glucose in real samples based on Tb-phen-AgNPs fluorescent biosensor. The result showed that the fluorescence intensity of the Tb-phen-AgNPs system was enhanced by glucose due to the replacement of Tb-phen complex from the surfaces of AgNPs and a good linearity for determination of glucose in the range of (5–900) × 10−8 M was found with detection limit of 1.98 × 10−8 M. The proposed method is very simple, precise, rapid, low cost, high stable and more sensitive and selective than the other methods that have been used for the detection of glucose until now. The developed non-enzymatic method was easily applied to the detection of glucose in real samples with excellent reproducibility, probably due to the fact that the most valuable feature of Tb-phen complex (as a luminescent probe) cause selectivity to enhance. Hence, glucose can be determined in real samples without any significant interference from the abundant (matrix).
