### ABSTRACT ###

Ratiometric sensing suffers from less interference and can obtain more accurate results than single-signal assay. Here, a new ratiometric optical sensing strategy for H2O2 detection is developed by etching silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) to deactivate fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) and reduce Rayleigh scattering based on a hyphenated technique of fluorescence and second-order Rayleigh scattering (SRS). The ratiometric detection of H2O2 is achieved through exploiting a hybrid system fabricated by fluorescent carbon dots and silver nanoparticles (CDs/AgNPs). In the CDs/AgNPs system, the fluorescence of CDs is quenched because of FRET, and the scattering is strong due to the intrinsic high light-scattering power of AgNPs. With the introduction of H2O2, the AgNPs are etched and the CDs are released from the AgNP surface, resulting in the fluorescence enhancement and scattering decline. As a result, ratiometric sensing of H2O2 can be achieved based on the CDs/AgNPs system by simultaneous collection of fluorescence and SRS signals. The sensing system is further used for H2O2-generation bioanalysis, and as a proof-of-concept, ratiometric assay of glucose and evaluation of glucose oxidase activity are performed successfully. This work provides a new perspective for sensing applications of plasmonic nanoparticles.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Plasmonic nanoparticles composed of silver and gold have attracted much research attention in many fields including bioimaging, diagnosis and therapy, nanophotonics, and photocatalysis because of their specific physicochemical properties [1]. In particular, the application of plasmonic nanoparticles to design chemical and biological sensors is one of the most research focuses [2,3]. Silver and gold nanoparticles feature strong localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) because of the collective oscillations of the free electrons on the nanoparticle surface under incident electromagnetic radiations [4]. The LSPR of silver and gold nanoparticles generates various so-called plasmon-based optical phenomena, such as resonance absorption, surface-enhanced Raman scattering, metal-enhanced fluorescence, fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET), plasmon resonance Rayleigh scattering [5]. Owing to these LSPR effects, plasmonic nanoparticles can provide great utility in signal transduction processes for sensing systems. A variety of sensors based on plasmonic nanoparticles have been developed successfully based on different spectroscopic techniques in recent decades [6,7].
Minor or no false positive or negative error during chemical or biological sensing is always in pursuit by analytical chemists. Quantitative assay of a target substance by the use of single response-signal is problematic sometimes because interferences may be caused by some analyte-independent factors, such as sample background, microenvironment around the sensor, and fluctuation of excitation light [8,9]. Thus, false results can appear. Ratiometric sensors can avoid these interferences to some extent and make assay results more accurate because of simultaneous measurement of two or more response signals and calculation of their ratio which provide a self-calibration [10,11]. The design of ratiometric sensor is one of research focuses in analytical science. Up to now, a deal of effort has been dedicated to fabricating ratiometric sensors, and numerous ratiometric sensing platforms have been created successfully on the basis of various techniques including fluorescence [12,13], UV–vis absorption [14,15], electrochemical strategies [16,17], electrochemiluminescence [18], surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy [19], and so on [20–22].
Generally, in the design of sensors based on plasmonic nanoparticles, only a single optical signal was used although analytes can cause change of more than one optical signal. Herein, we propose a new ratiometric optical mode for the detection of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) by simultaneous utilization of fluorescence quenching ability and high light-scattering power of Ag nanoparticles (AgNPs). When using a spectrofluorimeter, scattered lights observed at the excited wavelength and at double of the excited light wavelength are Rayleigh scattering and second-order Rayleigh scattering (SRS), respectively [23,24]. Scattering signals are rarely used generally. In the present study, by simultaneously collecting fluorescence and SRS signals, AgNPs and fluorescent carbon dots (CDs) have been utilized as a hybrid system to develop a ratiometric optical sensor for H2O2 detection. Since the interaction between AgNPs and CDs (or carbon nanodots or graphene quantum dots) obtained from different precursors has been well explored and also several CDs/AgNPs composites have been proposed for the detection of H2O2 and H2O2-related analytes based on oxidation etching effect in previous reports [25–28], our study in this work mainly focuses on the development of a ratiometric assay method.
Scheme 1 illustrates the ratiometric sensing principle. In the CDs/AgNPs system, simultaneous response of fluorescence and scattering to H2O2 can be achieved. On the one hand, the CDs with positive charges can bond to the surface of the electronegative AgNPs through electrostatic interaction. Thus, due to the close proximity, the fluorescence of CDs was quenched by the AgNPs through FRET. With the introduction of H2O2, the AgNPs were etched [28–30], which resulted in the release of CDs, accompanied by the fluorescence recovery. On the other hand, the scattering of the hybrid system declined remarkably with the decomposition of AgNPs. Namely, etching AgNPs can deactivate FRET and reduce scattering, which causes two-signal response of the CDs/AgNPs system: fluorescence enhancement and SRS decline, making ratiometric sensing of H2O2 possible. The response of CDs/AgNPs system to H2O2 provides a universal assay platform to determine analytes (e.g., glucose, choline, uric acid, cholesterol, and d-amino acids) relating to H2O2-generation reactions. As a proof-of-concept, here ratiometric glucose sensing and activity assay of glucose oxidase (GOx) have been performed. This newly proposed ratiometric optical sensor is not only simple and inexpensive but also provides a new perspective for sensing applications of plasmonic nanoparticles.


### EXPERIMENTAL AND METHODS ###



### MATERIALS AND APPARATUS ###

Silver nitrate (AgNO3), trisodium citrate, and citric acid were ordered from Aladdin Ltd., Shanghai, China. GOx and horseradish peroxidase were obtained from Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Glucose (Glu), fructose (Fru), lactose (Lac), sucrose (Suc), mannose (Man), cellobiose (Cel), and maltose (Mal) were supplied by Sigma-Aldrich (USA). H2O2 (30 wt %), phenol, 4-aminoantipyrine, and ethylenediamine were ordered from Chengdu Kong Chemical Reagents Factory (Chengdu, China). Other chemicals were of analytical reagent grade, and all the reagents were directly used without further purification. Human serum samples were got from Daping Hospital (Chongqing, China). Ultrapure water (18.2 MΩ cm) was used for solution preparation in all experiments. Na2HPO4 KH2PO4 buffers (1/15 M, Sorensen's phosphate buffer) with different pH values were prepared according to a standard protocol.
The fluorescence spectra and SRS spectra were recorded by an F-2700 spectrofluorometer (Hitachi, Japan). The photomultiplier tube (PMT) voltage was fixed at 400 V, and the slits were set at 10 nm for both excitation and emission. UV–vis absorption spectra were collected using a UV–vis 2450 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis were taken on a JEM 2100F transmission electron microscope (JEOL, Japan). The Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrum was tested with a Bruker IFS 113v spectrometer (Bruker, Germany). Zeta potentials were measured by a NanoBrook 90plus Zeta (Brookhaven Instruments, USA). A METTLER TOLEDO FE28 pH meter (Mettler-Toledo, Switzerland) was used to determine pH values of solutions.


### PREPARATION OF AG NANOPARTICLES ###

AgNPs were prepared through the well-known citrate reduction method [31]. All stir bars and glassware were treated with aqua regia and then thoroughly cleaned with ultrapure water. Typically, 50 mL of AgNO3 solution (1 mM) was heated under gentle stir until it began to boil. Then, 2 mL of trisodium citrate (1%, w/v) was added dropwise into the AgNO3 solution. With continuous stirring, the mixed solution was boiled for an additional 15 min. Subsequently, the solution was naturally cooled to ambient temperature and the resulting AgNP solution with brown yellow was obtained.


### PREPARATION OF FLUORESCENT CARBON DOTS ###

CDs were synthesized in accordance with a previously reported method [32]. The procedure is briefly described as follows. A portion of citric acid (1.0 g) and ethylenediamine (335 μL) were dissolved in 10 mL of ultrapure water. Then the solution was put into a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (25 mL) and heated at 200 °C for 5 h. After the reaction, the reactor was cooled to room temperature naturally. The product underwent dialysis against ultrapure water by a dialysis bag for 24 h and lyophilized and redispersed in ultrapure water. The CD solution with bright blue fluorescence was prepared.


### PROCEDURES FOR RATIOMETRIC DETECTION OF H2O2 ###

For H2O2 ratiometric sensing by fluorescence and SRS, the preparation of CDs/AgNPs system and the sensing procedures are described as follows. The as-synthesized AgNPs (2 mL) and 200 μL of CDs (86 μg mL−1) were mixed and stirred for 1 min, and the CDs/AgNPs system as the standardized detection platform was prepared. To evaluate the sensitivity, 100 μL of CDs/AgNPs and 100 μL of pH buffer (pH 6.5) were added to 700 μL of water, and the solution was mixed by vibration. After that, H2O2 solution (100 μL) with different concentrations was introduced into the mixtures, followed by shaking well. The mixed solutions were incubated at 60 °C for 10 min before spectral measurements. At last, the fluorescence and SRS spectra were collected with an excitation of 350 nm.


### PROCEDURES FOR RATIOMETRIC DETECTION OF GLUCOSE ###

For ratiometric detection of glucose, 100 μL of CDs/AgNPs and 100 μL of pH buffer (pH 6.5) were added to 690 μL of ultrapure water, and then glucose solution (100 μL) with various concentrations and 10 μL of GOx (10 U mL−1) were added to the mixed solution. After stirred well, the mixtures were incubated at 37 °C for 30 min firstly and then maintained at 60 °C for 10 min before spectral measurements. Finally, the fluorescence and SRS spectra were collected using excitation at 350 nm. For the determination of glucose in human serum samples, firstly the serum samples with 10-fold dilution were centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 10 min, and then 10 mM AgNO3 was added to the supernatant for removing the high level of chloride ions [33]. The samples finally were centrifuged using an ultrafilter (3000 molecular weight cutoff) at 16,000 rpm for 20 min. Other procedures were similar to the glucose detection mentioned above except that the glucose solution was replaced by a pretreated serum sample with 20-fold dilution.


### PROCEDURE FOR RATIOMETRIC ASSAY OF GLUCOSE OXIDASE ACTIVITY ###

For ratiometric assay of GOx activity, 100 μL of CDs/AgNPs and 100 μL of pH buffer (pH 6.5) were added to 600 μL of ultrapure water, and then 100 μL of glucose (10 mM) and GOx solution (100 μL) with various concentrations were added to the mixed solution. After stirred well, the mixtures were incubated at 37 °C for 30 min firstly and then kept at 60 °C for 10 min before spectral measurements. Finally, the fluorescence and SRS spectra were collected using excitation at 350 nm.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### CHARACTERIZATION OF AG NANOPARTICLES AND CARBON DOTS ###

The AgNPs were prepared by the typical citrate-reduction method [31]. Fig. 1 A is a TEM image of AgNPs, which shows that the nanoparticles are spherical shape with an average size of about 10.8 nm (Fig. 1B). The brown yellow AgNP solution has a typical plasmon resonance absorption peak at 436 nm (Fig. 1C). The synthesis of the amine-functionalized CDs was in accordance with Yang's method [32]. TEM measurement reveals that the CDs are uniformly dispersed (Fig. 1D) and have an average diameter about 2.7 nm (Fig. 1E). As exhibited in Fig. 1F, the UV–vis absorption spectrum shows that the CDs have a characteristic absorption peak at 339 nm. The CD solution is pale yellow and shows bright blue emission radiated by a 365 nm UV light (insets of Fig. 1F). Fig. 1F also displays the excitation and emission spectra of the CDs, and the excitation and emission peaks are located at 337 and 438 nm, respectively. With different excitations, a small shift of the emission maximum was observed (Fig. S1). In the FT-IR spectrum of CDs (Fig. S2), stretching vibration absorption of COH at 3406 cm−1, the vibration absorption band of CO at 1655 cm−1, characteristic absorption bands at 3075, 2952, and 1438 cm−1 consistent with the stretching and bending vibrations of CH2 bonds can be seen. The absorption peaks at 3262 and 1556 cm−1 belonging to NH bond demonstrate the existence of amine groups on the CD surface. The results demonstrate the successful preparation of AgNPs and CDs.


### DESIGN STRATEGY OF THE RATIOMETRIC SENSING PLATFORM ###

Fluorescence quenching can take place via FRET when fluorophores are appended onto Au or Ag nanoparticles [34–36]. Also, published literature have reported that the fluorescence of orange juice-derived CDs as well as citric acid and urea-derived CDs could be quenched by AgNPs through FRET effect [25,26]. Therefore, we can rationally speculate that in this work, the as-prepared CDs can bond to the AgNPs through electrostatic interaction because of the existence of electronegative carboxyl groups on the AgNP surface and positive amine groups of the CDs [37], followed by occurrence of FRET. Zeta potential measurements were performed, and the zeta potentials of sole AgNPs and CDs were −23.85 and 1.79 mV, respectively, whereas the zeta potential of the CDs/AgNPs hybrid was turned to be −19.32 mV. The experimental results suggest the interaction of AgNPs and CDs. In addition, as revealed in Fig. S3, the absorption peak of AgNPs is red-shifted from 436 to 441 nm after the addition of CDs, also demonstrating that there is an interaction between the AgNPs and CDs. Fig. 2 A displays that the emission spectrum of CDs is heavily overlapped with the absorption spectrum of AgNPs. The results further indicate the possibility of the FRET [35,38]. As anticipated and exhibited in Fig. 2B, the fluorescence of CDs is gradually decreased by increasing AgNP concentration, and the quenching efficiency exceeds 90% when the amount of AgNPs reaches to 400 μL mL−1 (Fig. S4).
According to Rayleigh scattering theory [39,40], the light-scattering intensity I of a spherical scatterer with radius of a is given by (1) I = 8 π 4 a 6 n 4 I 0 r 2 λ 0 4 | m 2 − 1 m 2 + 2 | 2 ( 1 + cos 2 θ ) where n is the refractive index of the medium; I 0 is the intensity of the incident light; r denotes the distance between the scatterer and the detector; λ 0 is the wavelength of the incident light; m denotes the relative refractive index of the scatterer, and θ is the angle between the incident light and the detector. AgNPs have high light-scattering ability on visible light region because of the intrinsic photophysical properties such as a large refractive index, and thereby AgNPs have been widely used to dark-field scattering imaging [6,41,42].
Additionally, in accordance with the Fraunhofer single-slit diffraction principle [43], the light intensity I of a diffracted wave is described by the following equation: (2) I = I 0 sin π a λ s i n θ π a λ sin θ 2 where I 0 is the light intensity at the pattern center; θ denotes the deflection angle; a represents the slit width and λ is the wavelength. It thereby can be known that the intensity of central maximum is stronger greatly (more than 95%) than those of secondary maxima. SRS is a diffraction light of Rayleigh scattering due to diffraction effect at the grating of the spectrofluorometer, and thus, the light intensity of SRS is far weaker than that of Rayleigh scattering.
We firstly investigated the scattering of the sole AgNPs and CDs. Using 350 nm light as excitation (the excited wavelength was selected based on an overall consideration of excitation of CDs and wavelength-dependent scattering intensity), the Rayleigh scattering intensity of AgNPs (100 μL mL−1, a concentration can cause apparent fluorescence quenching of the CDs) is very strong and exceeded measuring range of the instrument (data not shown). To obtain fluorescence and scattering signals simultaneously under a same excitation, SRS signal was proposed to be exploited. As shown in Fig. 3 A, the AgNPs have an appropriate intensity for the SRS signal. In contrast, the CDs just have a very weak SRS signal. Next, the fluorescence and SRS of the CDs/AgNPs system were studied in the absence and presence of H2O2. Published researches have been well demonstrated that H2O2 can etch AgNPs by the oxidation of Ag0 to Ag+ [29,30]. Fig. S5 presents the absorption spectra of CDs/AgNPs system in the absence and presence of H2O2, and it can be seen that the surface plasmon absorption of AgNPs nearly disappears after the addition of H2O2, which confirms the decomposition of AgNPs. As a consequence, AgNPs would be deprived of strong scattering. Meanwhile, the CDs would be released from the surface of the AgNPs and fluorescence would regain. Fig. 3B shows the fluorescence and SRS spectra of the CDs/AgNPs system in the absence and presence H2O2. It can be found that as anticipated, the CDs/AgNPs system has a weak fluorescence and strong SRS, whereas with the introduction of H2O2, the fluorescence increases and the SRS significantly decreases. The result indicates that the CDs/AgNPs system has the potential to be developed as a ratiometric optical sensor of H2O2 by using fluorescence coupled with SRS.


### RATIOMETRIC DETECTION OF H2O2 ###

We next assessed the feasibility of the CDs/AgNPs system as a ratiometric sensor for H2O2 detection. Some tests were carried out to optimize the assay conditions including reaction pH, time, and temperature. The oxidation and etching of AgNPs by H2O2 is pH-dependent because pH has great effect on the reactivity of H2O2 and AgNPs [44]. Thereby, the effect of pH on H2O2 sensing was explored firstly. As shown in Fig. S6A, with the increase of pH, initially, the fluorescence enhancement (F/F 0, here F 0 and F denote the fluorescence intensity of the CDs/AgNPs hybrid in the absence and presence of H2O2, respectively) is increased and has a maximum at pH 6.5. However, the F/F 0 is decreased slowly at higher pH values. For the scattering, Fig. S6B reveals that the SRS decline (S 0/S, here S 0 and S denote the SRS intensity of the CDs/AgNPs hybrid in the absence and presence of H2O2, respectively) is increased apparently from pH 6.0 to 6.5, and then the increase of S 0/S is not very significant when pH is over pH 6.5. Considering both the fluorescence and SRS, the pH 6.5 thereby was chosen as the optimum pH and to apply to the following experiments. Next, the response rate of CDs/AgNPs system to H2O2 was monitored. Fig. S6C and S6D reveals that the oxidation reaction is not complete within 120 min at room temperature. To improve the sensing efficiency, the incubation temperature was optimized. As exhibited in Fig. S6E and S6F, with the rise of incubation temperature, the fluorescence enhancement and SRS decline are significant with a reaction time of 10 min. We finally chose the incubation temperature of 60 °C as the optimum temperature in consideration of both sensing efficiency and the stability of H2O2. As a result, optimum conditions for H2O2 detection using the CDs/AgNPs system are at pH 6.5, temperature of 60 °C, and incubation time of 10 min.
The linearity and sensitivity for H2O2 sensing were investigated through recording fluorescence and SRS spectra of the CDs/AgNPs system upon the addition of various H2O2 concentrations under the optimum experimental conditions. Fig. 4 A exhibits that increasing H2O2 concentration gradually enhances the fluorescence, whereas the SRS intensity is decreased. Fig. 4B displays the relationship between the intensity ratio F/S (where F and S are the fluorescence and SRS intensity, respectively) and the concentration of H2O2. The F/S displays good linear relationships against H2O2 concentration in the range of 0–20 μM (R 2 = 0.993) and 40–200 μM (R 2 = 0.990) (Fig. 4C and D). The detection limit for H2O2 was found to be 0.86 μM on the basis of 3S b/slope (S b represents the standard deviation of the background signals). We summarized the detection performance including linear range and detection limit of H2O2 obtained by previously reported methods and our method as listed in Table S1. The data show that our ratiometric method is not inferior to most of those methods. The results demonstrate the feasibility for developing ratiometric optical sensor of H2O2 by utilizing fluorescence in combination with SRS based on the CDs/AgNPs system.


### RATIOMETRIC DETECTION OF GLUCOSE ###

The sensitive response of CDs/AgNPs system to H2O2 provides a favorable system to detect analytes (e.g., glucose, choline, uric acid, cholesterol, and d-amino acids) participating H2O2-generation reactions for biological applications [33,45–48]. As the proof-of-concept experiment, glucose here was chosen as the analyte. As known, GOx can selectively catalyze glucose oxidation to produce H2O2 and gluconic acid in the presence of oxygen, as described by the following reaction (3) [45]: (3) Glucose + O 2 + H 2 O → GO x Gluconic Acid + H 2 O 2
Accordingly, with the help of GOx, sensitive ratiometric assay of glucose is possible using the CDs/AgNPs system. Under an optimum concentration of GOx (100 U L−1), the sensitivity and linearity of GOx-assisted glucose sensing was evaluated. Fig. 5 A shows the fluorescence enhancement and SRS decline of the CDs/AgNPs system with increasing glucose concentration. Fig. 5B shows the relationship between the ratio of fluorescence and SRS intensity and the concentration of glucose. The intensity ratio F/S (where F and S denote the fluorescence and SRS intensity, respectively) displays good linear relationships versus glucose concentration ranging from 0 to 60 μM (R 2 = 0.992) and 80–1000 μM (R 2 = 0.990) (Fig. 5C and D). The detection limit for glucose was calculated to be 0.96 μM on the basis of 3S b/slope. A comparison to previously reported assay methods for glucose determination is summarized in Table S2, from which it can be found that our ratiometric sensor is not inferior to those methods. Besides, it is known that the ratiometric detection, which can provide a self-calibration, has better anti-interference ability and can make assay results more accurate [10,11].
For the effective glucose determination, good selectivity is desired. To evaluate the specificity of the developed method for glucose assay, we tested different sugars (lactose, sucrose, maltose, fructose, mannose, and cellobiose) and some common ions (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) using the CDs/AgNPs sensing platform. As shown in Fig. 6 , the ratios of fluorescence and SRS (F/S) of other substances have negligible change compared to that of glucose. These results prove that the GOx-assisted ratiometric method has favorable selectivity for glucose detection.


### ASSAY PERFORMANCES FOR GLUCOSE IN HUMAN SERUM ###

As a biological active, glucose is the most important source of energy for cellular respiration. For human health, the glucose level in blood is highly related to hypoglycemia or diabetes. Hence, glucose detection is very important for biology, clinical medicine, food, and pharmaceutical industry. To demonstrate the practical applications of our proposed ratiometric sensor based on the CDs/AgNPs system, in this work, the glucose in human serum samples was tested. The serum samples were assayed using our method and a clinical method in hospital (GOx/peroxidase/phenol/4-aminoantipyrine-based colorimetric method). Table S3 represents the experimental results from which we can find that the results provided by the developed ratiometric sensor are close to those detected by clinical method. Also, the recoveries of 95–112% and the relative standard deviations (RSDs) ranging from 3.18% to 6.52% for the proposed method were achieved. These results indicate that our ratiometric assay system has potential for glucose detection in human serum.


### RATIOMETRIC ASSAY OF GLUCOSE OXIDASE ACTIVITY ###

As a representative of oxidases, GOx has found widespread use in many fields, such as cancer diagnosis and treatment, industrial bioreactors, bio-fuel cell, and so on [49–51]. The assay of GOx activity is very significant for biomedicine and bioanalysis [52–54]. The CDs/AgNPs system can also be exploited as a ratiometric optical sensor for screening and evaluating GOx activity. As shown in Fig. 7 A, the fluorescence of the CDs/AgNPs system increases and SRS declines progressively with increasing GOx concentration. The intensity ratio F/S (where F and S denote the fluorescence and SRS intensity, respectively) displays good linear relationships versus GOx concentration ranging from 1 to 10 U L−1 (R 2 = 0.991) and 20–100 U L−1 (R 2 = 0.990) (Fig. 7B and C). The detection limit for GOx activity was calculated to be 0.98 U L−1 according to 3S b/slope. The experimental results indicate that the CDs/AgNPs system is qualified to ratiometrically detect GOx activity.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

In summary, a new ratiometric optical sensor for H2O2 detection was developed by etching AgNPs to deactivate FRET and reduce Rayleigh scattering based on a hyphenated technique of fluorescence and SRS. In the CDs/AgNPs system, the fluorescence of CDs was quenched by AgNPs through FRET and the scattering was strong because of the intrinsic high light-scattering ability of AgNPs. H2O2 can etch AgNPs, which gives rise to the decomposition of AgNPs. Therefore, with the introduction of H2O2 to the hybrid system, the AgNPs were etched and the CDs were released from the AgNP surface, leading to the fluorescence enhancement and scattering decline. Consequently, ratiometric detection of H2O2 was achieved by simultaneously recording fluorescence and SRS signals. The CDs/AgNPs system was further extended to act as a platform for H2O2-related ratiometric bioanalysis. GOx can catalyze the oxidation of glucose to produce H2O2, and thereby ratiometric assay of glucose and evaluation of glucose oxidase activity were obtained. Furthermore, the assay of glucose in human serum samples with satisfactory results implies the potential of the novel ratiometric sensing platform for the practical applications.
