### ABSTRACT ###



### GRAPHICAL ABSTRACT ###

Electrochemiluminescence resonance energy transfer (ECRET) strategy using luminol-doped silica nanoparticles (luminol-SiNPs) for both “turn-off” detection of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) and “turn-on” detection of anesthetic drug lidocaine hydrochloride (LDC) is developed for the first time.


### ABSTRACT ###

It is the first time to develop electrochemiluminescence resonance energy transfer (ECRET) strategy using luminol-doped silica nanoparticles (luminol-SiNPs) for both “turn-off” detection of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) and “turn-on” detection of anesthetic drug lidocaine hydrochloride (LDC). The spectral overlap of ECL emission of luminol-SiNPs with the absorption of TNP triggers ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP and results in the decreased ECL signals. Besides, the specific interaction between TNP and LDC consumes TNP, accordingly prevents ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP and achieves increasing ECL signals. The proposed ECRET strategy using luminol-SiNPs exhibits fast response (within 10 min), high sensitivity with LOD of 22.4 nM for TNP and 92.5 nM for LDC, respectively. It is also successfully applied in ECRET detection of TNP in lake water, LDC in human serum and commercial drug sample detections with satisfactory recoveries. More importantly, ECL of luminol-SiNPs is measured via linear sweep voltammetry by electrochemical reduction of dissolved oxygen at negative potential (– 1 V) and in no need of adding hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). Reactive oxygen species be generated in situ and make luminol-SiNPs system emit ECL, which makes ECL of the whole luminol-SiNPs system more stable than luminol-H2O2 system.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Electrochemiluminescence (ECL) has become a powerful analytical technique in analysis of clinical diagnosis, food safety, drug analysis or even biowarfare samples owing to its advantages of better reproducibility, lower background signal, easier temporal and spatial control of ECL reactions [1–5]. Electrochemiluminescence resonance energy transfer (ECRET) combined with ECL and energy transfer has attracted increasing attentions because of its no excitation light source and no interference from the scattered light. In the past decades, more and more researchers have focused on synthesizing stable nanoparticles with high ECL efficiency and low cost in order to set up donor-acceptor pairs in trace analysis. Compared with organic dyes and metal complex emitters, ECL nanoparticle donors become great candidates for their superior performance such as high ECL efficiency and easy modification. Semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) has been studied by many researchers [6–10]. However, QDs usually contain toxic heavy mental element, which greatly limits their application in bioassay. It is of great significance to develop environmentally friendly and nontoxic/low toxic ECL emitters. Gao and his co-researchers utilized upconverting nanoparticles as donors, and gold nanorods as acceptors in ECRET detection of prostate-specific antigen [11]. Zhou and his co-researchers utilized silver nanoclusters as donors and ruthenium complex as acceptors in ultrasensitive immunosensor fapolipoprotein-A1 [12]. Wang and his co-researchers used graphitic carbon nitride QDs as ECL donors and riboflavin as receptors in ECRET detection of riboflavin [13]. Sun and his researchers firstly synthesized polymer dots, investigated its ECL property and applied them in aptasensing [14]. In our previous work, ruthenium complex-doped silica nanoparticles were used as ECL donors in ECRET analysis of 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT) and ozone [15,16]. To achieve high energy transfer efficiency, Tian and his co-researchers put donor of luminol and acceptor of NGQDs together inside the composite [17]. Chen and his researchers co-encapsulated the donor of ruthenium complex and acceptor of CdTe QDs into a silica nanosphere [18]. Though so many ECL nanoparticles as mentioned above have been synthesized up to now, it still needs to give further studies on investigating ECL nanoparticles. Because compared with fluorescent nanoparticles, it is still limited to get proper ECL nanoparticles with high ECL efficiency, easy modification and nontoxicity / low toxicity.
Nitroaromatic compounds such as 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP), TNT or dinitrotoluene (DNT) are identified as the toxic polluters of subterranean water, land, and other security applications owing to their high toxicity as well as their high explosive nature [19]. Among of these nitroaromatic compounds, TNP possesses an explosive ability stronger than that of TNT. However, TNP is widely used in dye industry, rocket fuel, fireworks, pharmaceuticals or chemical laboratories, which pollutes drinking or surface water and even brings great threat to homeland security and public safety. Owing to the harmful of TNP to human beings such as affecting central nervous system, cardiovascular system, metabolism, liver and kidneys/urinary system, various methods for TNP detection are developed including mass spectrometry [20], surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy [21], electrochemistry [22], luminescent [23,24] / photoluminescent [25,26] and fluorescent methods [27–31]. To the best of knowledge, rare ECL methods for TNP are reported although ECL has so many advantages mentioned above in analytical detections. Lidocaine hydrochloride (LDC) as a kind of anesthetic drug can inhibit the ion flows required for transmission of impulses from pain sensation fibers. Besides, LDC is also frequently used as a therapeutic agent for treating cardiac disorders owing to its antiarrhythmic effect. Nowadays the reported methods for LDC detection are mainly focused on chromatography and mass spectrometry [32,33]. Though electrochemical method can also be used in LDC detection, sensitivity is limited for electrochemistry [34–36]. ECL or ECRET can overcome the shortcoming and achieve fast, sensitive and selectivity detection.
Different dyes-doped silica nanoparticles have been reported such as ruthenium complex Ru(bpy)3 2+, fluorescein, tetramethylrhodamine-5-isothiocyanate (TRITC). Silica matrix effectively protects dyes from the surrounding environment, increases photostability, provides signal enhancement for per nanoparticle and does not change luminescence wavelengths [37–41]. Inspired by this, luminol-doped silica nanoparticles (luminol-SiNPs) are designed in the present work and synthesized via water/oil (W/O) microemulsion method in the present work. Spherical luminol-SiNPs with luminol inside the silica matrix can give ultrasensitive electrochemical properties and much higher ECL efficiency than luminol [42]. Luminol-SiNPs are also easy modification and nontoxicity / low toxicity, which is important for ECL nanoparticle emitters in analysis. Since ECL emission of luminol-SiNPs overlaps with the absorption of TNP, ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP is triggered and “turn-off” detection of TNP is achieved (Scheme 1 ). Owing to the specific interaction between TNP and LDC, TNP is consumed, and accordingly ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP prevented, which brings “turn-on” detection of LDC. Therefore, a novel ECL donor-acceptor pair of luminol-SiNPs and TNP is utilized and a sensitive ECRET method for both “turn-off” detection of TNP and turn-on” detection of LDC is developed using luminol-doped silica nanoparticles for the first time. More importantly, ECL of luminol-SiNPs is measured via linear sweep voltammetry by electrochemical reduction of dissolved oxygen at negative potential (– 1 V) and in no need of adding hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). Reactive oxygen species be generated in situ and make luminol-SiNPs system emit ECL, which makes ECL of the whole luminol-SiNPs system more stable than luminol-H2O2 system.

### MATERIALS AND REAGENTS ###

2,4,6-Trinitrophenol (TNP), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT); 2,4-dinitrotoluene (DNT), 3,5-Dinitrobenzoic acid (DNBA) and were purchased from Shanghai Macklin Biochemical Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Luminol, NaClO and lidocaine hydrochloride (LDC) were obtained from Aladdin Reagent (Shanghai, China). Tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS), 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane (APTES), and Triton X-100 were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Beijing, China). 1-Hexanol was obtained from Beijing Yili Chemical Reagent Factory (Beijing, China). Cyclohexane was purchased from Beijing Chemical Reagent Factory (Beijing, China). Ammonium hydroxide (25%), Na2HPO4, NaH2PO4, NaHCO3, Na2SO4, NaNO3, AgNO3, CdCl2, CuCl2, MnCl2, BaCl2 and NiCl2 were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. (Shanghai, China). All these chemicals were analytical-reagent grade and used without any further purification. 20 mM luminol solution was firstly with 0.2 M NaOH and then diluted by water. 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH 10.9) was used during the experiments. Doubly distilled water was used throughout all experiments.


### INSTRUMENTATION ###

All electrochemiluminescent measurements were conducted with an MPI-A capillary electrophoretic electrochemiluminescence detector produced by Xi’an Remex Electronics (Xi’an China) and Changchun Institute of Applied Chemistry (Changchun, China). A conventional three-electrode cell were used with a glassy carbon electrode (GCE) (ϕ = 3 mm) as working electrode, a platinum wire counter electrode and an Ag/AgCl reference electrode (saturated KCl). ECL intensities were monitored through the bottom of the three electrode cell which was purchased from Xi’an Remex Electronics (Xi’an China). The photomultiplier tube voltage was kept at 1100 V during all the ECL measurements. GCE working electrode was polished with 0.05 μm alumina and then cleaned by ultrapure water in an ultrasonic bath prior to each use. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were taken by a JEM-2100 F transmission electron microscope (JEOL, Japan) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were taken by a SU8020 scanning electron microscope (Hitachi, Japan).


### PREPARATION OF LUMINOL-DOPED SILICA NANOPARTICLES (LUMINOL-SINPS) ###

Since dyes-doped silica nanoparticles have been investigated by many workers, luminol-SiNPs were prepared with water/oil microemulsion method according to the reports and our previous reports with a little modification [16,40,41,43]. Typically, 20 mM luminol (2 mL) aqueous solution was firstly added into the mixture including cyclohexane (15 mL), 1-hexanol (3.2 mL), Triton X-100 (3.44 mL), water (1.26 mL) and kept magnetically stirred for 15 min. Next, TEOS (200 μL) was injected into the solution. After 30 min, ammonium hydroxide (120 μL) was added and a polymerization reaction was initiated. The solution was kept stirred for 24 h. Then, APTES (100 μL) was added and kept stirred for another 24 h. Finally, the resultant nanoparticles were collected by centrifugation and washed three times with ethanol and water, and then resuspended with 5 mL water. The final concentration of luminol-NPs is 8 mM calculated by 20 mM luminol. Surfactant Triton X-100 was added to prevent the aggregation of luminol-SiNPs in the synthesis process, but luminol-SiNPs can be aggregated after washed and resuspended. Therefore the resuspended luminol-SiNPs solutions need an ultrasonic dispersion process 5 min before each use. In the following ECL measurements, 40 μL of the resuspended luminol-SiNPs was used in one sample solution.


### TNP DETECTION PROCEDURE ###

In a 1.5 mL plastic vial, 200 μL of 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH 10.9), different concentrations of TNP and appropriate water and 40 μL of the resuspended luminol-SiNPs were added to keep the final volume of the whole solution at 1 mL. The whole solution needed to be thoroughly vortex mixed and put in 37 °C water bath for 10 min. Finally ECL spectra were recorded.


### ECRET DETECTION OF TNP IN REAL WATER SAMPLE ###

Real lake water sample was first treated by ultra-filtration with 10 kD ultra-filtration membrane. Then 200 μL treated lake water was added into 200 μL of 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH 10.9). Other procedures were done similar like the above TNP detection procedure. Since no obvious TNP was detected in real lake water, three different concentrations of PA (0.1 μM, 0.5 μM, 1 μM) were used to obtain the recovery via recovery methods of standard additions.


### LDC DETECTION PROCEDURE ###

In a 1.5 mL plastic vial, 100 μL 0.01 mM TNP, different concentrations of LDC, appropriate water, 200 μL of 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH 10.9) and 40 μL of the resuspended luminol-SiNPs were added to keep the final volume of the whole solution at 1 mL. The reaction between TNP and LDC was kept at 2 min. The whole solution needed to be thoroughly vortex mixed before each ECL spectra measurement.


### ECRET DETECTION OF LDC IN HUMAN SERUM SAMPLES ###

100 μL 10-fold human serum and three different concentrations of PA (1 μM, 5 μM, 10 μM) were used to obtain the recovery via recovery methods of standard additions. Other procedures were done similar like the above LDC detection procedure.


### ECRET DETECTION OF LDC IN LDC MUCILAGE STANDARD SOLUTION SAMPLES ###

Since the standard content of LDC in LDC mucilage standard solution from three different three different factories was 0.2 g/10 mL (73.86 mM), LDC mucilage standard solution was firstly 100-fold diluted and then 100 μL 100-fold diluted LDC mucilage standard solution, 100 μL 0.01 mM TNP, appropriate water, 200 μL of 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH 10.9) and 40 μL of the resuspended luminol-SiNPs were added to keep the final volume of the whole solution at 1 mL. Other procedures were done similar like the above LDC detection procedure.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### SYNTHESIS AND CHARACTERIZATIONS OF LUMINOL-SINPS ###

As shown in TEM (Fig. 1 A–B) and SEM (Fig. 1C) images, the as-prepared luminol-SiNPs display a spherical structure with porous silica matrix, which is in accordance with the reports [40,41,43]. Both the silica matrix and electrostatic interaction retard dyes penetration. After calculated luminol-SiNPs size in TEM images, it can be seen that the average diameter is nearly 33 nm (Fig. 1D). Elements mapping results in supplementary information (Figure S1) confirm the presence of four main elements (C, O, Si and N). Though the dispersion of resuspended luminol-SiNPs is not very ideal, ECL is stable which is of great importance for ECL application. Luminol-SiNPs can be stable for more than 1 month when stored in 4 °C refrigerator by detecting ECL intensity. More importantly, luminol-SiNPs ECL system is no need of adding hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). Dissolved oxygen is electrochemical reduced at negative potential (− 1 V) and then enhances ECL of luminol-NPs at 0.6 V, which makes luminol-SiNPs ECL system more stable than traditional luminol-H2O2 ECL system by monitoring 60 min at room temperature (Fig. 2 ). It can be seen that luminol-NPs has stable ECL property.


### CONSTRUCTION OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR BOTH “TURN-OFF” DETECTION OF TNP AND “TURN-ON” DETECTION OF LDC USING LUMINOL-SINPS ###

Luminol-NPs have maximal ECL emission at 425 nm similarly with luminol (Figure S2) [44,45]. TNP has absorption from 300 nm to 450 nm (Figure S2) [28,46]. ECL emission of luminol-SiNPs effectively overlaps with the absorption of TNP. It is possible to trigger ECRET between luminol-SiNPs as ECL donors and TNP as acceptor. To verify the principle of ECRET between luminol-SiNPs and TNP, ECL of luminol-SiNPs system is detected in Fig. 3 . 56.2% ECL of luminol-SiNPs is quenched after the addition of TNP, which confirms the feasibility of ECRET platform for “turn-off” detection of TNP.
TNP is an effective and typical reagent to identify anesthetic drug LDC in pharmaceutical industry. LDC can form yellow precipitate with TNP in alkaline solutions and the yellow precipitate has melting point from 228 °C to 232 °C. Therefore the specific interaction between TNP and LDC is utilized in ECL “turn-on” detection of LDC. Because LDC can react with TNP and consume TNP, ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP is accordingly prevented. As shown in Fig. 3, after the addition of TNP and LDC to luminol-SiNPs, ECL intensity is increased nearly 1.45 times compared with TNP and luminol-SiNPs mixtures, and nearly the same as LDC and luminol-SiNPs mixtures. It indicates the feasibility of ECRET platform for “turn-on” detection of LDC.


### OPTIMIZATION OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR “TURN-OFF” DETECTION OF TNP USING LUMINOL-SINPS ###

To achieve “turn-off” detection of TNP, the effect of temperature on ECL “turn-off” detection of TNP via the proposed ECRET strategy is firstly optimized in supplementary information (Figure S3). ECL intensities are measured from room temperature (25 °C) to 70 °C (Fig. 3). I 0 represents ECL intensity of luminol-SiNPs; I represents ECL intensity of luminol-SiNPs and TNP mixtures; (I 0-I)/I 0 represents ECL quenching efficiency of TNP on the whole system. The highest value of ECL quenching efficiency ((I 0-I)/I 0) is obtained at 37 °C. Therefore the optimal temperature is kept at 37 °C for the following ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP using synthesized luminol-SiNPs.
Secondly, kinetic behavior of ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP is also investigated (Fig. 4 ). The proposed ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP is a time-dependent process. After the addition of TNP, ECL intensity of luminol-SiNPs system is sharply decreased. It suggests that ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP using synthesized luminol-SiNPs is a fast response process. After calculating ECL quenching efficiency ((I 0-I)/I 0), it is found that (I 0-I)/I 0 increases in the first 10 min and then decreases in the later time. To reach the maximum signal-to-background ratio and the highest ECL quenching efficiency for the detection of TNP, 10 min is chosen as the optimal condition for analytical purpose in the following experiments.


### SENSITIVITY AND SELECTIVITY OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR “TURN-OFF” DETECTION OF TNP USING SYNTHESIZED LUMINOL-SINPS ###

Fig. 5 shows ECL intensity of the proposed ECRET system in the presence of different concentrations of TNP under the optimal experimental conditions. ECL intensity of luminol-SiNPs system gets decreased with the increasing concentration of TNP from 0.08 to 1 μM. The plot of ECL quenching efficiency ((I 0-I)/I 0) versus the concentration of TNP is shown in Fig. 5B. A good linear relationship is obtained. The linear range of ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP using synthesized luminol-SiNPs is 0.08 – 1 μM with the linear equation of (I 0-I)/I 0 = 0.0046 + 0.5590 c(μM) and with correlation coefficient (r) of 0.9947. The limit of detection (LOD) for “turn-off” detection of TNP is 22.4 nM (3σ, n = 11), which is calculated based on the standard deviation of the response and the slope of the calibration curve. In comparison, luminol is also investigated for TNP detection (Figure S4). The linear range for “turn-off” detection of TNP is 0.8 – 10 μM with the linear equation of (I 0-I)/I 0 = 0.1023 + 0.0223 c(μM), correlation coefficient (r) of 0.9819 and LOD of 190 nM. The ECRET method using luminol-SiNPs is more sensitive than luminol method. LOD of 22.4 nM is comparable to the limit of concentration of TNP in centralized sources of drinking water (GB-3838-2002, 0.005 mg/L or 21.8 nM). Compared with other TNP detection reports (Table 1 ), LOD of ECRET method using synthesized luminol-SiNPs lower than most of other TNP detection. Therefore ECRET “turn-off” detection of TNP using luminol-SiNPs is a novel ultrasensitive method for TNP detection.
Fig. 6 shows the effect of some metal ions or other nitroaromatic compounds on ECRET system for “turn-off” detection of TNP using synthesized luminol-SiNPs. None of metal ions such as Cu2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Mn2+, Ba2+, Ag+ and Mg2+ and other nitroaromatic compounds such as 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2,4-dinitrotoluene (DNT) and 3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid (DNBA) shows similar ECL "turn-off" effect as TNP does. Because the absorption spectrum of metal ions or other nitroaromatic compounds can not effectively overlap with the ECL spectrum of luminol-SiNPs and therefore no ECRET occurs from luminol-SiNPs to metal ions or other nitroaromatic. All these results suggest that ECRET method exhibits high selectivity for “turn-off” detection of TNP towards other nitroaromatic compounds and metal ions. The prepared luminol-SiNPs is also a sensitive and selective promising probe for TNP detection.


### ANALYTICAL APPLICATIONS OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR TNP DETECTION ###

ECRET system using luminol-SiNPs is also applied in “turn-off” detection of TNP in real lake water samples (Table 2 ). When 0.1 μM, 0.5 μM and 1 μM TNP is added respectively, the recoveries from 95.0% to 102.0%, from 96.4% to 100.4%, from 97.5 to 102.1% are obtained respectively. All the relative standard deviations (RSDs) are lower than 5%. It indicates obviously that ECRET probe of luminol-SiNPs is supposed to be useful for the detection of TNP in the field of environmental science.


### KINETIC BEHAVIOR OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR “TURN-ON” DETECTION OF LDC USING LUMINOL-SINPS ###

As mentioned above, the proposed ECRET system using luminol-SiNPs can also be utilized in “turn-on” detection of anesthetic drug LDC owing to the specific interaction between TNP and LDC. The effect of the concentration of TNP on ECL “turn-on” detection of LDC is firstly optimized in supplementary information (Figure S5). To obtain the biggest and stable ECL enhancing efficiency, the optimal concentration of TNP is chosen as 1 μM. Secondly the kinetic behavior of ECRET system for “turn-on” detection of LDC is also investigated in Fig. 7 . The proposed ECRET system for “turn-on” detection of LDC is also a time-dependent process. After the addition of LDC to luminol-SiNPs and TNP mixtures, ECL intensity of the whole system gets enhanced by comparison with that of TNP and luminol-SiNPs mixtures. After the calculation of ECL enhancing efficiency ((I-I 0)/I 0), it is found that ECL enhancing efficiency gets increased obviously in the first 10 min and then reaches a stable value (nearly 0.76, Fig. 7). Therefore the optimal time for ECRET “turn-on” detection of LDC is kept at 10 min.


### SENSITIVITY OF ECRET SYSTEM FOR “TURN-ON” DETECTION OF LDC USING LUMINOL-SINPS ###

Fig. 8 shows ECL intensity of the proposed ECRET system in the presence of different concentrations of LDC. ECL intensity of luminol-SiNPs and TNP mixtures gets enhanced with the increasing concentration of LDC from 0.5 μM to 100 μM. The plot of ECL enhancing efficiency ((I-I 0)/I 0) versus the concentration of LDC is shown in Fig. 8B. A good linear relationship is obtained. It can be seen that the linear range of ECRET system for “turn-on” detection of LDC using synthesized luminol-SiNPs is 0.5 μM to 10 μM with the linear equation of (I-I 0)/I 0 = -0.0109 + 0.0696 c(μM) and with correlation coefficient (r) of 0.9959. The limit of detection (LOD) for “turn-on” detection of LDC is 92.5 nM (3σ, n = 11), which is calculated based on the standard deviation of the response and the slope of the calibration curve. Therefore the proposed ECRET method for “turn-on” detection of TNP using synthesized luminol-SiNPs possesses high sensitivity. More importantly, it is also the first reports about ECRET “turn-on” detection of LDC, which paves an effective way for ECRET to be applied in drug analysis.


### ANALYTICAL APPLICATIONS FOR LDC DETECTION ###

To evaluate the reliability and potential application of the proposed ECRET system for LDC detection, recovery experiments are performed by standard addition methods in human serum samples. The recoveries of human serum samples spiked with 1 μM, 5 μM and 10 μM LDC respectively are obtained (Table 3 ). The average recoveries of LDC in human serum samples range from 93.2% to 101.2%, 96.6% to 101.4%, 95.9% to 102.1% respectively. All the relative standard deviations (RSDs) are lower than 5%. Besides, the proposed ECRET system is also investigated for LDC detection in commercial LDC mucilage standard solution samples. By comparison with LDC mucilage standard solution from three different factories, the average detected content of LDC is comparable with the standard content of LDC (73.86 mM) with relative errors are less than 3% (Table 4 ). It is believed that the proposed ECRET system for “turn-on” detection of LDC is accurate and reliable. It shows the high promising for the application of the novel LDC ECRET detection in drug analysis.


### CONCLUSION ###

In conclusion, it is the first time to develop ECRET strategy using luminol-SiNPs for both “turn-off” detection of TNP and “turn-on” detection of anesthetic drug LDC. The overlap of ECL emission of luminol-SiNPs with the absorption of TNP triggers ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP is used in ECL “turn-off” detection of TNP. Moreover the specific interaction between TNP and LDC consumes TNP and accordingly prevents ECRET from luminol-SiNPs to TNP. Therefore ECL “turn-on” detection of anesthetic drug LDC is also achieved. This ECRET strategy for both detection of TNP and LDC possesses the following advantages. Firstly, it exhibits high sensitivity with LOD of 22.4 nM for TNP and 92.5 nM for LDC, selectivity and fast response (within 10 min). Secondly, ECL of luminol-SiNPs is measured via linear sweep voltammetry by electrochemical reduction of dissolved oxygen at negative potential (– 1 V), which makes reactive oxygen species be generated in situ and ECL of luminol-SiNPs enhanced. No need of adding hydrogen peroxide makes ECL of luminol-SiNPs system more stable than luminol-H2O2 system. Thirdly, the proposed ECRET method is also successfully applied in water, human serum and commercial drug sample detections. It paves an effective and wide way for the application of ECRET in the field of environmental and drug analysis.
