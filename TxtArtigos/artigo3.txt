### TITLE ###

Ratiometric fluorescence based on silver clusters and N, Fe doped carbon dots for determination of H2O2 and UA: N, Fe doped carbon dots as mimetic peroxidase

### ABSTRACT ###



### GRAPHICAL ABSTRACT ###

In this sensing system, uricase catalyzes UA to produce H2O2, and the fluorescent carbon dots with peroxidase activity were used to catalyze H2O2, generating hydroxyl radicals to etch silver clusters.


### ABSTRACT ###

In this study, a dual-emission ratiometric fluorescent sensing system is developed for the analysis of H2O2 and uric acid (UA). This sensing system is based on N, Fe-doped carbon dots (N, Fe-CDs) and bovine serum albumin (BSA)-stabilized silver nanoclusters (BSA-Ag NCs) with peroxidase activity. By the catalysis of uriase, UA was oxidized by oxygen dissolved in the solution and H2O2 is produced. The carbon site with peroxidase activity can catalyze H2O2 to generate hydroxyl radicals, which etches to the BSA-Ag NCs. At the excitation wavelength of 390 nm, there were dual emission peaks at 450 nm and 650 nm. With increasing concentration of H2O2 or UA, the red fluorescence at 650 nm for BSA-Ag NCs decreases due to the etching of BSA-Ag NCs by hydroxyl radicals, while the blue fluorescence of N, Fe-CDs is restored. Based on the relationship between the fluorescence intensity ratio (I450/I650) to detect H2O2 and UA, the linear range of H2O2 was 0.6–100 μM with a detection limit of 0.30 μM (S/N = 3), while the linear range of UA was 0.8–133 μM with a detection limit of 0.49 μM (S/N = 3). Moreover, the present method was used for the analysis of UA in human urine with satisfactory accuracy as well as recovery. This ratiometric fluorescent approach can be used for analysis of UA and H2O2 for biomedical applications.

### INTRODUCTION ###

As the main product of purine metabolism in human [1], uric acid (UA) is slightly soluble in water and the normal amount of UA in human urine is around 1.49–4.46 mM [2]. Abnormal UA content will do harm to human’s health and lead to various diseases, such as gout, kidney stones, hypertension, hyperuricemia, cardiovascular disease, and Lesch-Nyhan syndrome, etc [3]. Therefore, UA is an important marker for these diseases, and detection of UA is highly important. In recent years, a plethora of approaches have been developed for determination of UA, including electrochemical methods [4,5] {Sun, 2011 #29;Yu, 2016 #30}, chemiluminescence [6,7], colorimetry [8,9] and chromatography [10]. Compared with these methods, the fluorescence method has great advantages, including low cost, convenience, rapidity and high sensitivity. Most reported methods for detecting UA are generally based on detecting the fluorescence intensity at a single emission wavelength [11–14], but such methods are susceptible to the test instrument and the external environment. For the ratiometric method, it is based on the measurement of the intensity ratio of two different wavelengths. It has a self-calibration function, accurate and high sensitivity, and is also used for the determination of H2O2 and UA [15–17]. For example, Xiao et al. reported a ratiometric fluorescence based on oxOPD and B, N-CDs to detect H2O2 and UA, in which ZnFe2O4 magnetic microspheres with peroxidase activity catalyze H2O2 to oxidize o-phenylenediamine to produce oxOPD [15]. Meng et al. synthesized catalase-modified gold nanoclusters (CAT-GNCs) by one-step method in 15 min for the determination of H2O2 and UA, and CAT-GNCs have dual fluorescence emission [16]. Yang et al. designed a ratiometric fluorescence sensor based on BSA-Au NCs and coumarin using Fenton effect. Fe2+ catalyzed H2O2 quenching BSA-Au NCs fluorescence and oxidized coumarin to produce fluorescence, achieving the purpose of detecting H2O2 and UA [17].
Metal nanoclusters (such as Au, Ag, Cu), as a new class of fluorophores, are widely used to construct biosensors due to their unique physical and optical properties. Metal nanoclusters possess a variety of advantages, such as simple and convenient synthesis, good light stability, large Stokes shift and low toxicity, thus they have potential applications in bioimaging, catalysis and medicine [18,19]{Wang, 2016 #83;He, 2018 #84}. Among the metal nanoclusters, Ag NCs are widely used in fluorescent biosensors. To date, various materials such as thiols, DNA polymers, polypeptides and proteins have been used to synthesize silver nanoclusters. Ag NCs have extensive application in the detection of metal ions [20–22], thiol molecules [23], DNA [24], protein [25], intracellular staining [26], etc. Bovine serum albumin (BSA) has a strong affinity for inorganic salts, thus it is used as stabilizer and capping agent in the process of synthesizing nanoparticles [27].
Based on the unique properties, hemin has been used in biomimetic catalysis for the synthesis of carbon dots. Because it is insoluble in water but soluble in acidic acetone or alkaline aqueous solution, branched polyethyleneimine (PEI) is beneficial to increase its solubility [28]. In order to increase its fluorescence intensity, citric acid can be used together with hemin and PEI as the carbon precursor [29]. The resulting N, Fe-CDs possess good fluorescent properties as well as peroxidase properties.
Herein, we report a ratiometric fluorescent sensing system based on BSA-Ag NCs and N, Fe-CDs doped carbon dots for the determination of UA. Firstly, H2O2 is produced through the catalytic oxidation of UA by uricase. Next, the carbon dots catalyze H2O2 to generate hydroxyl radicals that etch silver nanoclusters. The intensity of the red fluorescence emission of BSA-Ag NCs decreases, while the intensity of the blue emission of N, Fe-CDs increases. This method has good selectivity and high sensitivity with a low detection limit, and it was applied in the determination of UA in urine samples. This method can be used for sensitive and convenient determination of UA in biomedical applications.


### EXPERIMENTAL ###



### MATERIALS ###

The reagents of UA, UA oxidase, bovine serum albumin, H2O2, sodium hydroxide, silver nitrate, urea, creatinine, oxalic acid, ascorbic acid, citric acid, lactose, galactose, fructose, and sucrose, glucose, tyrosine, arginine, histidine, glutamic acid, glycine, alanine, phenylalanine, valine, serine, dopamine, glutathione, cysteine, KCl, MgCl2, NaCl, CaCl2, UA oxidase and UA were obtained from Shanghai Ryon Biological Technology CO., Ltd.(Shanghai, China). Phosphotungstic acid kit was manufactured by from Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute (Nanjing, China). All reagents were analytically pure without further procession before use. PBS buffer (pH 7.2–7.4) used in the experiment was purchased from KeLong (Chengdu, China). Ultrapure water (Milli-Q, 18.25 MΩ cm) was used as solvent during the whole experiment.


### INSTRUMENTATION ###

Fluorescence spectroscopy experiments were performed on FL-4600 fluorescence spectrometer (Hitachi, Japan). The fixed excitation wavelength was 390 nm and the fluorescence spectra were in the range of 330–760 nm, with the excitation and emission slit widths of 10.0 nm and 20.0 nm, respectively. The scanning speed was 1200 nm/min with a response time of 2.0 s, and the PMT voltage was 610 V. UV–vis spectrophotometer (Beijing Purkinje General Instrument Co. Ltd., Beijing, China) was used for collecting the absorption spectrum of BSA-Ag NCs. BSA-Ag NCs and N, Fe-CDs TEM images were collected with JEM 2100 high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) (JEOL, Japan). Hydrated particle size and surface charge were measured by ZS90 (Malvern Instruments Ltd, UK). The Escalab 250 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, US) was used to characterize the elemental composition and valence states.


### SYNTHESIS OF N, FE-CDS ###

The N, Fe-CDs was prepared based on a previous report with certain modifications [28]. Specifically, 2.0 g PEI was mixed in 10 mL deionized water, followed by the addition of 50.0 mg hemin and 20.0 mg trisodium citrate. They were mixed ultrasonically and heated for 10 h at 180 °C in an autoclave. Final product was preserved at 4 °C for further use.


### SYNTHESIS OF BSA-AG NCS ###

BSA-Ag NCs was prepared by a modified method according to a previous report [30]. 10.0 mL BSA solution (50 mg/mL) was placed at room temperature, then 10.0 mL AgNO3 (0.01 M) was slowly added to the BSA solution with vigorous stirring. After reacting for 30 min, 0.6 mL NaOH solution (1.0 M) was added to the solution with continuous stirring for another 30 min. Subsequently, 4.0 mL NaBH4 (0.01 M) was added and mixed well with continuous stirring for 2.5 h. Once the color of the BSA-Ag NCs solution turned to reddish brown, this synthesis was completed and the final solution was stored at 4 °C for subsequent experiment.


### H2O2 DETERMINATION ###

Firstly, 10 μL 30% H2O2 and 100 μL N, Fe-CDs solution were diluted to 100 mL and 5 mL, respectively. Next, 50 μL diluted carbon dots solution was mixed with 400 μL BSA-Ag NCs, and H2O2 solution was added to reach an overall volume of 3 mL. After incubation for 35 min at 35 °C, fluorescence measurement was performed. Since H2O2 is easily decomposed, it is necessary to reconstitute the H2O2 solution once the concentration of H2O2 is appreciably reduced. The fluorescence emission spectrum was collected with an excitation wavelength of 390 nm.


### UA DETECTION ###

UA oxidase (10 unit/mg) was dissolved in PBS buffer (pH 7.2–7.4) to prepare a urate oxidase solution, which was stored at −20 °C for subsequent use.
UA of different volumes was mixed into the enzyme solution with identical concentration, and the mixture was incubated at 37 °C for 60 min. Afterwards, the N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs were added to the mixture, and the resulting mixture was kept at 35 °C for 35 min with a final volume of 3 mL. Subsequent experimental steps are the same as above.


### DETERMINATION OF UA IN HUMAN URINE ###

Adult male and female’s urine samples were collected to assess the performance of the proposed sensing system, which were incubated in water at 50 °C for further use. The measurement conditions and experimental procedures are the same as above. The concentration of UA is derived from the I450/I650 on the working curve. The pretreated samples were detected according to the proposed method. The recovery of the spiked sample was then measured. The concentrations of UA in urine samples were calculated from the linear equation.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### FEASIBILITY AND PRINCIPLE OF RATIOMETRIC FLUORESCENCE ###

To test the feasibility of the experiment, the emission fluorescence of N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs were firstly measured. The fluorescence emission of carbon dots and silver clusters were at 450 nm and 650 nm, respectively (Fig. 1 A). By fixing the amount of N, Fe-CDs and changing the concentration of silver clusters (Fig. 1B), it can be observed that as the concentration of the silver clusters increases, the fluorescence emission intensity of the N, Fe-CDs decreases, and the fluorescence intensity of BSA-Ag NCs increases. Then the optimal excitation wavelength of the ratio fluorescent sensing system was measured. With the excitation wavelength of 390 nm, the emission peak shape of the carbon dots was better, and the fluorescence ratio of the carbon dots and the silver clusters was the lowest (Fig. 1C). As shown in Fig. 1D, the fluorescence of N, Fe-CDs did not significantly change after mixing with UA and uricase, respectively. However, with the addition of UA and uricase at the same time, the fluorescence intensity of BSA-Ag NCs decreased, while the fluorescence intensity of the carbon dots significantly recovered. These results demonstrate that the present ratiometric fluorescent sensing system is feasible.
Silver clusters can be etched by hydroxyl radicals to quench the fluorescence of silver clusters. Up to now, many reports have shown that H2O2 can quench the fluorescence of metal nanoclusters [31–33]. When H2O2 is catalyzed into hydroxyl radicals, the oxidizing ability of hydroxyl radicals is much stronger than that of H2O2, and hydroxyl radicals can quench metal nanoclusters more efficiently [17,34]. Based on this, a new fluorescence analysis method is established in this study for determine of UA. The principle for this sensing system is illustrated in Scheme 1 . In the presence of oxygen, UA is catalyzed by uricase to produce H2O2 and allantoin. N, Fe-CDs possesses fluorescent properties as well as peroxidase activity, and it can catalyze H2O2 to generate hydroxyl radicals to etch silver clusters, thereby effectively quenching the fluorescence of silver clusters. The combination of the fluorescent emission from silver clusters and carbon dots forms a ratiometric fluorescence sensing system. Thus, using H2O2 as an intermediary, a UA biosensor was constructed to achieve quantitative determination.


### PEROXIDASE ACTIVITY OF N, FE-CDS ###

N, Fe-CDs act similarly to Fenton's reagents, and it has peroxidase activity that catalyzes the production of hydroxyl radicals by H2O2 [28]. In order to study the catalytic activity of N, Fe-CD, different concentrations of H2O2 were added in the presence or absence of N, Fe-CDs (Fig. S1). It was found that in the presence of N, Fe-CDs, as the concentration of H2O2 increased, the color of TMB changed from colorless to blue, and it had an absorption peak at 652 nm (Fig. S1 A). When N, Fe-CDs are absent, as the concentration of H2O2 increases, TMB remains colorless and will not be oxidized (Fig. S1B). In addition, other typical peroxidase substrates were tested in the presence of H2O2 and N, Fe-CDs. It can be seen from Fig. S2 that N, Fe-CDs catalyze H2O2 to produce OH radicals to deoxidize ABTS and OPD, the color of OPD turns yellow and ABTS turns green.


### CHARACTERIZATION OF THE N, FE-CDS ###

To characterize the morphology and size of the carbon dots, HRTEM and DLS data were collected. Fig. 2 A shows that the carbon dots are mono-dispersed with spherical or spheroidal morphology. The average diameter is approximately 10.7 nm (Fig. 2B). Zeta potential of the surface of the carbon dots is -9.54 mV (Fig. 2C), indicating that the N, Fe-CDs has a negative charge. which can be caused by partial ionization of the hydroxyl or carboxyl groups on the carbon dots to form oxygen anions.
It is essential to learn the surface composition and chemical state of N, Fe-CDs, which can be characterized by XPS analysis. The full scan XPS spectrum (Fig. 3 A) shows that the elements of C, N, O and Fe are present in the carbon dots. The high-resolution spectrum of C 1s displays three component peaks at 284.7, 286.1 and 288.1 eV, due to the CC, CO/CN and CO, COOH bonds, respectively (Fig. 3B). The N1s spectrum of the carbon dots (Fig. 3C) shows three peaks at 399.1, 400.6 and 401.7 eV corresponding to the CNC, N(C)3 and NH bonds, respectively. The peak energy of O 1 s is approximately 532.1 eV ( Fig. 3D), corresponding to the CO/CO bonds. The binding energy of the high resolution XPS spectrum at 711.8, 724.4 eV satellite peak confirmed the presence of Fe (Fig. 3E). [28,35]


### CHARACTERIZATION OF THE BSA-AG NCS ###

In order to investigate the morphology, chemical structure and composition of BSA-Ag NCs, HRTEM, zeta potential and XPS data were collected. From the TEM image, the silver clusters are well dispersed without obvious aggregation, which clearly shows the high crystallinity of BSA-Ag NCs with a lattice parameter of 0.14 nm. The surface zeta potential is -28.5 mV, indicating that BSA-Ag NCs are negatively charged. The XPS spectrum (Fig. S3 A) shows that the binding energies correspond to C 1s, N 1s and O 1s. The high-resolution spectrum of C 1 s (Fig. S3B) shows peaks of 284.8, 286.2, 288.1 and 289.6 eV, corresponding to CC, CO/CN and CO, COOH bonds, respectively [36]. The N1s spectrum is mainly the CN bond corresponding to 399.9 eV (Fig. S3C). As shown in Fig. S3D, the spectrum of O 1 s correspond to CO/CO. Besides, a weak peak at a binding energy was observed due to Ag 3d. As shown in Fig. S3E, for the Ag 3d XPS spectrum, the satellite peaks of 368.1 and 374 eV in the high-resolution spectrum verified the existence of Ag0, indicating the successful synthesis of BSA-Ag NCs [37].


### OPTIMIZATION OF THE EXPERIMENTAL FACTORS ###

The experimental conditions were optimized using the intermediate of H2O2. The reaction temperature, time and stability were adjusted. As shown in Fig. S4, the higher temperature has a significant impact on the system. In the absence of H2O2, the temperature itself affects the fluorescence emission intensity of the sensing system. The ratio of I450/I650 is maximal at 35 °C. As for the time, it was observed that the system gradually became stable after 35 min, thus 35 °C and 35 min (Fig. S5) were selected as the optimum conditions for subsequent experiments. The ratiometric sensing system is barely affected by different concentrations of NaCl solution and pH (Fig. S6), which proves that the system is relatively stable.


### IFE BETWEEN N, FE-CDS AND BSA-AG NCS ###

To investigate the fluorescence quenching between the two fluorescence emission constituting the ratiometric fluorescence, the UV–vis spectrum and the fluorescence emission spectrum were collected. As exhibited in Fig. S7, the BSA-Ag NCs have a wide absorption band (380–700 nm), and the UV–vis spectrum of BSA-Ag NCs overlaps with the fluorescence emission spectrum of N, Fe-CDs (450 nm). Thus, it is speculated that the fluorescence quenching mechanism is either fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) or internal filter effect (IFE). IFE refers to the concentration effect caused by the overlap of emission and absorption spectra of fluorescent substances [38]. To confirm the quenching mechanism, as shown in Fig. S8, the UV–vis spectrum of the BSA-Ag NCs solution was recorded in the absence and presence of N, Fe-CDs. In the presence and absence of N, Fe-CDs, the position and intensity of the absorption band of BSA-Ag NCs remains essentially the same, indicating that no complex was formed and FRET did not occur between BSA-Ag NCs and N, Fe-CDs. In order to further verify the mechanism of quenching due to IFE, the zeta potential was measured. The zeta potentials of N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs were -9.54 mV (Fig. 2C) and -28.50 mV, respectively (Fig. 4 B). N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs all have negative charge, thus there is no electrostatic interaction between them. N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs will not stick together, and the distance between them should be more than 10 nm [39]. It is speculated that the quenching mechanism of N, Fe-CDs/BSA-Ag NCs double-emitting fluorescent probes is IFE rather than FRET [15]. Therefore, fluorescence resonance energy transfer is unlikely to occur between the carbon dots and the silver clusters, and the fluorescence change between N, Fe-CDs and BSA-Ag NCs is attributed to IFE.


### CALIBRATION CURVES AND DETECTION LIMIT ###

Prior to the determination of UA, the N, Fe-CDs/BSA-Ag NCs ratiometric fluorescence was used to determine H2O2, which is an intermediate product obtained by uricase catalyzing UA. Under optimal experimental conditions, as the concentration of H2O2 increases, the fluorescent emission intensity of BSA-Ag NCs at 650 nm decreases, but the fluorescent emission of N, Fe-CDs at 450 nm increases (Fig. 5 A). The linear range of H2O2 was 0.6–100 μM with a good linear relationship with the fluorescent emission ratio (Fig. 5B). The linear equation of H2O2 is I450/I650 = 0.03994CH2O2 + 0.55863 (CH2O2: μM), and the detection limit is 0.30 μM (R2 = 0.99296) (S/N = 3). F represents the fluorescence emission ratio of N, Fe-CDs/BSA-Ag NCs in the system, and C is the concentration of H2O2. These results show that N, Fe-CDs/BSA-Ag NCs are sensitive to the fluorescence response of H2O2. This method is comparable to previous reports on determination of H2O2 by fluorescence (Table. S1).
The response of the sensing system to UA was determined under optimized experimental conditions. It is observed in Fig. 6 A, with increasing UA concentration, the intensity of fluorescence emission from BSA-Ag NCs decreases, while the intensity of the fluorescence emission from N, Fe-CDs increases. With the concentration range of UA between 0.8 and 133 μM, there is a good linear relationship between intensity ratio and UA concentration, with a detection limit of 0.49 μM (S/N = 3) (Fig. 6B). The corresponding linear equation is as follow: I450/I650 = 0.02439CUA + 0.52866 (CUA: μM, R2 = 0.99106). As shown in Fig. 6B, F is the ratio of the fluorescence intensity of the composite sensor in the system and C refers to the concentration of UA. As shown in Table 1 , this sensing system has high sensitivity for the determination of UA. Compared with other literature reports on UA’s detection, this method has significant advantages (Table S2).


### THE SELECTIVITY ###

The selectivity of the present method was studied against the interfering substances (Fig. 7 ) including ascorbic acid, citric acid, dopamine, KCl, MgCl2, NaCl, CaCl2, oxalic acid, glucose, lactose, sucrose, fructose, galactose, urea, alanine, glycine, glutamic acid, arginine, tyrosine, histidine, phenylalanine, proline, serine, cysteine and glutathione. The concentration of inorganic salt ions and urea is 100 times that of UA, the concentration of amino acids and other substances is 10 times that of UA. Glutathione and cysteine interfere with the system, but their content in urine is much lower than that of UA [40], so they take half of the UA concentration. The I450/I650 ratio of the final detected interfering substances is far below the UA, so the degree of change in the fluorescence intensity of the system caused by these interfering substances is negligible compared with UA. Thus, this method has reasonably high selectivity.


### DETECTION OF UA IN HUMAN URINE ###

To evaluate the practicability of this method, human urine samples were collected to detect the UA content. The test results of the sensing system were compared with the phosphotungstic acid kit. The precision and accuracy of this sensing system was assessed by determination of the recovery of spiked samples. As illustrated in Table 1, the results from the present sensing system were consistent with those obtained by the phosphotungstic acid kit. The recovery is 96.17%–104.58% and the RSD is 2.38%–5.27%. Hence, the dual-emission ratiometric fluorescent sensor is suitable for the determination of UA content in human urine. This method has great potential for application in analysis of UA in real blood samples, including clinical blood samples.


### CONCLUSION ###

In this work, a ratiometric fluorescence determination method for UA based on BSA-Ag NCs and N, Fe-CDs was constructed. In this sensing system, uricase catalyzes UA to produce H2O2, and the fluorescent carbon dots with peroxidase activity were used to catalyze H2O2, generating hydroxyl radicals to etch silver clusters and achieving the purpose of detecting UA. This method delivers a broad linear range of 0.8–133 μM and a low detection limit of 0.49 μM (S/N = 3). This method exhibits high selectivity and sensitivity under various conditions, and has been applied to determine H2O2 and UA. It is expected that this method can be widely applied to the detection of various compounds involving H2O2 as the intermediate.
