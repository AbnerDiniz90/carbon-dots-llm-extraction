### ABSTRACT ###

This work initially describes a sample-in-answer-out paper-based assay for simple, rapid, sensitive, specific, and low-cost detection of silver (I) (Ag+) ions. It combines Ag+-triggered in situ autocatalysis for signal amplification with an instrument-free, timing readout in 3D origami paper-based analytical devices integrated with nanopore membranes (NMs). The autocatalytic amplification is based on the oxidability of Ag+ towards 3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) loaded in the NMs. The Ag+ ions are rapidly reduced by the TMB to form silver nanoparticles that further act as nanocatalysts to catalyse the oxidation of more TMB molecules to their derivatives by Ag+ ions for producing bigger (and more) metal particles. The NM’s pores are thus partially blocked by these metal particles to adjust (prolong) the flowing time of a coloured reagent in the paper device. Under optimized conditions, this developed point-of-need method could quantitatively detect the Ag+ ions in a linear concentration range of 1.9–62.5 nM within 4 min, by simply using a ubiquitous phone (or a timer). Its detection limit was estimated to be ˜0.91 nM according to the 3σ rule. The recoveries of analyzing real water examples (i.e., pond water, tap water, drinking water, and soil water) were from ˜95.2 to ˜103.9%, and the corresponding relative standard deviations ranged from 3.3 to 8.1% (n = 6).

### INTRODUCTION ###

With rapid, increasing global industrializations, the contamination of water by heavy metal ions has become one of the most serious environmental problems facing the world today [1]. Silver (I) (Ag+) ion belongs to the highest toxicity class of heavy metal pollutants, together with cadmium, chromium (VI), copper, and mercury [2]. As it is widely used in the electronic, pharmaceutical, and photographic industries, the annual amount of Ag+ released to the surface water from industrial wastes and emissions has been estimated at ca. 80 tons 40 years ago [2]. According to the reports of US Environmental Protection Agency, the Ag+ ion at levels above 1.6 nM is toxic to fishes and microorganisms, and its maximum acceptable concentration in drinking water is restricted to 0.9 μM [3]. The excessive Ag+ intake would cause argyria in humans and damage nervous, renal, immune, and cardiovascular systems.4−6 Therefore, these environmental and health problems caused by the Ag+ have prompted researchers to develop efficient methods for sensitive, specific detection of this contaminant for environmental protection, waste management, and water safety.
The existing toolbox toward this goal for the Ag+ detection mainly includes traditional techniques such as atomic absorption and emission spectrometry [7,8] and inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry [9] and some recently established methods, e.g., fluorescence spectrometry (with organic dyes [10] or inorganic quantum dots such as carbon dots [11]), absorption spectrometry (typically adopting gold nanoparticles as colorimetric probes [12,13]), electrochemical sensors [14,15], and paper-based test strips [16,17]. The instrumental analysis techniques based on spectrometry and electrochemistry can offer high sensitivity, but most of them may suffer from the drawbacks of bulky, complicated, costly instrumentation, or time-consuming sample pretreatment processes. Moreover, the paper strips for Ag+ disclosed by far just enabled qualitative analysis of nanomolar analyte under tedious and precise control of reaction temperatures. Thus, it still remains a challenge to develop more desirable assays that allow for simple, rapid, economic, and portable detection and quantification of Ag+ at nanomolar levels to meet the growing demand of on-site or point-of-need water monitoring by limitedly-trained personnel or even unskilled home-users.
In this communication, we respond to this challenge by designing a type of 3D point-of-care testing (POCT) device for instrument-free timing assay of sub-nanomolar Ag+ ions that uses a target-triggered in situ autocatalysis strategy for facile, efficient signal amplification and a ubiquitous phone for portable quantitative readout. Detailed experimental section was provided in Supplementary Material. The construction of such a POCT device is shown in Fig. 1 A. Each device is composed of two layers of function thin membranes that are patterned with hollow structures via laser cutting [18]. The layer 1 is a piece of polycarbonate nanopore membrane (PNM, ˜50 nm in average pore size) that has a 5 mm-diameter circular function zone a. The layer 2 is a piece of nitrocellulose membrane that has a 5 mm-diameter circular function zone b and a 1 mm × 2.5 cm rectangular function zone c. The 1 cm-length part of zone c (beginning from its circular part) is marked with a 1 mm-length hollow bar cut by laser (as indicated by a red arrow in Fig. 1A). Supporting (non-functional) zones of the two hollow structure-patterned membranes are hydrophobized with wax [19]. The zones a and b are loaded with 3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and a coloured reagent (red ink in this case), respectively. The two layers of reagent-loaded function membranes are assembled into a 3D device and are further sandwiched (packaged) by two pieces of transparent adhesive tape. Note that, the top of zone a in the layer 1 is covered solely with a small piece of adhesive tape that can be taken off for sample introduction (as indicated by a red dotted box in Fig. 1A).
As illustrated in Fig. 1B, our POCT device for Ag+ assay involves two key operations: 1) addition of a sample solution to trigger the in situ autocatalysis for producing silver nanoparticles in the PNM; and 2) measurement of the time required for the red ink (re-dissolved in zone b by reaction solution) flowing to the hollow bar mark in the POCT device using a phone. The “autocatalysis” refers to that the Ag+ ions are reduced by the TMB to form silver nanoparticles that in turn automatically catalyze the oxidation of more TMB molecules to their derivatives (TMBox) by Ag+ ions. As a result, bigger (and more) metal particles can be formed. The as-formed metal particles could partially block the PNM’s nanopores, thus prolonging the flowing time of red ink. The number of blocked pores in the PNM and the ink’s flowing time positively rely on the Ag+ concentration in the sample. The results demonstrate that the proposed instrument-free method only needs a ubiquitous phone (or a commercially available, far cheaper timer) to sensitively, selectively quantify the Ag+ levels in artificial water samples and real samples of pond water, tap water, drinking water, and soil water within several minutes. In particular, amplified detection of Ag+ with a limit less than 1 nM could be achieved, which makes our assay one of the most sensitive Ag+ sensing approaches compared with many other recently reported methods [4–6,10–15,20–22] (Table S1 in Supplementary Material).


### MATERIAL AND METHODS ###



### MATERIALS AND APPARATUS ###

3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) and dimethylsulfoxide were purchased from Sangon Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Silver nitrate (AgNO3) and other metal salts or acids were obtained from Xilong Chemical Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Red ink is the product of Shanghai Hero Group Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The original red ink solution is composed of solvents, red dyes, resins, solubilizers, surfactants, and other materials. Besides the red ink, other types of coloured reagents such as blue ink and food green dye are also suitable. All other chemicals were of analytical grade and used as received without further purification. Unless otherwise specified, all solutions were prepared with deionized water (with a specific resistivity >18.2 MΩ•cm) from an ultrapure water system (UPS-II-20 L) bought from Chengdu Yuechun Technology Co., Ltd. (Chengdu, China). The stock solutions of TMB (40 mM) were prepared in dimethylsulfoxide and diluted with water to required concentrations before use. The real water samples include pond water collected from a pond in our campus, tap water obtained from our lab, commercially available drinking water (the product of Hangzhou Wahaha Co., Ltd., Hangzhou, China), and soil water collected from the vicinity of a factory.
Hydrophilic polycarbonate nanopore membranes (PNMs) (Whatman, ˜50 nm in average pore size, ˜3 × 108 pore/cm2 in average pore density) were the products of GE Healthcare Life Sciences. Nitrocellulose membrane (˜0.2 μm in average pore size) was from Shanghai Sinopharm Group Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Mini-type CO2 laser cutting machine (XB-3020) was provided by Shangdong Xinbang Laser Equipment Co., Ltd. (Shandong, China). Optical characterization of reaction solutions was performed on a UV–vis spectrometer (Cary 50, Varian, USA). The morphology and element composition of nanoparticles formed in the reaction solutions were characterized using a transmission electron microscope (TEM, JEM-2100F, JEOL, Japan) equipped with energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS). The morphologies of the PNMs used in the assays of a blank sample and an Ag+ sample were characterized by a scanning electron microscope (SEM, SU 5000, Hitachi, Japan).


### PATTERNING OF FUNCTION THIN MEMBRANES AND IMMOBILIZATION OF TMB AND RED INK ###

For fabrication of a 3D point-of-care testing (POCT) device, a piece of PNM (2 cm × 2 cm) and a piece of nitrocellulose membrane (2 cm × 4 cm) were patterned with hollow microstructures using a previous laser cutting method [18]. The patterns were designed on a computer with Coreldraw X5 drawing software. The PNM has a 5 mm-diameter circular function zone a. The nitrocellulose membrane has a 5 mm-diameter circular function zone b and a 1 mm × 2.5 cm rectangular function zone c. The 1 cm-length of zone c beginning from the end of zone b was marked with a 1 mm-length hollow line. The one-step patterning of laser cutting was carried out in the laser machine at an applied current of 5 mA, a cutting rate of 20 mm/s, a laser wavelength of 10.64 μm, and a laser power of 40 W. After paper cutting, supporting (non-functional) zones of the two hollow structure-patterned membranes were further hydrophobized by wax [19].
Then, ˜2.5 μL of a 200 μM TMB solution and ˜2.5 μL of a red ink solution were spotted on the zone a in the PNM and the zone b in the nitrocellulose membrane, respectively, and allowed to air dry. The TMB-loaded PNM (as layer 1) and ink-loaded nitrocellulose membrane (as layer 2) were assembled into a 3D device that was further sandwiched by two pieces of transparent adhesive tape. It should be noted that the top zone a in the device was covered solely with another small piece of adhesive tape. The freshly as-prepared TMB- and ink-immobilized 3D POCT devices were stored in a sealed bag and kept away from light under room temperature. They could remain stable for at least one month without significant loss of assay sensitivity. More satisfactory storage stability might be expected if using vacuum packaging technique to package these devices.


### ASSAY PROCEDURES ###

In a typical assay, after taking off the small piece of adhesive tape that covered the top zone a of a 3D POCT device, 10 μL of an Ag+ sample solution (artificial sample in deionized water or real water sample) was added onto this function zone. Then a phone (i.e., iPhone 5 s) or a timer was immediately used to record the time required for the red ink (re-dissolved in the zone b by the reaction solution) to flow to the marker line in the zone c of the device. The response signal of flowing time change (ΔT) was defined as ΔT = Ts − Tb , where Ts and Tb were the flowing time recorded for the Ag+ sample and a blank sample (i.e., deionized water without the analyte), respectively, and used to estimate the Ag+ concentration. In addition, comparative (specificity) experiments were carried out in the same way but using other eighteen types of metal ions (i.e., Pt4+, Cr3+, Al3+, Fe3+, Au3+, Hg2+, Pb2+, Cd2+, Cu2+, Fe2+, Ni2+, Ca2+, Zn2+, Mg2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Na+, and K+) instead of the analyte Ag+.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###

The core concept of this new assay focuses on the conversion of the Ag+ detection into the measurement of the blocked degree of PNM’s pores in the POCT device. Therefore, the analyte-triggered autocatalytic production of silver nanoparticles via the Ag+-TMB redox reactions was first verified. As shown in Fig. 2 A, the original TMB solution (5 mM) is colourless (image 1), and no meaningful absorption was measured during the tested wavelength range of 350–800 nm in its UV–vis spectrum (curve 1). As expected, the mixing of 13 nM Ag+ resulted in a blue reaction mixture (image 2); it exhibits three strong characteristic absorption peaks at 370, 458, and 659 nm (curve 2), indicating the oxidation of TMB to TMBox by Ag+ [23–25]. As additionally shown in the real-time Video S1 in Supplementary Material, the redox reactions between the Ag+ and TMB was quite fast. The accompanying colour change from colourless to blue in the solution could be clearly observed in a few seconds as soon as the mixing operation was done.
In order to understand such a fast chromogenic reaction, its resulting mixture was further characterized using both transmission electron microscope (TEM) imaging and energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) techniques. As displayed in Fig. 2B, a large number of spherical or ellipsoidal silver nanoparticles had been unexpectedly generated during the Ag+-TMB redox reactions. According to this direct experimental evidence, the reason for the rapid colour change occurred in the mixture solution could be presumably explained as follows. When the Ag+ sample was mixed with the TMB solution, the analyte ions were reduced by TMB to zerovalent silvers which in turn formed small silver nanoparticles [26]. These small metal particles further served as in situ, autocatalytic, active nanoseeds capable of catalyzing more TMB molecules to their derivatives (TMBox) by Ag+ ions. Meanwhile, more Ag+ ions were reduced to zerovalent metal species, thus either making these nanocatalysts themselves grow or producing more particles [27].
After demonstrating the Ag+-triggered in situ autocatalytic formations of silver nanoparticles, the feasibility of the designed POCT devices for timing detection of Ag+ was then validated. As shown in Fig. 3 A, when a blank water sample (without analyte) was analyzed, it took ˜19.3 s for the red ink solution to flow to the hollow bar mark in layer 2 of the device used (Fig. 3A, right). Scanning electron microscope (SEM) image of the zone a in corresponding layer 1 revealed obviously that almost all of PNM’s circular nanopores kept their original “open” state facilitating the ink’s rapid passing (Fig. 3B). On the other hand, for a 13 nM Ag+ sample, no flow of red ink was observed during the 19.3-s period (Fig. 3C, middle). After ˜80.4 s, this coloured reagent finally reached the hollow bar mark (Fig. 3C, right). The far longer flowing time of red ink should be based on a fact that the Ag+-triggered autocatalysis enabled the in situ creation of lots of silver nanoparticles in the zone a of used PNM, which was clearly confirmed by its SEM and EDS results (Fig. 3D). Since these particles blocked (“closed”) many pores of PNM, the volume and flowing rate of re-dissolved red ink solution were highly limited (reduced). In addition, it was experimentally found that too high Ag+ levels (e.g., >10 μM) would lead to decreases in the ink’s flowing time in the device, while its PNM surface colour changed from white to blue because of the relatively large amount of blue TMBox produced. This unusual phenomenon may be attributed to the generation of too big silver particles in the presence of excess Ag+ ions, resulting in non-uniform blocking of nanopores in the whole body of zone a in the PNM used.
Next, to test the selectivity (specificity) of the proposed method, fifteen samples of other potential interfering metal ions in water (i.e., Pt4+, Cr3+, Al3+, Fe3+, Au3+, Hg2+, Pb2+, Cd2+, Cu2+, Fe2+, Ni2+, Ca2+, Zn2+, Mg2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Na+, and K+; 10 μM each) were separately added into fifteen POCT devices, followed by timing measurement, comparing with the results of assaying a blank sample and a 13 nM Ag+ sample. As shown in Fig. 4 A, for Pt4+, Al3+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Ca2+, Na+, or K+, a similar short flowing time of ca. 19.7 s was measured for each type of ion. No meaningful difference was observed between such time response and that obtained from the blank sample (ca. 19.3 s). Moreover, a relatively long flowing time of red ink (ca. 21.1 s) was recorded for other eleven types of interfering ions. In the Ag+ assay, however, a long flowing time up to ˜80.7 s was obtained, although the analyte level (13 nM) was about three orders of magnitude lower than that of the tested non-specific ions (10 μM). The data implied that only the Ag+ analyte could specifically trigger the in situ autocatalytic formations of silver nanoparticles to efficiently block the PNM’s nanopores in the used POCT device. Moreover, it was interesting to find that the TMB’s oxidation by Fe3+ and Au3+ ions producing TMBox could also result in two blue mixture solutions (insets of Fig. S1 in Supplementary Material). Nevertheless, the red ink’s flowing time as low as ˜21.2 s recorded for the two types of ions along with the results of scanning electron microscope image (Fig. S1 in Supplementary Material) suggested that there were no (or negligible) metal nanoparticles generated in the zones a of involved PNMs.
Before the evaluation of the analytical ability of our devices, the main experimental factors have been studied and optimized, including TMB volume and concentration and sample volume. The flowing time change (ΔT) of red ink between an Ag+ sample tested (7 nM) and a blank sample was calculated for each experiment. Figs. S2 and S3 in Supplementary Material show the effects of the volume and concentration of TMB loaded in PNMs on the proposed Ag+ assay. One can find that there were no obvious increases in ΔT values when the TMB volume and concentration were more than 1.5 μL and 5 mM, respectively, which were recommended for the PNM modification. Moreover, as can be seen in Fig. S4 in Supplementary Material, the sample volume could also play an important role in the analysis. When the volume of Ag+ sample increased from 6 to 10 μL, the final amount of Ag particles generated in the PNMs greatly increased allowing ΔT values to arise from 5.5 up to 33.1. However, the continuing increase in sample volumes, i.e., from 10 to 14 μL, enabled the formation of too large particles with ΔT values changing from 33.1 down to 27.2. The optimal sample volume of 10 μL was thereby chosen for all of the experiments.
Then, a series of artificial water samples with varying Ag+ concentrations were assayed under optimal conditions. The ink’s ΔT value between each Ag+ sample and a blank sample was calculated. As shown in Fig. 4B, as the analyte level increases from 1.9 to 96 nM, the ΔT increases from 15.1 to 196.2 s, suggesting Ag+-controlled timing responses of the coloured reagent. The inset of Fig. 4B additionally presents that our assay is linearly sensitive to the Ag+ target in the artificial samples in a concentration range of 1.9–62.5 nM. The detection limit for Ag+ was estimated to be ca. 0.91 nM (3σ). In comparison with some recently reported quantitative Ag+ assays with the fluorescence [4–6,10], absorbance [12,13,20,21], or electrochemistry [14,15,22] measurements, our new method is simpler and cheaper, allows limitedly-trained personnel (or even unskilled home-users) to achieve comparable or even better sensitivity, and especially offers a POCT capability for on-site uses (Table S1 in Supplementary Material).
The accuracy and applicability of the analytical device described herein was also assessed by conducting recovery tests using several real water samples, namely pond water collected from a pond on campus, tap water obtained from our lab, commercially available drinking water, and soil water collected from the vicinity of a factory. Before the analysis, all the water samples were filtrated with PNMs to remove potential interferences. All the tests were performed six times and the results were summarized in Table S2 (Supplementary Material). One can find from Table S2 that recovery results obtained are in a range of 95.2–103.9% and relative standard deviations range from 3.3 to 8.1% (n = 6). The high recovery percentages and small standard deviations in the experiments validate high accuracy and practicability of our devices for specific identification and quantification of Ag+ in real complex matrices such as environmental water samples.
Moreover, ambient temperature and sunlight are two important factors which have to be considered when performing in-field Ag+ detection. As displayed in Fig. S5 in Supplementary Material, quite similar red ink’s flowing time was obtained at tested ambient temperatures ranging from 4 to 50 ℃. Negligible differences were also observed in the results of assaying the same sets of Ag+ samples in sunlight and indoors (Fig. S6 in Supplementary Material). The ignorable influences of ambient temperature and sunlight on the performance of this new assay may be due to capillary action-driven rapid reactions in the hydrophilic paper body. It should be noted that each of reagent-loaded paper devices freshly prepared should be stored in a single seal bag and kept away from light. They could remain stable for at least one month without significant loss of analytical sensitivity. More satisfactory storage stability might be expected if using vacuum packaging technique to package these devices.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

We have developed a new type of 3D POCT device with the phone readout for instrument-free timing detection and quantification of sub-nanomolar Ag+ ions within several minutes. We attested that such rapid, enhanced Ag+-dependant timing responses may result from highly efficient target-triggered in situ autocatalysis amplification effect. That is, this effect allowed for the in situ production of lots of silver nanoparticles, which could, in turn, catalyze the oxidation of more TMB molecules to their derivatives by Ag+ ions. As a result, bigger (and more) metal particles can be formed to block nanopores of the PNM integrated into the device. Because of its simplicity, high sensitivity and selectivity, and sample-in-answer-out capability, our POCT devices should find wide field-based applications for water monitoring, particularly in various resource-poor environments.
