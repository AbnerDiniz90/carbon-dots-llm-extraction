### TITLE ###

Nitrogen and chlorine dual-doped carbon nanodots for determination of curcumin in food matrix via inner filter effect

### ABSTRACT ###

In this work, a novel simple, fast, selective and inexpensive fluorescence method for the determination of curcumin based on the fluorescence quenching of nitrogen and chlorine dual-doped carbon nanodots (N,Cl-CDs) was for the first time presented. The N,Cl-CDs were fastly and greenly produced by simply mixing glucose, 1,2-ethylenediamine (EDA) and hydrochloric acid (HCl). The fluorescence of N,Cl-CDs was significantly quenched by curcumin via a synergistic effect of dynamic quenching and inner filter effect (IFE). The N,Cl-CDs shows high selectivity and sensitivity towards curcumin sensing, achieving a linear range of 0.1–35 μM and a limit of detection (LOD) as low as 38 nM. The proposed fluorescence method was successfully utilized for curcumin detection in food samples with recoveries in a range of 96.8–106.1%. The findings of this study suggest the feasibility and usefulness of N,Cl-CDs as an effective fluorescence probe for the determination of curcumin in complex food matrix.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Curcumin, 1,7-bis-(4-hydroxy-3-methoxyphenyl)-1,6-heptadiene-2,5-dione (Fig. S1 and Supplementary data), is a natural polyphenolic pigment derived from powdered rhizome of the plant Curcuma longa (Zokhtareh & Rahimnejad, 2018). It is usually used as a common ingredient in cosmetics, herbal supplement, food coloring and food flavoring. The curcumin has attracted tremendous attention because of its biological and pharmacological activities such as antioxidant, anticancer, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial and antidiabetic effects (Shi et al., 2015). Previous studies have shown that the curcumin could serve as an efficient agent in the treatment of various diseases, including allergy, HIV, Alzheimer's, different types of cancers, cardiovascular and respiratory diseases (Afkhami, Pirdadeh-Beiranvand, & Madrakian, 2017). However, an overdose of curcumin was reported to have prooxidant activity on deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) (Ziyatdinova, Nizamova, & Budnikov, 2012) and can cause the decrease of intracellular ATP levels and trigger the necrosis process (Chan, Wu, & Chang, 2006). With this information, it can be seen that the anti- or prooxidant activity of curcumin depends on its concentration and therefore, the control of curcumin concentration in foodstuffs entering a human body is of utmost importance and it is highly necessary to develop an accurate detection method to measure curcumin.
The conventional analytical methods that have been developed for the determination of curcumin include voltammetry (Chaisiwamongkhol, Ngamchuea, Batchelor-McAuley, & Compton, 2017; Wray, Batchelor-McAuley, & Compton, 2012; Zokhtareh & Rahimnejad, 2018), high performance thin-layer chromatographic (HPTLC) (Ansari, Ahmad, Kohli, Ali, & Khar, 2005), high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) (Lee & Choung, 2011; Mahale et al., 2018; Rahimi, Hashemi, & Nazari, 2014; Tønnesen & Karlsen, 1983), ultra-high performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC) (Mohsin, Mohammad, Mshaan, Muhammad Delwar, & Fars, 2018), liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) (Kunati, Yang, William, & Xu, 2018), and spectrophotometry (Afkhami et al., 2017; Tang, Ma, Wang, & Zhang, 2002). These methods enjoy favorable accuracy, desirable selectivity and satisfactory sensitivity; however, most of these methods require complex sample pretreatment, complicated operation procedures, expensive instrumentation and long analysis time. Recently, the spectrofluorimetry methods (Baig & Chen, 2017; Bian et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2015), which display high accuracy, remarkable selectivity, significant sensitivity, great simplicity, high economy-effectiveness and fast analysis, have been reported and attracted increasing interests of the scientists.
The fluorescent carbon dots (CDs), with typical sizes below 10 nm, is one of the newest addition to the family of nanoscale carbons (Baker & Baker, 2010). The CDs have attracted considerable attention due to their superlative photoluminescence (PL) properties, such as excitation wavelength (λex)-dependent fluorescence, tunable emission color and high photostability (Bourlinos et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2013; Zhai et al., 2012). In view of these superior PL properties, the CDs have shown high potential for various applications, especially in the aspects of cell imaging and fluorescence sensors (Yi Liu et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 2013). To date, numerous efforts have been contributed to further enhance the PL performance of CDs and enlarge its application scope. Taking advantages of the easy functionalization feature of CDs, heteroatoms doping is considered as the most useful approach to tune the PL properties of CDs (Bian et al., 2018). Nitrogen (N)-doping is the most popular way to improve the fluorescence of CDs; doping with N could induce an upward shift in the Fermi level and electrons in the conduction band, resulting in substantial fluorescence enhancement of CDs (Q. Zhang et al., 2015). However, the N-doped CDs were reported to have low-stable aqueous decentrality and weak resistance to the interferences of metal ions, acid or base (Ding, Yu, Wei, & Xiong, 2016; Gong et al., 2016). To overcome these shortcomings, the other heteroatoms such as sulfur (S) (Zhang et al., 2018), boron (B), phosphorus (P) (Wang, Sun, Zhuo, Zhang, & Wang, 2014), fluorine (F) (Zuo et al., 2017) and chlorine (Cl) (Zhong et al., 2017) are usually further doped into N-doped CDs to improve the water dispersity and the anti-interference ability of N-CDs. So far, numerous reports have shown that the doped CDs are efficient in various sensing applications, especially in the aspect of metal ions detection (e.g. Cu2+ (Shao et al., 2013), Hg2+ (Xu et al., 2018), Pb2+ (Wang et al., 2015), Fe3+ (Zhong et al., 2017), Ag+ (Gao et al., 2015) and etc). However, rare reports have shown the capability of CDs in sensing curcumin with only few attempts have been made (Baig & Chen, 2017; Bian et al., 2018; Yang Liu et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2015). The CDs-based sensing mechanisms that have so far been reported for curcumin detection include two types, i.e., fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) and inner filter effect (IFE). The FRET sensing system involves the intermolecular interaction between a chemosensor and a target molecule, and it only works when a chemosensor and a target molecule are at a particular distance or have a complementary geometry, leading to the method being complicated and time-consuming while the IFE occurs if the absorption band of an absorber has overlap with the excitation and/or emission band of a fluorophore to some extent (Zheng et al., 2013). Compared to the FRET mechanism, the IFE provides an enhanced sensitivity and faster response since the changes in the absorbance of a fluorophore could transform exponentially into the change of the fluorescence intensity. For example, a sensing system based on the FRET between N-CDs and curcumin achieved a limit of detection (LOD) of 44.8 ng mL−1 (0.12 µM) for curcumin detection (Shi et al., 2015). However, the sensing mechanisms based on the fluorescence quenching of P- and N-codoped CDs (P,N-CDs) (Yang Liu et al., 2018) and B- and N-codoped CDs (B,N-CDs) (Bian et al., 2018) by curcumin via IFE could achieve much lower LOD at a nanomolar level of 58 nM and 65 nM, respectively. To this end, it is highly necessary to explore more efficient CDs-based IFE sensing system for curcumin detection.
In this work, a fluorescence method based on N- and Cl-codoped CDs (N,Cl-CDs) was for the first time presented for the determination of curcumin through a facile and green one-step preparation procedure (Scheme 1 ). The N,Cl-CDs were produced with glucose as carbon source, 1,2-ethylenediamine (EDA) as N dopant and hydrochloric acid (HCl) as Cl dopant via the acid-base neutralization heat. The absorption band of curcumin quite precisely overlaps with the excitation band of N,Cl-CDs and partially overlaps with the emission band of N,Cl-CDs. With this information, we proposed a N,Cl-CDs-based fluorescent nanosensor for the detection of curcumin via a synergistic effect dynamic quenching and IFE where the curcumin serves as a light absorber and the N,Cl-CDs act as a fluorophore. Our proposed fluorescence method show much higher sensitivity than most of the previously reported detection methods, such as voltammetry methods (Chaisiwamongkhol et al., 2017; Zokhtareh & Rahimnejad, 2018), spectrophotometry methods (Afkhami et al., 2017), spectrofluorimetry methods (Baig & Chen, 2017; Bian et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2015) and even some advanced modern instrumental methods, such as HPTLC (Ansari et al., 2005), UHPLC-UV-Vis (Mohsin et al., 2018) and HPLC-PDA (Lisiane, Tamara Ramos, & Sandra Elisa, 2018). The proposed fluorescence method was successfully used for the detection and quantification of curcumin in food samples. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report in utilizing eco-friendly N,Cl-CDs as a fluorescence probe to determine curcumin in a simple, fast, selective and inexpensive way. It is anticipated that the proposed fluorescence method could potentially be further explored and widely used for food quality monitoring.


### EXPERIMENTAL ###



### CHEMICALS AND REAGENTS ###

HCl and EDA were purchased from Aldrich (Milwaukee, WI, USA). Curcumin and all amino acids (aspartic acid, alanine, leucine, valine, tryptophan, phenylalanine, DL-cysteine, glycine, tyrosine, glutamate, threonine, arginine, methionine, histidine, serine and methionine) were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Geniposide, adenosine, guanosine hydrate (GH) were bought from Shanghai Yuanye Bio-Technology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Glutathione reduced (GSH), folic acid (FA) and l-ascorbic acid (AA) were purchased from Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The dialysis membrane (MWCO = 500–1000 Da) was purchased from Spectrum Laboratories (Rancho Dominguez, CA, USA). The other reagents used were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). The mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder were bought from the commercial supermarket at Yangzhou city, China. Unless otherwise stated, all the reagents used in this work were of analytical grade or above and were used as received without further purification. The ultrapure water obtained from a Milli-Q Plus water purification system (≥18.25 MΩ cm, Bedford, MA, USA) was used in all runs.


### SYNTHESIS OF N,CL-CDS ###

In a typical synthetic procedure, 0.4 g of glucose was transferred into a 50.0 mL glass beaker, followed by the addition of 4.0 mL of concentrated HCl and 6.0 mL of EDA. The reaction mixture became foamy within seconds under the acid-base neutralization spontaneous heat, implying the formation of CDs. The obtained reaction product was naturally cooled down to room temperature and was then dialyzed against 1L ultrapure water in a dialysis membrane tube (MWCO = 500–1000 Da, Spectrum Laboratories, Rancho Dominguez, CA, USA) with stirring over a course of 3 days. During the dialysis process, fresh ultrapure water was recharged at a time interval of 24 h. The resultant N,Cl-CDs solution was in dark brown color and was finally freeze-dried to get the dry N,Cl-CDs product.


### CHARACTERIZATION OF THE AS-SYNTHESIZED N,CL-CDS ###

The morphology and size of N,Cl-CDs were studied using a JEOL JEM-2100 transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Tokyo, Japan). The Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) measurements were carried out on a Bruker Tensor II FTIR spectrometer (Bremen, Germany). The elemental analysis was performed on an Elementar Analysensysteme vario EL cube organic elemental analyser (Hanau, Germany). The UV–vis spectra were acquired a UV-2550 absorption spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan). The PL spectra were obtained using a RF-5301PC fluorescence spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan). The fluorescence decay curves were acquired on a FLS-980 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Edinburgh Co., Ltd., England).


### PREPARATION OF THE WORKING SOLUTIONS ###

Unless otherwise stated, all the solutions used in this work were prepared in phosphate buffered saline (PBS) solution (10 mM, pH 4). Fourteen kinds of amino acids (aspartic acid, alanine, leucine, valine, tryptophan, phenylalanine, dl-cysteine, glycine, threonine, arginine, methionine, histidine, serine and methionine), geniposide, two kinds of carbohydrates (glucose and fructose), GSH, FA, AA, eighteen kinds of cations (K+, Na+, Li+, Pb2+, Ag+, Al3+, Ca2+, Mn2+, Mg2+, Fe3+, Co2+, Ba2+, Fe2+, Zn2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Ni2+ and Cd2+) at a concentration of 75 mM were prepared, respectively. Different concentrations of curcumin (0.3 mM, 3 mM and 75 mM) were prepared in ethanol. The solutions of another two amino acids (tyrosine and glutamate), adenosine and GH at a concentration of 75 mM were prepared in methanol.


### STUDY OF THE SELECTIVITY OF N,CL-CDS TOWARDS CURCUMIN DETECTION ###

To study the selectivity of N,Cl-CDs, 2, 4 and 20 µL of the stock solutions (75 mM) of curcumin, sixteen kinds of amino acids (aspartic acid, alanine, leucine, valine, tryptophan, phenylalanine, dl-cysteine, glycine, tyrosine, glutamate, threonine, arginine, methionine, histidine, serine and methionine), two kinds of carbohydrates (fructose and glucose), six kinds of drugs (adenosine, geniposide, GSH, FA, AA and GH) and eighteen kinds of cations (K+, Na+, Li+, Pb2+, Ag+, Al3+, Ca2+, Mn2+, Mg2+, Fe3+, Co2+, Ba2+, Fe2+, Zn2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Ni2+ and Cd2+) were mixed with 3.0 mL of the N,Cl-CDs solution (1.25 mg/mL), respectively. The PL intensities were recorded at a λex/emission wavelength (λex/λem) of 380/467 nm. 2 µL of ultrapure water was mixed with 3.0 mL of the N,Cl-CDs solution (1.25 mg/mL) and used as a control sample.


### LINEARITY MEASUREMENT OF N,CL-CDS-BASED FLUORESCENCE METHOD ###

To study the linearity of the N,Cl-CDs towards curcumin sensing, different volumes of 0.3 mM curcumin (1, 2, 2, 1, 2 and 2 µL) and 3.0 mM curcumin (1, 1, 1, 1, 5, 5, 5, 5, 5 and 5 µL) were step-wisely titrated into 3.0 mL N,Cl-CDs (1.25 mg/mL), respectively, making the added concentration of curcumin at 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.6, 0.8, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30 and 35 µM, respectively. The PL intensities were recorded at a λex/λem of 380/467 nm.


### REAL SAMPLE ANALYSIS BY N,CL-CDS-BASED FLUORESCENCE METHOD ###

0.1 g mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder were weighted and mixed with 1.0 mL ethanol, respectively. After vigorously vortexing for 1 min, the mixtures were centrifuged at 15,000 rpm for 15 min. The supernatants were collected and filtered through 0.45 µm, 13 mm id cellulose acetate syringe filters (Tianjin Jinteng Experiment Equipment Co., Ltd., Tianjin, China). The filtered sample extractions were appropriately diluted with ethanol to a concentration in the linear detection range. In detail, the filtered extraction of Mustard powder was directly analyzed without further dilution while the filtered extractions of ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder were diluted 3, 5 and 30 times, respectively. Then, 5 µL of the diluted extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder were mixed with 3.0 mL N,Cl-CDs (1.25 mg/mL), respectively. The PL intensities were recorded at a λex/λem of 380/467 nm.


### REAL SAMPLE ANALYSIS BY HPLC ###

An Agilent 1260 series HPLC coupled with a VWD detector was used for the analyses of curcumin contents in the food samples. The chromatograms were acquired by monitoring the VWD at a wavelength of 420 nm. The separation was carried out by using a chromatographic column (150 × 4.6 mm id stainless steel) packed with 5-µm C18 bonded silica with 80-Å pore size (Agilent, Santa Clara, California, USA). The mobile phase was composed of 75: 25 (v/v) acetonitrile (ACN) and 5% glacial acetic acid (HAc). The flow rate was set at 1.0 mL/min, the injection volume was 20 µL, and the column temperature was 30 ± 1 °C. Before injection, the extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, and red paprika powder were concentrated for 15, 6, and 5 times, respectively while the extraction of turmeric powder was diluted for 2 times in order to make the concentrations of the food samples extractions in the linear detection range. The food samples were filtered through 0.45 µm, 13 mm id cellulose acetate syringe filters (Tianjin Jinteng Experiment Equipment Co., Ltd., Tianjin, China) prior to injection.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### CHARACTERIZATION OF N,CL-CDS ###

Various optical characterization techniques were used to characterize the properties of N,Cl-CDs. The morphology and size of N,Cl-CDs were characterized using TEM. As shown by the TEM image (Fig. S2A and supplementary data), the N,Cl-CDs are of spherical structure and are uniformly dispersed on the TEM grid without any apparent aggregation. The particle size distribution histogram (Fig. S2B) was obtained by randomly counting 80 particles at different spots of the TEM image. It can be seen that the N,Cl-CDs have a size range of 1.2–4.4 nm with an average diameter of 2.97 ± 0.3 nm.
The structural property of N,Cl-CDs was determined by FTIR. The IR spectrum of N,Cl-CDs (curve a) is displayed in Fig. S3 (Supplementary data). The IR spectrum of glucose was acquired for comparison (curve b, Fig. S3). The N,Cl-CDs shows a sharp absorption peak at 3268 cm−1 and a broad absorption peak at 2985–3197 cm−1 which are attributing to the NH stretching vibration and the OH stretching vibration, respectively. The strong absorption peaks at 1620, 1550, 1462 and 1362 cm−1 are corresponding to the CO stretching vibration, NH bending, CH3 bending and the CO stretching vibration, respectively. The absorption peak at 845 cm−1 corresponding to the CCl stretching vibration is also observed. For glucose, the characteristic peaks attributing to OH, CH, CO and CO stretching are found. The IR spectra suggest that the N and Cl heteroatoms are incorporated into N,Cl-CDs. The doping of heteroatoms into N,Cl-CDs was further demonstrated by elemental analysis. As shown by Table S1 (Supplementary data), the N,Cl-CDs are mainly composed of 25.86% carbon (C), 9.29% hydrogen (H), 25.75% nitrogen (N), 22.17% chlorine (Cl) and 16.93% oxygen (O). In essence, the N,Cl-CDs are functionalized with amine, hydroxyl, carboxylic and alkyl chloride groups, suggesting the hydrophilic nature of N,Cl-CDs.
The optical properties of N,Cl-CDs were studied by UV–vis and PL spectroscopy and the results are displayed in Fig. 1 . As shown by Fig. 1A, the UV–vis spectrum of N,Cl-CDs (red line) shows obvious optical absorption in the UV region with a tail extending to the visible range. Two typical absorption peaks at 285 nm and 360 nm are found on the UV–vis spectrum of N,Cl-CDs with the former one is attributed to the n → π* transition of the CO bonding and the later one is likely attributed to the excited defect surface states induced by N and Cl heteroatoms (Gong et al., 2017). The excitation spectrum of N,Cl-CDs (green line) shows that the N,Cl-CDs have two excitation peaks located at 309 and 380 nm, and the maximum excitation peak is 380 nm. The N,Cl-CDs depicts an emission peak at 467 nm upon excitation at 380 nm (blue curve), exhibiting strong blue emission under the UV light (Inset of Fig. 1A). Fig. 1B displays the PL spectra of N,Cl-CDs under various excitation wavelengths. When the λex increases from 310 to 510 nm, the N,Cl-CDs display λex-dependent PL behaviour with the λem varing from 452 to 535 nm, which is believed to due to the presence of different particle sizes or various surface energy traps in N,Cl-CDs (Shang et al., 2012; Tang et al., 2012). The QY of the N,Cl-CDs is determined to be 12.15% using quinine sulfate as the reference (Fig. S4 and Supplementary data).


### OPTIMIZATION OF THE SENSING CONDITIONS ###

The effect of reaction time on the fluorescence intensity of N,Cl-CDs was firstly investigated. Fig. S5A (Supplementary data) depicts the change of the fluorescence intensity of N,Cl-CDs as a function of the reaction time after addition of 6 µM curcumin in PBS (pH 4). The ratio of the fluorescence intensity of N,Cl-CDs before the addition of curcumin to that after the addition of curcumin (F0/F) does not show obvious change after a reaction time of 1 min. As such, 1 min was selected as the optimal reaction time for the detection of curcumin.
The effect of pH on F0 and F0/F was investigated. Fig. S5B depicts the plots of F0 and F0/F as a function of pH. It can be seen that the F0 drops continuously with the increase of pH from 3 to 8 and remains nearly constant in high pH range (≥8). The F0/F fluctuates in the pH range of 3–8 with the maximum value obtained at pH 4 and remains nearly constant in high pH range (≥8). Therefore, 4.0 was chosen as the optimal pH for the detection of curcumin.
The effect of N,Cl-CDs concentration on F0 and F0/F was studied. Fig. S5C depicts the plots of F0 and F0/F as a function of the N,Cl-CDs concentration. It can be seen that the F0 decreases significantly when the concentration of N,Cl-CDs increases from 0.25 to 1.25 mg/mL, which afterwards increases sharply to the highest value when the concentration of N,Cl-CDs is increased to 1.75 mg/mL. Further increase of the concentration of N,Cl-CDs (1.75–2.75 mg/mL) causes the decrease of F0. In comparison to F0, the F0/F shows an opposite trend of change with the increase of the N,Cl-CDs concentration. The maximum value of F0/F is achieved when the concentration of N,Cl-CDs is at 1.25 mg/mL. As such, 1.25 mg/mL was chosen as the optimal concentration of N,Cl-CDs for the detection of curcumin.
Collectively, the optimal reaction time, pH and concentration of N,Cl-CDs are 1 min, pH 4 and 1.25 mg/mL, respectively, which were used for the subsequent analyses.


### DETECTION OF CURCUMIN ###

The possibility of using the N,Cl-CDs as a fluorescence nanosensor for curcumin detection was explored under the optimal experimental conditions. Fig. 2 displays the changes of the PL intensities of N,Cl-CDs in the presence of various concentrations of curcumin. It can be seen that when increased concentrations of curcumin (curves 1–16) are introduced, the PL intensity of N,Cl-CDs decreases gradually, indicating that the curcumin could effectively quench the fluorescence of N,Cl-CDs. The F0/F (ratio of the fluorescence intensity of N,Cl-CDs before the addition of curcumin to that after the addition of curcumin) is plotted as a function of the curcumin concentration (Inset at the top-right of Fig. 2). There is a good linear relationship between F0/F and the concentration of curcumin in the range of 0.1–35 μM. The fluorescence quenching of N,Cl-CDs by curcumin is fitted by the following Stern-Volmer (SV) Eq. (1): (1) F 0 / F = 1 + K SV C where [C] is the concentration of curcumin and SV means the quenching constant. The KSV is calculated to be 2.79 × 104 L/mol with a correlation coefficient R2 of 0.9978. The three times the standard deviation rule (2) was used to measure the LOD, (2) LOD = 3 σ / K SV where σ is the standard deviation of 12 replicate measurements of the blank N,Cl-CDs solution. The LOD of the proposed fluorescence method is calculated to be 38 nM.
A comparison between the results obtained by the proposed fluorescence method and those achieved by the previously reported method is displayed in Table 1 . The comparison shows that the fluorescence method proposed here achieved much higher sensitivity than the voltammetry methods (Chaisiwamongkhol et al., 2017; Zokhtareh & Rahimnejad, 2018). Compared to the previously reported spectrophotometry (Afkhami et al., 2017) and spectrofluorimetry methods (Baig & Chen, 2017; Bian et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2015), the proposed fluorescence method has a comparable linear range and exhibits much higher sensitivity. More importantly, it can be seen that the sensitivity of the proposed fluorescence method is even much higher than that of some advanced modern instrumental methods, such as HPTLC (Ansari et al., 2005), UHPLC-UV-Vis (Mohsin et al., 2018) and HPLC-PDA (Lisiane et al., 2018). Even though in some cases, the analytical performance of our proposed fluorescence method is not as superior as that of the LC-MS/MS method (Kunati et al., 2018), our proposed N,Cl-CDs-based fluorescence method provides advantages of simplicity, low cost and reduced analysis time in comparison with the LC-MS/MS method.


### SELECTIVITY OF THE FLUORESCENCE METHOD ###

To study the selectivity of the fluorescence method, the fluorescence quenching effect of curcumin and the possible interfering substances (various kinds of amino acids, carbohydrates, drugs and cations) were thoroughly investigated (Liu et al., 2018). In detail, the curcumin, sixteen kinds of amino acids (aspartic acid, alanine, leucine, valine, tryptophan, phenylalanine, dl-cysteine, glycine, tyrosine, glutamate, threonine, arginine, methionine, histidine, serine and methionine), two kinds of carbohydrates (fructose and glucose), six kinds of drugs (adenosine, geniposide, GSH, FA, AA and GH) and eighteen kinds of cations (K+, Na+, Li+, Pb2+, Ag+, Al3+, Ca2+, Mn2+, Mg2+, Fe3+, Co2+, Ba2+, Fe2+, Zn2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Ni2+ and Cd2+) were mixed with 3.0 mL N,Cl-CDs solution (1.25 mg/mL) at different concentration levels of 0.5, 1 and 5 mM, respectively, and the corresponding changes in fluorescence intensity of N,Cl-CDs caused by these possible interfering substances were recorded and are displayed in Fig. S6A, Fig. 3 , and Fig. S6B, respectively. It can be seen that only curcumin causes significant fluorescence quenching of N,Cl-CDs while the effects of the other possible interferences are almost negligible at the investigated concentration levels, indicating that N,Cl-CDs have high selectivity towards curcumin over the other possible interfering substances.


### MECHANISM DISCUSSION ###

The sensing mechanism for curcumin detection was studied by investigating the absorption spectrum of curcumin and the excitation and the emission spectra of N,Cl-CDs. As shown by Fig. 4 A, the curcumin displays a wide absorption peak (black curve) at around 290–560 nm. The excitation spectrum of N,Cl-CDs (blue curve) shows two peaks at 310 and 380 nm with the later one as the maximum excitation peak. When excited at 380 nm, the N,Cl-CDs displays an emission band at 467 nm (red curve). With this information, it can be seen that the absorption spectrum of curcumin quite precisely overlaps with the excitation band of N,Cl-CDs and partially overlaps with the emission band of N,Cl-CDs. These observations demonstrate that the fluorescence quenching mechanism is based on the fluorescence IFE. In the N,Cl-CDs-curcumin sensing system, the curcumin on one hand shields the excitation light for N,Cl-CDs and on the other hand absorbs the fluorescence emission from N,Cl-CDs.
The quenching mechanism can be divided into static quenching and dynamic quenching. The presence of a dynamic quencher reduces the fluorescence lifetime of a fluorophore while the static quencher process does not change the fluorescence lifetime of a fluorophore. Since the fluorescence lifetime is a crucial parameter for judging static and dynamic quenching (Bian et al., 2018), the effect of curcumin on the fluorescence lifetime of N,Cl-CDs was investigated. Fig. 4B displays the fluorescent decay curves of N,Cl-CDs before and after the introduction of curcumin. The corresponding data obtained by fitting the fluorescent decay with a bi-exponential function (3) are displayed in Table S2 (Supplementary data). (3) I ( t ) = A 1 exp - t / τ 1 + A 2 exp - t / τ 2 where τ1 and τ2 are the time constants of the two radiative decay channels; A1 and A2 are the corresponding amplitudes. In the absence of curcumin, the N,Cl-CDs has an average lifetime of 8.67 ns (χ2 = 1.153; the lifetimes of τ1 and τ2 are 4.43 ns (32.86%) and 10.75 ns (67.14%), respectively). In the presence of 6 µM curcumin, the N,Cl-CDs has an average lifetime of 7.81 ns (χ2 = 1.088; the lifetimes of τ1 and τ2 are 2.47 ns (16.27%) and 8.85 ns (83.73%), respectively). With this information, it can be seen that the introduction of curcumin causes the decrease of the fluorescence lifetime of N,Cl-CDs. This indicates that that fluorescence sensing belongs to dynamic quenching process. In essence, the fluorescence quenching mechanism between the interaction of N,Cl-CDs and curcumin is a synergistic effect of dynamic quenching and IFE.


### ANALYTICAL APPLICATION OF THE FLUORESCENCE METHOD ###

The proposed fluorescence method was applied to detect the concentration of curcumin in different kinds of food samples. In order to make the concentrations of the food sample extractions in the linear detection range, the filtered extractions of ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder were diluted 3, 5 and 30 times, respectively while the filtered extraction of Mustard powder was directly analyzed without further dilution. As shown by Table 2 , the curcumin contents in the diluted extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder are determined to be 2.25, 1.56, 1.55 and 2.32 µM, respectively. As such, the curcumin contents in the original extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder are 2.25, 4.68, 7.75 and 69.60 µM, respectively. To determinate the recoveries of the fluorescence method, different concentrations of curcumin were spiked into the food sample extractions. The quantitative spike recoveries for the determination of curcumin are 96.8–106.1% with the relative standard deviations (RSDs) < 5.61% (n = 5). The results indicate that the proposed fluorescence method could be used to quantify the curcumin contents in complex food samples with a high degree of simplicity and accuracy.
In order to check the accuracy of the proposed fluorescence method, a HPLC-UV method, which has been widely used for curcumin detection (Mohsin et al., 2018; Lisiane et al., 2018), was used to detect curcumin in the food samples. The typical chromatograms of the curcumin standard and the extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, red paprika powder and turmeric powder are depicted in Fig. S7A. The HPLC-UV method shows a linear range from 30 to 135 µM for curcumin detection (Fig. S7B). In order to make the concentrations of the food samples extractions in the linear range, the extractions of mustard powder, ginger powder, and red paprika powder were concentrated for 15, 6, and 5 times, respectively while the extraction of turmeric powder was diluted for 2 times before injection. The analysis results are displayed in Table S3. It can be seen that there is a good agreement between the results obtained by our proposed fluorescence method and that obtained by the HPLC-UV method, which further demonstrate the accuracy of our proposed fluorescence method.


### CONCLUSION ###

In summary, a novel simple, fast and inexpensive fluorescence method was developed for the determination of curcumin based on the fluorescence quenching of N,Cl-CDs prepared by simply mixing glucose, EDA and concentrated HCl. The proposed fluorescence method exhibits many advantages for the detection of curcumin, such as wide linear range (0.1–35 μM), high selectivity, significant sensitivity (LOD = 38 nM), great simplicity, high economy-effectiveness and fast analysis. The proposed fluorescence method was successfully applied to detect curcumin in food samples with satisfactory results were achieved; the recoveries vary from 96.8% to 106.1% with the RSDs < 5.61%. These findings clearly demonstrate that the fluorescence method possesses high potential for quantifying curcumin in complex food matrix. Our proposed fluorescence method is simple and eases of operation, indicating the great potential for food quality monitoring.
