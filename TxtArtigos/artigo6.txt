### TITLE ###

Dialysed caramel as an effective fluorophore for the simultaneous detection of three nitrophenols

### ABSTRACT ###

Low-cost and nontoxic fluorescent reagents are important for on-site analysis performed by non-professionals. In this work, a cheap and widely available food additive, caramel was used as an effective fluorescence reagent for the sensitive detection of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP), 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) and 4-nitrophenol (4-NP). Through a simple dialysis, fluorescent components of a commercial caramel were harvested with a yield as high as 60%. The structural characterization demonstrated that the fluorescent components were dehydrated oligomers of carbohydrates. Their fluorescence can be quenched by these nitrophenols. The quenching mechanism was speculated as inner filter effect. At pH 8, a linear range of 0.2–22 μM and a detection limit of 90 nM could be achieved for TNP. Based on the difference of their quenching efficiency at different pHs, TNP, DNP and 4-NP can be simultaneously determined by solving the linear equations obtained at pH 3, 5 and 8. Successful detection of these nitrophenols in the water and soil samples was performed with relative standard deviations of 1.1–5.9% and recoveries of 95–108% of spiked standards.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Fluorescent materials have been widely used for chem- and bio-sensing [1], bio-imaging [2], photoelectric conversion [3], photocatalysis [4], photodynamic therapy [5], whitening of paper and fabric [6], improving the vividness of color coatings [7] and enhancing the efficiency of sunlight utilization through incorporation of fluorescent materials in agricultural films [8]. Numerous fluorescent dyes and nanomaterials have been prepared to meet the requirements of various applications, which include small molecule organic dyes, organometallic complexes, polymer-based fluorescent materials, inorganic quantum dots (QDs), carbon dots and metallic nanocrystals. However, with the rapid increase of their applications, their safety has become a serious concern. Most of fluorescent materials are not easy to be degraded, their accumulation in environment may cause detrimental effects to the ecosystem, endangering human health through food chain [9]. Lots of traditional organic dyes possess conjugated structures (such as phenyl, naphthyl, oxazolyl, thiophene and other groups), may have potential carcinogenic risks. Inorganic QDs, such as CdTe, CdS and CdSe, may release heavy metal ions and cause acute toxicity to organisms [10]. Many ways have been proposed to eliminate or reduce the potential toxicity. Modification or coating on the toxic fluorescent materials with bio-compatible layers is a frequently adopted method. It is effective to coat semiconductor QDs with shells to prevent the leakage of the heavy metals and reduce their toxicity [11]. However, these operations are rather complicated, normally need multiple steps, significantly increase the cost for final products. Preparation of fluorescent materials with low toxicity and good performance is still a major task. Fluorescent carbon dots have been proven to be a good alternative, but their compositions are still unclear, their long term safety may need further investigation [12,13]. Based on this fact, exploring fluorescent substances from safe resources can be an alternative route for safer fluorescence application.
Caramel is an important dietary material produced and consumed widely for coloring and flavoring of food and beverage all over the world for a long time [14]. It is normally obtained through heating of sugars with or without additives [14,15]. As a commercialized product with a history of over 150 years, its safety as a food additive has undergone strict scrutiny. Studies on its potential effect on toxicokinetics, genotoxicity, subchronic toxicity, carcinogenicity and reproductive/developmental toxicity have been conducted [16], potential carcinogen impurities such as 4-methylimadazole (4-MeI) and 2-Acetyl-4-tetrahydroxy-butylimidazole (THI) are strictly regulated either in US or other countries [17]. Therefore, caramel can be a safe reagent if it can be used for sensing purpose. Although it was known that caramel is fluorescent, and quantitative analyses of caramel with fluorescence methods have been reported [18–20], application of caramel as fluorescent reagent is seldom found, possibly because of its complicated composition. Actually there are consistent efforts to unravel the composition of caramel [21,22], but as mentioned by Golon et al. [21], lack of suitable analytical techniques with sufficient resolution power for this complicated mixture is the main obstacle. According to Golon et al. [21], caramel contains oligomers with up to six carbohydrate units in forms of dehydration products, hydration products, disproportionation products and colored aromatic substances. There is large opportunity to have fluorescent components in this complicated cocktail.
Nitrophenols are listed as priority pollutants by the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) [23]. Among them, 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) and 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) are widely used for manufacturing of explosives, fireworks, matches and rocket fuels [24]. Nitrophenols such as 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), DNP and TNP also act as the raw materials in industries of leathers, dyes and medicines [25,26]. Due to their widespread applications, it is easy to be released into groundwater and soil, may cause stimulation to the skin and eyes and even respiratory damage of whom comes into contact occasionally [27,28]. Some researches show that TNP and its metabolites picramic acid are carcinogenic, teratogenic and mutagenic [29]. Due to its strong electron withdrawing ability, it is difficult to be degraded into non-toxic substances [30]. Therefore, the effective monitoring and detection of trace TNP, DNP and 4-NP in the environment are very important. So far, many methods have been used for the detection of nitrophenols, such as mass spectrometry [31], surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy [32], X-ray imaging [33], ELISA [34], but these techniques normally need expensive equipment, professional operation and tedious procedures. Electrochemistry can be used for the detection of such nitroaromatic explosives, but its selectivity and robustness may need further improvement [35]. Comparing with these methods, fluorescence spectroscopy has attracted intensive attention due to its advantages including high sensitivity, rapid detection speed and convenient operation. A group of functionalized fluorescent materials have been used for the detection of nitrophenols, which include organic dyes [36–38], metal nanoclusters [39], conjugated polymers [40], carbon nanomaterials [41] and quantum dots [42]. However, potential biotoxicity of these materials together with other difficulties in material preparation and stability in aqueous solution still implies the necessity of non-toxic and easily available materials for rapid analysis of nitrophenols.
Herein, a commercially available caramel was simply separated into two portions through dialysis. The dialysate with low molecular weight (low MW) exhibited bright blue fluorescence under irradiation of UV light and the retentate with high molecular weight (high MW) showed dark typical color of caramel but it is barely fluorescent. The dialysate was systematically characterized to deduce the possible origin of fluorescence. The fluorescent dialysate was successfully used for the rapid and sensitive detection of TNP, DNP and 4-NP. The ratios of nitrophenols can be estimated by controlling the pH value during the assay. The dialysed caramel was successfully used for water and soil samples, which demonstrates its potential as a low-cost, non-toxic fluorescent reagent.


### MATERIAL AND METHODS ###



### REAGENTS AND MATERIALS ###

Caramel was purchased from Apple Flavor & Fragrance Group Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China, complying with the standard GB 1886.64–2015). Dialysis membrane tubes with MW cutoff of 8000–14400 Da (Wordbio Co. Ltd., Xi’an, China) were used. Nitrobenzene (NB), 1-Chloro-4-nitrobenzene (ClNB), 1,3-dinitrobenzene (DNB), 1-Chloro-dinitrobenzene (ClDNB), methylbenzene (MB), 4-nitrotoluene (NT), 2,4-dinitrotoluene (DNT), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), benzoic acid (BA), 2-nitrobenzoic acid (NBA), 4-Chlorobenzoicacid (ClBA), 3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid (DNBA), aniline (AN), phenol (PHE), 2-aminophenol (AP), 2-nitrophenol (2-NP), 3-nitrophenol (3-NP), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), and 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) were purchased from Aladdin Reagent Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) was obtained from Xilong Chemical Co. Ltd. (Guangdong, China). All reagents were of analytical grade and used without further purification. Ultrapure water (18 MΩ cm from a Milli-Q water purification system (Millipore Corp., Bedford, MA)) was used in all experiments.
Warning! TNT and TNP, are highly explosive, should be handled carefully in small quantities with appropriate safety measures.


### INSTRUMENTATION AND CHARACTERIZATION ###

Elemental analysis was carried out using a vario EL cube Elemental Analyzer (elementar, Langenselbold, Germany). Fourier transform infrared spectrometry (FT-IR) was recorded on a Nicolet Nexus 670 spectrometer (Madison, WI) using KBr pellets. 1H Nuclear magnetic resonance (1H NMR) spectra were recorded on a JNM-ECS (JEOL, Tokyo, Japan, 400 MHz). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed on an FEI Tecnai G2 F30 transmission electron microscope (Eindhoven, The Netherlands) operating at 200 kV. Hydrodynamic diameters of the fluorescent dialysate were measured by a dynamic light scattering instrument (DLS, BI-200SM, Brookhaven Instruments Corp., Holtsville, New York). The X-ray diffraction pattern (XRD) was obtained on an X′Pert diffractometer (Philips, Almelo, The Netherlands). The fluorescence spectra were measured on an RF-5301 spectrofluorophotometer (Shimadzu, Tokyo, Japan) with both excitation and emission slits set at 10 nm and the UV–Vis absorption spectra were recorded on a UV 2800SPC spectrophotometer (Shanghai Sunny Hengping Scientific Instrument Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China). Fluorescence lifetime measurements were performed using a time-correlated single-photon counting (TCSPC) system combined with an FLS920 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Edinburgh Instruments, West Lothian, U.K.).


### DIALYSIS OF CARAMEL ###

To completely separate the fluorescent components, aqueous solution of caramel (100 mg/mL) of 5 mL was loaded into a dialysis tube (MW 8000–14400 Da), the tube was clamped and soaked in 2 L ultrapure water. The dialysate was collected after 24 h and fresh water (2 L) was replenished for another cycle of dialysis for 24 h. The process was repeated (3 times) until the dialysate was not fluorescent (checked with a 405 nm laser pointer). All dialysates were combined and concentrated using a rotary evaporator to a volume of 20 mL. Finally, the fluorescent components were freeze-dried for further use. To get dialysed caramel for the detection, 1 cycle of dialysis for 4–6 h was normally adopted.


### PRETREATMENT OF REAL SAMPLES ###

Water samples collected from the Yellow River and a eutrophic pond were filtered through 0.22 µm filters before analysis.
Three kinds of soil samples (loess, the Yellow River sediments and street dust) were collected from the campus of Lanzhou University, the bank of Yellow River and Donggang Road (Lanzhou, China), respectively. The soil samples were treated using a previously reported protocol with slight modification [43]. First, the soil was naturally dried and grinded with a mortar and pestle. Then 1.0 g of sample was extracted with 4.00 mL acetone through ultrasonication for 15 min and centrifuged for 10 min at 10,000 rpm. The supernatant was collected and dried under nitrogen. The residual was dissolved in 2.5 mL pure water for analysis. Each sample was measured under pH 3, 5 and 8.


### FLUORESCENCE DETECTION OF TNP, DNP AND 4-NP ###

Three aliquots of 2.5 mL nitrophenols (NPs) solution of various concentrations were added separately into 0.5 mL of dialysed caramel (420 μg/mL) in McIlvaine's buffer at pH 3, 5 and 8. The fluorescence intensities were measured at λex/λem of 350/434 nm, and ΔF (ΔF = F 0 − F) values were calculated, where F 0 is the fluorescence intensity of the dialysed caramel mixed with 2.5 mL pure water and F is that with NPs. The calibration curves were prepared by plotting F 0/F − 1 versus NP concentrations. All measurements were performed at least thrice at room temperature.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### FLUORESCENT CARAMEL ###



### DIALYSIS OF RAW CARAMEL ###

Caramel used in this work is produced by heating sugars in the presence of ammoniate. It is reasonable to have fluorescent components after a series of condensation, dehydration, Maillard reaction and carbonization [44]. However, commercially available caramel is weakly fluorescent, partially because of its dark color and self-quenching. To remove the dark color, dialysis was utilized to separate the components based on its size. It was observed that components of low MW are highly fluorescent, a dialysis membrane with a MW cutoff of 8000–14400 Da could well separate the components into a portion of fluorescent dialysate and a portion of non-fluorescent retentate ( Fig. 1). The dialysis was continued with repetitive water change every 24 h until no fluorescent components came out. All dialysates were combined and dried to get pale yellow powder, which is 60% (w/w) of original caramel. The other 40% possesses typical dark color of caramel but it is barely fluorescent. It should be mentioned that multiple dialysis steps with membranes of different MW cutoff were employed for the characterizing purpose, to be used as a fluorescent reagent, one step of dialysis is sufficient.


### OPTICAL PROPERTIES AND STRUCTURAL CHARACTERIZATION ###

The optical properties of the fluorescent dialysate were examined by UV–Vis and fluorescence spectrometry. UV–Vis spectrum shows a strong characteristic absorption peak at 270 nm ( Fig. 2a), corresponding to π-π* electron transition of the conjugated C˭C bonds and a broad absorption peak between 300 and 400 nm, which can be attributed to n-π* electron transition of the C˭O bonds [45]. As shown in the fluorescence spectra, the optimum excitation and emission wavelengths of the dialysate are 350 nm and 434 nm, respectively (Fig. 2a). Under the 365 nm UV lamp, bright blue fluorescence was observed. With quinine sulfate as the reference, the measured quantum yield of dialysed caramel was 6.5%. Fig. 2b is the emission spectra obtained at excitation from 310 to 400 nm range, it is obvious that emission is excitation wavelength dependent, which indicates the multiple emission mechanisms. The bathochromic-shift is clear in the normalized spectra (Fig. S1). Based on these phenomena we presumed that the fluorescence emission is produced by randomly distributed 3-dimensionally conjugated structure.
Elemental analysis results of the dialysed caramel (C, 47.4; H, 4.6; N, 3.6; O, 44.4%) represent a formula of C31H36N2O22, roughly corresponds to a loss of 1.8 molecule of H2O per hexose unit. Based on the O/C and H/C ratios, the fluorescent components still belong to dehydration products of sugar oligomers according to Golon et al. [21]. This speculation is also confirmed by the FTIR spectrum. The absorption peak at 3426 cm−1 indicates N-H and O-H stretching vibration, the absorption peak at 1630 cm−1 indicates the C˭O (in amide or carboxyl group) or C˭C stretching vibration, the absorption peak at 1409 cm−1 is caused by the deformation vibration of C-H, both 1202 and 1045 cm−1 point to C-O stretching vibration ( Fig. 3a), these peaks indicate the presence of hydroxyl, amino, carbonyl and ether functional groups.
The difference between of the fluorescent and dark colored portions was characterized by 1H NMR. Caramel (Fig. S2) and its fluorescent portion (Fig. 3b blue line) show the similar 1H NMR spectra, with peaks at 0.7–4.0 ppm (sp3 C-H protons and the protons attached with hydroxyl, ether and carbonyl groups), 4.0–5.4 ppm (HC˭CH protons), 6.2–8.7 ppm (aromatic protons) and 9.3 ppm (the protons associated with aldehyde group). By contrast, the 1H NMR spectrum of the dark colored portion (Fig. 3b black line) is mainly composed of several broad peaks in the corresponding positions and is absence of the protons from aldehyde group at 9.3 ppm, which reveals high degree of polymerization and crosslinking in the dark colored portion.
TEM indicates the presence of two kinds of nanoparticles with different sizes in the dialysed caramel ( Fig. 4a). The size of the larger ones ranges from 7.9 to 12.1 nm (Fig. S3a), while the smaller ones in a range of 1.6–4.4 nm (Fig. S3b). Meanwhile, average hydrodynamic diameter of the dialysed caramel is determined as 4.2 nm by DLS result (Fig. S4), which implies that the larger nanoparticles were probably formed by the aggregation of small nanoparticles during the process of drying [46]. These images are similar to the carbon dots reported previously, but we didn't refer to the dialysed components as carbon dots because it is found that dialysate obtained with a membrane of cutoff MW as small as 500 Da is still highly fluorescent. It is unreasonable to draw a conclusion that the fluorescence is originated from these nanoparticles, just as mentioned by Essner et al. [47]. On the other hand, since about 60% of caramel is fluorescent and the carbonized portion (colored components) is not fluorescent, caramel is apparently a more pertinent name based on the conventional nomenclature. XRD pattern (Fig. 4b) showed a broad peak at 23°, which indicates an amorphous structure, consistent with the random condensation and dehydration of carbohydrate units.


### STABILITY OF DIALYSED CARAMEL ###

Influence of external conditions on the fluorescence intensity of the dialysed caramel was also investigated. Fig. 5a shows the fluorescence intensities under different pH conditions, fluorescence emission is stable at pHs from 4 to 10. It decreased in strong acidic and basic medium, which demonstrates that protonation or deprotonation may change the structure and electron transition [48]. This behavior is consistent with the fact that there are amino, hydroxyl and carboxyl groups in the dialysed caramel as discussed above. Ionic strength showed no influence on the fluorescence intensity (Fig. S5), which demonstrates that electrostatic interaction plays a very limited role in the fluorescence emission. This is good for situations where high concentration of salts have to be used, for example, PBS buffers used in most of biological applications. The fluorescence was stable after continuous illumination for 1 h, which proves suitability for long time continuous analysis (Fig. 5b).


### SENSING OF TNP, DNP AND 4-NP WITH DIALYSED CARAMEL ###



### DETECTION MECHANISM ###

As shown in Fig. 6a, b and c, the fluorescence intensity of the dialysed caramel decreased evidently with the increase of the concentration of TNP, DNP or 4-NP, the fluorescence emission peak was also slightly red-shifted. To elucidate the possible mechanism, fluorescence spectra of the dialysed caramel and UV–Vis spectra of NPs were taken. As shown in Fig. 7a, the absorption band of NPs are overlapped with the excitation and emission bands of the dialysed caramel, suggesting the possibility of the inner filter effect (IFE) and fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) [49,50]. But the fluorescence lifetime of the dialysed caramel was basically unchanged after the addition of TNP, which eliminates the possibility of FRET during the quenching process (Fig. 7b). In addition, the attenuation of fluorescence of dialysed caramel is proportional to NPs' absorbance at 350 nm (the maximum excitation wavelength of dialysed caramel), rather than the overlapped area of the absorption band and the excitation /emission peaks. This is also an evidence of IFE [51].
NP exhibits strong absorption in the range of 300–460 nm at pH higher than its pKa, where it converts to phenoxide. The pKa of 4-NP, DNP and TNP is 7.1, 4.2 and 0.38, respectively. As shown in Fig. 8, fluorescence quenching efficiencies by NPs at different pHs are quite different, providing a possibility to resolve them through the measurements with different acidity [51].


### SIMULTANEOUS DETECTION OF THREE NPS ###

The linear relationships between concentration of NPs and F 0 /F − 1 at different pH values were described in Fig. 9. The content of each nitrophenol in a sample with mixed NPs can be calculated by solving the following equation obtained at three different pHs (pH 3, 5 and 8): (1) K TNP ⋅ C TNP + K DNP ⋅ C DNP + K 4 − NP ⋅ C 4 − NP = F 0 / F − 1 where C represents the concentration of corresponding NP, K is the slope of the calibration curve obtained at each pH. The equations with real K values are given in Supporting information.


### ANALYTICAL PERFORMANCE ###

The detection range and limit of detection (LOD) of NPs were summarized in Table S1, the relative standard deviations (RSDs) for the measurement of 5 µM NPs in the range of 0.9–3.6% (intra-day, n = 3) and 1.7–4.2% (inter-day, n = 3). The linear range of TNP is 0.2–22 μM with an LOD of 0.09 µM, which is superior to or comparable with those of fluorimetric methods reported recently using various materials (Table S2), but the advantage with regard to the cost of dialysed caramel is evident.
To evaluate its selectivity for NPs, metal ions may co-exist in the normal water samples, which include Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe3+, Fe2+, Ni2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Zn2+, Al3+, Cr3+, Ag+, Mn2+, Hg2+, Cu2+, Ba2+, Pb2+ and organic molecules with similar structures to NPs, including NB, DNB, ClNB, ClDNB, MB, NT, DNT, TNT, BA, NBA, ClBA, DNBA, AN, PHE, AP, 2-NP and 3-NP, were tested under the same condition. The fluorescent components show very good selectivity, as shown in Fig. 10a, only Fe3+ showed slight interference on the detection of NPs, because of its electrophilic nature and its color, but it can be readily eliminated by addition of reducing reagent, ascorbic acid (AA). AA itself does not interfere the analysis, the presence of 1 mM AA causes virtually no change in the fluorescence intensity of dialysed caramel. The low quenching efficiencies toward 2-NP and 3-NPs are due to their much lower extinction coefficients than 4-NP [51]. The selectivity of dialysed components for NPs among other nitroaromatic substances is surprisingly good (Fig. 10b), which may provide a choice to quantitatively detect NPs among other nitro compounds.


### DETECTION OF NPS IN REAL SAMPLES ###

The Stern-Volmer equations for the mixed NPs at pH 3, 5 and 8 listed in the Supporting information were used to determine each NP in the real samples. As shown in Table 1, none of NPs was found in the Yellow River water, eutrophic pond water, loess, Yellow River sediments or street dust, which is reasonable due to the strict control of pollution. Mixed NPs were spiked at different concentrations, each of NP was reliably detected with recoveries in a range of 95–108%. RSD (n = 3) are less than 5.9%. These results indicate that dialysed caramel can be used in real analysis. The lower limits of quantitation of the proposed method (Table S1) is comparable to the method of EPA for the determination of nitrophenols in water and soil/sediment samples by GC/MS (50 µg/L and 3300 µg/kg for 4-NP/DNP) [52]. The sensitivity can be further improved through extraction or other preconcentration techniques. It should be mentioned that a sample pretreatment including extraction of the NPs with acetone, removal of organic solvent and re-dissolving the analytes with water, is needed for soil samples, which makes the method a little complicated. Further optimization of the process can be a remedy of this flaw.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

It is proven that caramel can be an efficient and low-cost fluorescent reagent for the simultaneous detection of TNP, DNP and 4-NP. Through a simple dialysis, about 60% of highly fluorescent components can be isolated from commercial caramel products. Due to the strict regulation of the hazardous impurities of caramel, the isolated components should be safe and environment compatible. The fluorescent components belong to dehydration products of carbohydrate oligomers and the dark colored components show high degree of polymerization and crosslinking. The fluorescent components were successfully used for resolving TNP, DNP and 4-NP by adjusting the pH values and the sensing mechanism is most probably IFE. Due to the wide availability of UV LED of 365 nm, this actually provides a way for the detection of NPs on-site without the concern of possible threaten from the fluorescence reagents. Their application in biological sensing is also expected.
