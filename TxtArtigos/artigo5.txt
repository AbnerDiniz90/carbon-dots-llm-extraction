### ABSTRACT ###

An ultrasensitive photoelectrochemical (PEC) aptasensor was designed for detection of chloramphenicol (CAP) based on graphene quantum dots-sensitized TiO2 nanotube arrays (GQDs/TiO2 NTs). The GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanohybrids were prepared by a coupling technique of linker molecule binding and electrophoretic deposition. It exhibited significantly enhanced visible-light photoelectrochemical activity, which was firstly employed as the photoactive material for fabrication of PEC aptasensor. As the recognition unit, the aptamers of CAP were immobilized on GQDs/TiO2 NTs photoelectrode via π–π stacking interaction between GQDs and the nucleobases of the aptamer. In this signal-on proposal, the aptasensor was used for the label-free analysis of CAP by monitoring the increase in photocurrent that resulted from the formation of aptamer-CAP bioaffinity complexes with ascorbic acid as an efficient electron donor for scavenging photogenerated holes. Under the optimized conditions, the aptasensor showed a wide linear range from 0.5 nM to 100 nM for CAP detection with a low detection limit of 57.9 pM (S/N = 3). With good selectivity and sensitivity, the PEC aptasensor was applied to the determination of CAP in spiked honey samples with satisfactory results, suggesting that the GQDs/TiO2 NTs photoelectrode has a promising application in constructing PEC sensor platform.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Chloramphenicol (CAP) is a broad-spectrum antibiotic that is effective against infections caused by a wide variety of bacteria. Although CAP is clinically important, it has some serious harmful effects. For instance, the overdose of CAP intake from food or medicinal can result in bone marrow suppression, kidney damage and leukemia [1,2]. However, low cost and efficiency against infections made it become widely used antibiotics in veterinary medicine, which had led to its accumulation in animal products, such as milk, meat and honey. The United States Food and Drug Administration has set the minimum required performance limit of CAP as 0.3 μg kg−1 for the detection of its residues in food products [3]. Therefore, highly sensitive and selective determination of CAP in trace quantities is very important in food and pharmaceutical analysis. Recently, many analytical methods were reported for the detection of CAP, e.g. HPLC [4], GC-MS [5], capillary electrophoresis [6] and electrochemical technique [7]. Despite some advances in this field, it is still highly desired to develop new protocols to improve sensitivity and feasibility for determination of antibiotic residues in food samples.
Due to its excellent features of high sensitivity and good selectivity, photoelectrochemical (PEC) aptasensor has attracted a great deal of attention in the analytical chemistry field [8]. Because the separation of excitation source (light) and detection signal (current) can reduce the background signals, PEC detection shows higher sensitivity compared with conventional electrochemical methods. However, the PEC method is poor in selectivity since its super high oxidation power [9]. As biological recognition element, aptamers can selectively bind to a wide range of targets including small molecules to cells. Compared with antibodies, aptamers exhibit unprecedented advantages, such as easy chemical medication, low cost, synthesis convenience, small size and stability in heat. In addition, aptamers can bind to their targets with high affinity and specificity [10]. Because of their unique characteristics, aptamers usually are used as capturing agent to replace antibodies recognition receptor. Therefore, highly sensitive PEC aptasensors for specific detection of various analytes such as microcystin-LR [11], 17β-estradiol [12], thrombin [13] and SK-BR-3 cancer cells [14] were successfully constructed in recent years. However, only a few reports appeared for the determination of CAP by PEC aptasensor approaches. Zhang's group developed a PCE aptasensor based on nitrogen-doped graphene quantum dots for chloramphenicol determination with a detection limit of 3.1 nM [15]. In this paper, we employed graphene quantum dots-sensitized TiO2 nanotube arrays (GQDs/TiO2 NTs) as visible light-responsive materials to develop a novel signal-on PEC aptasensor for highly sensitive determination of CAP with a detection limit of 57.9 pM.
PEC active species immobilized on the substrate electrode are the key factor for sensitivity of PEC aptasensor. Among various photoactive materials, vertically aligned titanium dioxide nanotube arrays (TiO2 NTs) have gained considerable attention because they can offer a large interior and outer surface area and provide a direct pathway for the transfer of photoexcited electrons, which causes the enhancement of light absorption and carrier separation [16]. Nevertheless, the PEC efficiency of the TiO2 NTs utilizing sunlight is relatively low because of the limited optical response to ultraviolet light and the fast recombination of photogenerated electron–hole pairs. Therefore, extending the optical absorption to the visible light region and suppressing the recombination of photocarriers are the two key ways to improve the PEC activity of the TiO2 NTs. As a rising star in the graphene family, Zero-dimensional graphene quantum dots (GQDs) consist of single- or few-layer graphene with a size of only several nanometers. As a new type of quantum dots, GQDs have unique properties combining the graphene nature and size-resulted quantum effects [17,18]. Recently, TiO2 NTs sensitized with GQDs (GQDs/TiO2 NTs) have been prepared by electrophoretic deposition (EPD) or the impregnation–precipitation (IP) method, which exhibits significantly higher PEC activity in visible light region than pristine TiO2 NTs because the recombination of photocarriers can be effectively suppressed and the visible light can be fully used [19,20]. However, direct deposition methods such as EPD or IP easily lead to clogging of the pores before the insides of the TiO2 NTs can be uniformly decorated with GQDs, thus preventing full utilization of the internal area of the TiO2 NTs towards forming intimate GQDs/TiO2 NTs heterogeneous junctions. In this work, coaxial heterogeneous graphene quantum dot-sensitized TiO2 nanotube arrays are prepared by a coupling technique of linker molecule binding and electrophoretic deposition. The silane linker molecules act as a superb medium for integrating GQDs and TiO2 NTs by covalent amide linkage, thus preventing GQDs from clogging the tube entrances and forming a uniform GQD layer tightly attached to the inside tube walls during the following EPD process. Compared to the pristine TiO2 NTs and GQDs/TiO2 NTs prepared by the conventional EPD or IP method, the fabricated hybrids exhibit significantly enhanced PEC activity due to their broad photo-absorption range, fast separation of photogenerated charge and stability.
In this paper, we reported for the first time on a visible-light driven PEC aptasensor based on coaxial heterogeneous GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanocomposites. As shown in Scheme 1, aptamers of CAP were immobilized on GQDs/TiO2 NTs photoelectrode via π–π stacking interaction between GQDs and the nucleobases of the aptamer. The aptasensor was used for label-free analysis of CAP by monitoring the increase in photocurrent that resulted from complete dissociation of the aptamers from the surface of electrode due to the specific recognition reaction between CAP and the aptamer. The experiment results indicated that the signal-on PEC aptasensor exhibited high sensitivity and good selectivity, which opens up a new avenue for quantitation of CAP at low concentrations in food samples.


### EXPERIMENTAL SECTION ###



### REAGENTS ###

Graphene quantum dots (GQDs, 6–9 nm diameter, 0.8–1.2 nm thickness) were obtained from Nanjing Xian Feng Nano Material Technology Co., Ltd. (Nanjing, China). 3-amino-propyl-triethoxysilane (APTES, 98%) was purchased from Aladdin Reagent Database Inc. (Shanghai, China). Hydrofluoric acid (HF, 40%), acetone, ethanol absolute, bovine serum albumin (BSA), ascorbic acid (AA), chloramphenicol, oxytetracycline, ciprofloxacin, florfenicol, kanamycin and gentamicin sulfate were received from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All of the other chemicals were of analytical reagent grade and used without further purification. The aptamer was purchased from Sangon Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China) with base sequences as follows: 5′-ACT TCA GTG AGT TGT CCC ACG GTC GGC GAG TCG GTG GTA G-3′. The stock solution of oligonucleotides was prepared in 10 mM Tris-EDTA buffer solution (TE, pH 8.0). CAP and BSA were dissolved in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (PB, pH 7.0) which was prepared by mixing the stock solution of 0.1 M KH2PO4 and 0.1 M Na2HPO4. All aqueous solutions were prepared with doubly distilled water.


### APPARATUS ###

The surface morphology was characterized by field emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM, S-4800, Hitachi, Japan). The UV–visible diffuse reflectance spectra were obtained on a UV-2600 UV–visible spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were performed with D8 advance X-ray diffractometer (Bruker AXS, Germany). Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrum was obtained with an Alpha FT-IR spectrometer (Bruker, Germany). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed on a CHI660D electrochemical workstation (Chenhua Instrument Shanghai Co., Ltd., China). PEC measurements were carried out with a homemade PEC system. A 350 W xenon lamp with an ultraviolet filter was used as the light source to provide visible light (λ > 400 nm). Photocurrent was recorded on a LK3200A electrochemical workstation (LANLIKE, Tianjin, China).


### PREPARATION OF GQDS/TIO2 NTS ELECTRODE ###

Titanium foils (99.8% purity, 0.5 mm thick) were cut into 0.5 × 5.0 cm2 pieces and then sonicated in acetone, ethanol and distilled water for 20 min, consecutively. After that, the Ti slices dried under the nitrogen stream for future use. The TiO2 NTs were prepared by an anodization method similar to previous reports [21]. Briefly, a two-electrode electrochemical cell, with a working electrode of Ti slice and a counter electrode of platinum sheet, was used for the preparation of TiO2 NTs. Then the Ti slice was anodized in 0.5 wt% HF electrolyte under 20 V for 20 min. Finally, the as-anodized TiO2 NTs were annealed at 450 °C for 30 min.
A coupling technique of linker molecule binding and electrophoretic deposition was used to prepare GQDs/TiO2 NTs electrode according to the previous report with a slight modification [22]. The as-prepared TiO2 NTs were firstly immersed in 5.0 wt% APTES in 95% ethanol solution for 3 h under magnetic stirring condition at room temperature. Then a two-electrode cell, with the working electrode of APTES-modified TiO2 NTs, the counter electrode of platinum sheet, and an aqueous electrolyte of 0.1 mg mL−1 GQDs (pH = 9), was employed to integrate the GQDs with the modified TiO2 NTs by electrophoretic deposition at 4 V for 5 min. Then, a GQDs functionalized TiO2 NTs (denoted as GQDs/TiO2 NTs) electrode was gained.


### FABRICATION OF PEC APTASENSORS ###

The aptamer was immobilized onto the GQDs/TiO2 NTs electrode via the π-π stacking interaction between GQDs and the aptamer. 20 μL of 2.0 μM aptamer was dropped onto the electrode and it was incubated at room temperature overnight, and then the electrode was rinsed with Tris-EDTA buffer to remove unbound aptamer. The obtained electrode was noted as aptamer/GQDs/TiO2 NTs. Subsequently, 1% BSA was employed as the blocking buffer solution to block nonspecific binging sites. Then the as-prepared BSA/aptamer/GQDs/TiO2 NTs electrode was incubated in various concentrations of CAP for 60 min to allow the reaction between aptamer and CAP. Finally, the electrode was rinsed with Tris-EDTA buffer and then applied in PEC detection (Scheme 1).


### PHOTOELECTROCHEMICAL DETECTION ###

After the incubation of CAP, PEC measurement was performed immediately. The PEC experiments were carried out using a conventional three electrodes system including a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) as reference electrode, a platinum sheet electrode as the counter electrode and GQDs/TiO2 NTs electrode with a geometrical area of 0.25 cm2 used as working electrode. A 350 W xenon lamp with a 400 nm UV-cut filter was used as the light source to provide visible light. The distance between the light source and the working electrode was fixed at 20 cm. Photoelectrochemical detection was carried out in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.0) containing 0.01 M AA which served as a sacrificial electron donor. Photocurrent was measured by the current-time curve experimental technique on an electrochemical workstation at an applied potential of 0 V under visible light source irradiation. Both light duration and no light duration were 30 s. All experiments were operated at room temperature.


### PREPARATION OF THE HONEY SAMPLES ###

The honey was obtained from the supermarket as chloramphenicol-free sample. The 2 mL of honey sample was blended with 2 mL of distilled water for 10 min. Then the chloramphenicol-free honey samples were spiked with appropriate amounts of CAP. A 7 mL of ethyl acetate was added and the spiked samples were mixed completely. After ultrasonic extracted by the ultrasonic shaking for 15 min, they were centrifuged for 15 min at 4000 rpm and the supernatant was transferred to another tube. To enhance the extract efficiency, the extracting step for CAP was repeated three times. The collected supernatant was dried under gentle nitrogen flow at 40 °C. Then the final precipitate was dissolved in 10 mL of phosphate buffer (0.1 M, pH 7.0). The obtained sample solution containing spiked CAP was stored in 4 °C for future use.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### MORPHOLOGICAL AND SPECTROSCOPIC CHARACTERIZATION OF GQDS/TIO2NTS NANOCOMPOSITES ###

Fig. 1A-C presents SEM images of TiO2 NTs and GQDs/TiO2 NTs. The top view SEM image of TiO2 NTs in Fig. 1A shows highly ordered and vertically-aligned nanotubes microstructure. The inner diameter of TiO2 NTs is about 90–100 nm and the wall thickness is ca. 10 nm with regularly round-shaped and smooth open structure.
The TiO2 NTs that open at the top and close at the bottom are perpendicular to the substrate with a length about 350 nm (Fig. 1B). Fig. 1C shows the coaxial heterogeneous structured GQDs/TiO2 NTs with an inner diameter of 70–80 nm, which means that GQDs were embedded into the TiO2 NTs by electrophoretic deposition. The GQD coating layer with the thickness of about 20 nm uniformly decorated the inside tube walls of TiO2 NTs.
X-Ray diffraction (XRD) measurements were employed to investigate the phase and structure of as-prepared materials. As shown in Fig. 1D, the XRD pattern of the GQDs (curve a) showed a sharp peak at 14.8°, corresponding to the (002) reflection of graphene oxide [23]. The XRD pattern of pure TiO2 NTs (curve b) exhibited the characteristic diffraction peaks of the anatase phase (JCPDF 89-4921). The diffraction peaks at 25.2°, 38.9°, 47.9°, 53.9° and 62.5° can be indexed to the reflection from planes (101), (112), (200), (105) and (213) of anatase TiO2 [24]. As compared with curve b, the XRD spectra of GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanocomposites (curve c) maintained the characteristic peaks of pure TiO2 NTs and exhibited one additional diffraction peak (14.8°) which was attributed to GQDs in curve a. This well illustrates the successful fabrication of GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanohybrids.
The FT-IR spectrum of the TiO2 NTs, GQDs and GQDs/TiO2 NTs were displayed in Fig. 1E. As observed in curve a, the absorption peak at 600–1000 cm−1 were the infrared characteristic absorption peak of TiO2, which was attributed to a combination of Ti–O–Ti vibration in pristine TiO2 NTs [25]. For GQDs, the absorption peaks at 1750 cm−1 (C=O), 1620 cm−1 (C=C), 1415 cm−1 (C–OH) and 3495 cm−1 (–OH) can be observed, which indicated that GQDs were loaded with plentiful carboxyl groups and other oxygen-containing functional groups such as hydroxyl, carbonyl groups [26]. In the FT-IR spectrum of GQDs/TiO2 NTs (curve c), it can be seen that both the infrared characteristic absorption peak of GQDs and TiO2 NTs appeared. The corresponding appearance of peaks at about 1550 and 1260 cm−1 assigned to the N–H bond stretching vibrations of the amine groups in APTES and amide C–N stretch, respectively [22]. These results clearly indicate that GQDs are covalently bonded to the APTES molecules by the amide linkage and attached to the TiO2 NTs to form GQDs/TiO2 NTs heterojunction structure.
The UV–vis diffuse reflectance spectra (DRS) of the prepared GQDs, TiO2 NTs and GQDs/TiO2 NTs are shown in the Supporting information Fig. S1. As can be seen in curve a, pure TiO2 NTs displayed a strong light absorption in the ultraviolet region with an absorption band lower than 390 nm because of the large energy edge of TiO2 at 3.2 eV [27]. The DRS of GQDs showed that its absorption peak edge shifted to 450 nm, showing enhanced absorption in the visible light range (curve b). With the modification of GQDs, an obvious red shift of the absorption edge to 480 nm and stronger absorption in the visible range are observed for the GQDs/TiO2 NTs in comparison with the pristine TiO2 NTs (curve c). These results could prove that the addition of GQDs improved the absorbency of TiO2 NTs in the visible light range, which enhanced PEC performance of the GQDs/TiO2 NTs hybrid in the visible region.


### PHOTOELECTROCHEMICAL PROPERTIES OF GQDS/TIO2 NTS NANOCOMPOSITES ###

To investigate the photoelectrochemical properties of GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanocomposites, the photocurrent responses of different modified electrodes under the visible light illumination were measured in 0.1 M PB ( pH 7.0) and shown in Fig. 2A, Under the visible light irradiation, Ti substrate had no photocurrent response (curve a). The pure TiO2 NTs exhibited a low photocurrent response of 0.4 µA (curve b), which ascribed to its limited visible light absorption, and in particular, the high recombination rate of photogenerated charges. In great contrast, the photocurrent of GQDs/TiO2 NTs increased to 0.9 µA (curve c), which was about 2.25 times compared with that of the TiO2 NTs. The enhancement of photocurrent response is clearly attributed to the decoration of GQDs to the TiO2 NTs, which can increase visible light absorption to generate photocurrent and induce more sensitizer GQDs to generate photoexcited electron–hole pair. Furthermore, GQDs has unexpectedly excellent conductivity due to its two-dimensional planar structure, leading to a rapid transport of charge and an effective charge separation. Moreover, firm and uniform bonding between GQDs and TiO2 NTs can further promote the charge separation and allow a fast and efficient transfer of the photogenerated electrons from GQDs to the collected TiO2 substrate, which can effectively suppress the recombination of electrons and holes, resulting in enhanced photo-to-current conversion efficiency and improved PEC performance of GQDs/TiO2 NTs hybrids. As seen in curve d, an obvious ascent of photocurrent intensity of the GQDs/TiO2 NTs photoelectrode was observed as high as 1.2 µA when the photoelectrochemical detection was carried out in 0.1 M PB (pH 7.0) containing 0.01 M AA. The reason may be ascribed that AA served as a sacrificial electron donor can suppress photoexcited electron–hole recombination, thus resulting in a further increasing of photocurrent.
The charge transfer process of GQDs/TiO2NTs modified electrode under visible-light irradiation was depicted in Fig. 2B. Under visible light illumination, the electrons on the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the GQDs are firstly excited to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of GQDs. Then the excited electrons stored on the LUMO of GQDs quickly flow to the conduction band (CB) of TiO2 NTs because of the CB energy levels of TiO2 NTs being lower than the LUMO of GQDs. Meanwhile, the separated holes of TiO2 NTs can enter the HOMO of GQDs and then transfer to the interface between the GQDs/TiO2 NTs photoanode and the aqueous electrolyte, which are able to oxidize AA in phosphate buffer. Consequently, the holes were considerably reduced while electrons greatly enhanced. Thus, the increased electron density at the electrode could result in higher photocurrent generated in the presence of AA. The above phenomenon indicated that the construction of GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanocomposites can boost the charge transfer and improve separation efficiency of photogenerated charges, resulting in a dramatically enhanced PEC performance.


### PEC CHARACTERIZATION OF THE PHOTOELECTROCHEMICAL APTASENSOR ###

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was used as an effective tool to monitor the fabrication process of the aptasensor. The measurements were carried out in 0.1 M KCl solution containing 5.0 mM K3Fe(CN)6/K4Fe(CN)6. Fig. 3A showed the Nyquist plots of the electrodes with different modification process using [Fe(CN)6]3-/4- as redox probe, in which the diameter of the high frequency semicircle corresponds to electron transfer resistance (R et ) and the linear portion at lower frequency region corresponds to diffusion limited process [28]. An equivalent circuit was applied to model the impedance data for the present system and shown in the inset of Fig. 3A, which included the solution resistance (R s ), the constant phase element (CPE), the electron transfer resistance (R et ) and the Warburg impedance (Zw). The values for the parameters were obtained by fitting the experimental data to the equivalent circuit using Zview impedance software. Compared with pristine TiO2 NTs electrode with a fitting R et of 720 Ω (curve a), the resistance R et obviously decreased to 515 Ω after modified with GQDs (curve b), indicating that GQDs were successfully anchored on the TiO2NTs electrode and accelerated electron transfer of the redox-probe due to the good electrical conductivity of GQDs. However, the R et value drastically increased to 1168 Ω after aptamer was immobilized on the GQDs/TiO2NTs electrode (curve c). This increase in resistance is due to the electrostatic repulsion between the negatively charged phosphate backbone of the aptamer and negatively charged redox species of [Fe(CN)6]3-/4- [29]. The results confirmed that aptamers of CAP were effectively immobilized on GQDs/TiO2NTs photoelectrode via π–π stacking interaction between GQDs and the nucleobases of the aptamer [30]. Subsequently, the treatment with BSA resulted in significant increase in R et (1663 Ω) because the low conductive proteins hindered interfacial electron transfer (curve d). Moreover, it can be found that the semicircle was depressed and the low frequency straight line was not at 45°, which means that the surface of BSA/aptamer/GQDs/TiO2 NTs electrode was not uniform due to adsorption of proteins. Finally, after being incubated with CAP, the R et value of the electrode significantly reduced to 1356 Ω (curve e). This appearance might be aroused by the analyte-induced conformational change of the aptamer [31]. The forming of aptamer-CAP bioaffinity complexes can induce conformational change of the aptamer, which decreases the weak binding between aptamer and GODs surface resulting in complete dissociation of some aptamer-CAP complexes from the GODs surface after rinsing with buffer solution [31]. So the reduction in amount of aptamers adsorbed on the GODs surface could lead to significantly decreased R et . The above EIS results indicated that the PEC aptasensor had been successfully fabricated, which can be applied for the determination of CAP.
The fabrication process the aptasensor was also monitored by PEC method. Photocurrent responses of various modified electrodes were recorded in pH 7.0 PB containing 0.01 M AA at an applied potential of 0 V. As shown in Fig. 3B, the pure TiO2 NTs electrode (curve a) exhibited a weak photocurrent due to its poor visible-light responses. After modified GQDs on the TiO2 NTs, the photocurrent of GQDs/TiO2NTs electrode obviously increased due to the enhanced photo-to-current conversion efficiency of as-prepared nanocomposites under visible light irradiation (curve b). After that, the photocurrent gradually decreased with the aptamer anchoring and BSA blocking (curve c-d). This phenomenon could be attributed to the increased steric hindrance effect of the modified oligonucleotides and proteins molecules which suppressed the interfacial electron transfer and partly obstructed electron donor to the surface reaction with the photogenerated holes. While in the presence of CAP, aptamer specifically bound with CAP which decreased the amount of aptamers adsorbed on the electrode surface, resulting in an obviously enhanced photocurrent response due to the increasing of electron transfer between AA and electrode (curve e). The above photocurrent change tendency was well in accordance with that of EIS spectrum as expected, further indicating successful fabrication of the sensing interface.


### OPTIMIZATION OF EXPERIMENTAL CONDITIONS FOR PEC DETECTION ###

In order to achieve a better PEC response and high detection sensitivity, several experimental parameters were optimized, including electrophoretic deposition time of GQDs, AA concentration, aptamer concentration and binding time of CAP. Respective data and Figures are given in the Supporting information. The following experimental conditions were found to give best results: (a) electrophoretic deposition time: 5 min; (b) AA concentration: 0.01 M; (c) Aptamer concentration: 2.0 μM; (d) Binding time of CAP: 60 min at room temperature.


### QUANTITATIVE DETERMINATION OF CAP ###

In this work, an effective PEC aptasensor for CAP detection was fabricated by monitoring the variation of photocurrent signal. Fig. 4A showed the photocurrent intensity of the aptasensor in the presence of different concentrations of CAP under the optimized experimental conditions. It can be observed that the photocurrent intensity increased gradually with increasing the CAP concentrations. The quantitative behavior of the aptasensor was assessed by measuring the dependence of the increased photocurrent before and after incubated with different concentrations of CAP. As expected, the ΔI (ΔI = I−I0, where I0 and I are aptasensor without and with incubating with CAP, respectively) increased with the increasing concentration of CAP. The ΔI was proportional to the logarithm of the CAP concentration in the range of 0.5–100 nM (Fig. 4B). The regression equation was ΔI (μA) = 0.0642 + 0.1098lg [c] (nM), with a correlation coefficient of 0.9968. The detection limit (LOD) was calculated to be 57.9 pM based on 3σ/S, in which σ is the standard deviation of the blank signal, and S is the slope of the linear calibration plot. Compared with previously reported analytical methods, the PEC aptasensor exhibited a lower detection limit as well as wider linear range ( Table 1), which might be attributed to the combination of the high binding affinity of aptamer to CAP and the ultrasensitive PEC signal amplification through the sensitization effect of GQDs on TiO2 NTs.


### REPRODUCIBILITY, SELECTIVITY, AND STABILITY OF THE APTASENSOR ###

Five independently prepared biosensors were prepared under the same conditions for the detection of 10 nM CAP to examine the reproducibility of the PEC aptasensor. The relative standard deviation (RSD) of 3.62% was obtained, indicating that the fabricated biosensor was repeatable and had an acceptable accuracy. The stability of the aptasensor was also investigated. There was no obvious change of photocurrent response when recorded under ten on/off cycles (RSD = 3.34%), which proved the good stability of signal collection. After the proposed sensor was stored at 4 °C for 14 days, 95% of the initial response was gained for the detection of 10 nM CAP, suggesting the satisfactory storage stability of the developed aptasensor.
To assess the possibility of interference, the photocurrent response of the aptasensor was individually measured toward various antibiotics including oxytetracycline, ciprofloxacin, florfenicol, kanamycin, gentamicin sulfate and CAP under the same experimental conditions. As shown in Fig. 5, there was no remarkable photocurrent change of the aptasensor when incubated with non-target antibiotics molecules as compared with target CAP. These results indicate that proposed aptasensor has an excellent specificity for CAP detection.


### REAL SAMPLE ANALYSIS ###

To evaluate the feasibility of fabricated aptasensor for the determination of CAP in real samples, three spiked honey samples with various CAP concentration were assayed using the PEC aptasensor by the standard addition method. The results were presented in Table 2. The average recoveries were from 96.0% to 102.1% and the relative standard deviation (RSD) was 2.5–3.3%. Thus, the designed aptasensor took advantage of easy operation, low cost, fast response and high sensitivity, which could be regarded as a valid alternative to conventional assays for the determination of CAP in honey samples and other animal derived foods.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

In this study, coaxial heterogeneous graphene quantum dots-sensitized TiO2 nanotube arrays was firstly employed as the photoactive material for fabrication of novel PEC aptasensor for ultrasensitive determination of CAP. The prepared GQDs/TiO2 NTs nanohybrids exhibited significantly enhanced visible-light PEC activity due to its excellent capability to inhibit the photogenerated charge recombination, adequate light absorption ability and fast electron transfer efficiency. Moreover, aptamer was effectively anchored on GQDs/TiO2 NTs without any modification by π–π stacking interaction. Due to the advantages of aptamer and the GQDs/TiO2NTs photoactive material, the “signal-on” PEC aptasensor exhibited high sensitivity, good selectivity, acceptable reproducibility and stability. This novel strategy could afford a promising application in trace levels of antibiotic residue analysis in food safety assessment.
