### TITLE ###

Investigation of photo-induced electron transfer between amino-functionalized graphene quantum dots and selenium nanoparticle and it's application for sensitive fluorescent detection of copper ions

### ABSTRACT ###

Copper ions play an essential role in some biological processes. Currently, there is a need for the development of convenient and reliable analytical methods for the Cu2+ measurement. In the present work, a sensitive fluorescence method was developed for the determination of copper ions. Amino-functionalized graphene quantum dots (af-GQDs) and selenium nanoparticles (Se NPs) were synthetized, respectively, and they were characterized via transmission electron microscope, infrared spectrum analysis and X-ray photoelectron spectrum measurement. Photo-induced electron transfer (PET) between the prepared two nanomaterials could effectively quench the fluorescence of af-GQDs. Cu(II) was reduced to Cu(I) in the presence ascorbic acid and Cu2Se was finally generated on Se NPs surface, which led to the declined PET efficiency and inhibited the fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs. The change in fluorescence intensity was linearly correlated to the logarithm of the Cu2+ concentration from 1 nM to 10 μM, with a detection limit of 0.4 nM under the optimal conditions. The detections of copper ions in water samples were realized via standard addition method and the recovery values varied from 98.7% to 103%. The proposed fluorescence method was also employed to analyze the uptake of Cu2+ into human cervical carcinoma HeLa cells and cisplatin-resistant HeLa cells (HeLa/DDP cells). The experimental results indicate that the decreased hCTR1 expression level in HeLa/DDP cells weakened the uptake of copper ions into these drug-resistant tumor cells.

### INTRODUCTION ###

As everyone knows, optimal uptake of trace elements is necessary for maintenance of homeostasis and improvement of cell protection [1]. Copper ions are important to numerous biological processes. For example, Cu2+ is a cofactor for many enzymes, including Cu, Zn superoxide dismutase (Cu, Zn SOD), lysyl oxidase, cytochrome c oxidase, and dopamine β-hydroxylase [2]. The excessive accumulation of Cu2+ in organisms is highly toxic because this heavy metal is non-degradable and therefore persistent. Disruption of copper homeostasis at the cellular level is associated with serious diseases, including cancer, Menkes syndrome, Alzheimer's and Wilson's diseases [3]. Not a few methods for Cu detection have been proposed, for example, ion chromatography [4], electrochemical measurement [5], fluorescence spectrometry [6], UV/vis spectrophotometry [7], chemiluminescence analysis [8], surface plasmon resonance [9], inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry [10], atomic absorption spectroscopy [11], etc. Among these, fluorescence analysis has received great attention owing to facile operation, high sensitivity, good accuracy and stability.
Semiconductor nanoparticles with three dimensions confined to 2–8 nm length scale are usually called as quantum dots (QDs). They exhibit some unique optical properties including size-dependent tunable photoluminescence, high quantum yields, excellent photostability, narrow emission spectra and long fluorescence lifetimes [12–15]. QDs have attracted increasing attention in the past decade and they found their wide application on the fluorescent analysis of inorganic anions and cations, drugs, biomolecules, and so on. In recent years, a class of novel luminescent nanomaterials that is also named as QDs is graphene quantum dots (GQDs). It is well known that graphene is a kind of sp2 hybridized two dimensional carbon material with honeycomb network structure and it exhibits poor optical property due to the absence of band gap. However, GQDs, which are planar nanocrystals of graphene fragments, display superior photoluminescence performance [16,17]. The quantum yield of GQDs can be further enhanced once this material is functionalized at the surface and edge or doped with heteroatoms such as boron, nitrogen fluorine, sulfur and chlorine. Recent studies show that amino-functionalized GQDs (af-GQDs) and nitrogen-doped GQDs (N-GQDs) have attracted a great deal of interest as excellent fluorescent probes. The detected objects include metal ions [18], amino acid [19], DNA [20], enzyme [21], tumor marker [22], bacteria [23], and virus [24].
Selenium is well known for its semiconductor property, physiological function and pharmacological action including antioxidant, antitumor and immunoregulation. Nanomaterials display structural characteristics that are not available from bulk solids or molecules. It is reported that selenium nanoparticles (Se NPs) exhibit some prominent advantages such as chemical stability and biological activity because of their nanoscale size [25]. Se NPs have attracted considerable attention as a good nutritional supplement source of selenium element [26], and a possible candidate in clinical practice [27] although the high-dosage intake of Se NPs may induce chronic toxicity [28,29]. Some chemical and biologic analyses by using Se NPs have been reported. For example, Se NPs could be used as an alternative label for constructing lateral flow immunochromatographic strip detection of human chorionic gonadotropin, lipoprotein, and melamine [30]. A novel Se NPs-amplified chemiluminescence method has been applied for the determination of dinitrobutylphenol in water samples [31]. A disposable electrochemical paper-based sensor functionalized with Se NPs for the detection of both NO3 - and Hg2+ in water has been developed [32].
To the best of our knowledge, there are no reports on the detection of Cu2+ ions via fluorescence assay by using af-GQDs and Se NPs simultaneously. In this work, these two nanomaterials were prepared, respectively, and transmission electron microscope (TEM), infrared spectroscopy (IR) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) were used to characterize them. The fluorescence quenching mechanism of af-GQDs by Se NPs was investigated. Sensitive fluorescent detection of Cu2+ was achieved based on the fluorescence intensity change of the af-GQDs-Se NPs system in the presence of copper ions and ascorbic acid. The proposed sensor was employed to measure the Cu2+ contents in water samples and investigate the uptake of copper ions into tumor cells and drug-resistant tumor cells.


### EXPERIMENTAL ###



### MATERIALS AND APPARATUS ###

All chemicals used in this work were of analytical grade. Selenious acid, ascorbic acid (vitamin C, Vc), sodium alginate, citric acid (CA), NaOH and copper sulphate were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Cisplatin (cis-diaminedichloroplatinum, DDP) was obtained from Jiangsu Hansoh Pharmaceutical Group Co., Ltd. China. Phosphate buffer saline (PBS, 0.1 M) and acetate buffer saline (ABS, 0.1 M) were prepared with the KH2PO4-Na2HPO4 mixture and the HAc-NaAc system, respectively. Milli-Q ultrapure water was used for the preparation of all aqueous solutions.
An Hitachi F-7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer, equipped with a 300-μL micro quartz cell (1 cm path length), was employed for fluorescence measurements. The particle sizes of af-GQDs and Se NPs were determined ex situ by a Tecnai G2 20ST transmission electron microscope (TEM, FEI, USA). A Nicolet Nexus 670 FTIR spectrometer (Nicolet, USA) was applied for the infrared spectral analysis, and XPS analysis was carried out with an ESCALAB 250Xi X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (Thermo Fisher, UK). The absorption spectra were recorded on a UV-2450 UV–vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). The cell-modality was determined via an Eclipse Ti-S inverted fluorescence microscope (Nikon, Japan), in which the bright-field mode, ×10 eyepiece lens and ×20 objective lens were used.


### PREPARATION OF AF-GQDS AND SE NPS ###

Af-GQDs were synthesized based on CA pyrolysis by using the three-step method. Firstly, 0.5 g CA was heated at 175 °C for 30 min in oil bath until the colorless solid changed gradually to the orange liquid, which suggests the generation of GQDs. Secondly, 7 mL of aqueous ammonia was added into the obtained liquid slowly under ultrasonic condition in order to prepare the homogeneous solution. Finally, the pH of the solution was adjusted to 7.0 via dialysis, using a 300 D dialysis bag. The yellow af-GQDs solution was stored at 4 °C prior to use.
Se NPs were prepared in aqueous solution containing sodium alginate, which served as the soft template. First, the appropriate amount of sodium alginate solution was mixed with the selenious acid solution, and the ascorbic acid solution was added dropwise. The reaction proceeded under continuous ultrasonication for 2 h. The final concentration of sodium alginate, selenious acid and ascorbic acid was 0.2%, 1 mM and 4 mM, respectively. To collect the precipitate, the solution was centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min. Finally, the supernatant was removed, and 5 mL of pH 7.4 PBS containing 0.1% sodium alginate was used to induce the re-dispersion of the precipitate. The required red Se NPs suspension was obtained and it was stored at 4 °C for more than 1 month without obvious sedimentation.


### MEASUREMENT PROCEDURE ###

To a 0.5-mL polyethylene centrifugal tube, 100 μL of Se NPs suspension, 10 μL of 30 μM ascorbic acid solution and 10 μL of Cu2+ solution with different concentrations were added sequentially, and the mixture was incubated at room temperature for 150 min. Next, 30 μL of af-GQDs suspension were added into the mixture, and the volume of the solution was adjusted to 300 μL with 0.1 M PBS or ABS. The fluorescence emission spectra were recorded in the wavelength range from 380 to 650 nm at the excitation wavelength of 345 nm. Both the excitation and emission slits were set at 5 nm.


### CELL CULTURE AND ANALYSIS ###

Human cervical carcinoma HeLa cells were obtained from XiangYa Central Laboratory of Central South University, China. RPMI1640 (Gibco) growth medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (Gibco) was used for the cell culture experiments. Cisplatin-resistant HeLa cells (HeLa/DDP cells) were obtained by incubating HeLa cells in the growth medium containing DDP. Initially 1 ng mL−1 DDP was introduced to HeLa cells and the content of the drug was gradually increased once HeLa cells matched their surroundings. As a result, HeLa/DDP cells could grow steadily in the presence of 0.2 μg mL−1 DDP. When the adherent HeLa cells or HeLa/DDP cells formed a dense monolayer on the substrate, they were trypsinized and separated from the growth medium by centrifugation. To remove adsorbed biomolecules, the cells were gently washed two times with sterile pH 7.4 PBS. They were re-suspended in pH 7.4 PBS at a concentration of 5 × 104 cells mL−1. Next, Cu2+ solution was mixed with the HeLa cells and HeLa/DDP cells suspension, respectively, and the final concentration of copper ions was 10 nM. Several hours later, the mixture composed of the tumor cells and Cu2+ was centrifuged. The supernatant was removed using a micropipette without disturbing the cell pellet. Finally, the Cu2+ content in the supernatant was investigated by using the above mentioned measurement procedure and the fluorescence method.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### CHARACTERIZATION OF AF-GQDS AND SE NPS ###

Fig. 1A shows the TEM image of the as-prepared af-GQDs. These quantum dots were well dispersed and their size was reasonably uniform with the average diameter of 5 nm. As shown in Fig. 1B, the af-GQDs aqueous suspension was the transparent and light yellow solution. The functional groups of af-GQDs were investigated by FTIR analysis. As indicated in Fig. 1C, the absorption peaks at 3200 cm−1 and 1575 cm−1 were associated with the stretching vibration and the bending vibration of N–H bond, respectively. The absorption peak at 1406 cm−1 was derived from the bending vibration of C–N bond of amide group. The above results suggest the existence of amines at the surface and edge of graphene. The photoluminescence of af-GQDs can be improved due to the excellent electron-donating ability of amino groups [33]. The absorption peaks at 1706 cm−1 and 1130 cm−1 were ascribed to the stretching vibration of C˭O and C–O bonds. They imply the presence of oxygen-containing groups in af-GQDs, which can ensure the good water solubility of the prepared quantum dots. In this study, sodium alginate was used in the preparation of Se NPs to regulate nanoparticle growth and prevent agglomeration. One can observe from the TEM image (Fig. 1D) that the Se suspension was comprised of monodispersed nanoparticles with a size distribution from 30 to 60 nm. As shown in Fig. 1E, the transparent and orange red solution was finally obtained after selenious acid was mixed with ascorbic acid. It means that monodinic selenium rather than trigonal selenium was generated because the latter is black [34]. As a surface-sensitive spectroscopic technique based on the photoelectric effect, XPS is normally used for the analysis of elemental composition. Each element can generate XPS peaks at characteristic binding energy values. The XPS analysis of Se NPs was performed and the result is shown in Fig. 1F. Two characteristic peaks at 55.9 eV and 55.1 eV corresponds to Se 3d was found, which are identical with the reported binding energies of elemental selenium [35,36]. So it can be concluded from Fig. 1D–F that elemental selenium nanoparticles were successfully prepared in the present work.


### FLUORESCENCE QUENCHING OF AF-GQDS INDUCED BY SE NPS AND INHIBITION EFFECT OF CU2+ ###

The fluorescence excitation and emission spectra of af-GQDs in PBS (pH 7.5) in the absence and presence of Se NPs, Cu2+, and Vc are shown in Fig. 2. The maximum fluorescence excitation and emission peaks of af-GQDs were obtained at 345 nm and 443 nm, respectively (curves a′ and a). The fluorescence intensity of af-GQDs decreased considerably upon the addition of Se NPs (curves e′ and e). The UV–vis absorption spectra of af-GQDs, Se NPs and their mixture were measured, respectively, and the results are shown in Fig. 3. A weak absorption peak at 324 nm was present on the spectrum of af-GQDs (curve a). Se NPs exhibited an obvious peak at about 264 nm (curve b). These characteristic absorbance peaks could be found on the spectrum of the mixture solution containing af-GQDs and Se NPs (curve c). The absorbance of af-GQDs derived from curve a and that of Se NPs obtained from curve b at the same wavelength was added together and the sum was displayed as curve d. Curves c and d almost overlapped with each other, suggesting that the two kinds of nanoparticles could not form a complex in the ground state. Se NPs with different concentration were added into the af-GQDs solution (in pH 7.5 PBS), and the fluorescence emission spectra were recorded. One can find from Fig. 4 that the fluorescence intensity decreased significantly with increasing Se NP concentration and the emission peak exhibited a dramatic red shift. As shown in the insert, the quenching response was fitted to the Stern–Volmer equation, (1) F 0 / F = 1 + K sv [ Se NPs ] where F 0 and F correspond to the fluorescence intensity of af-GQDs before and after the addition of Se NPs, respectively. The quenching constant, K sv, was estimated to be 2.85 × 103 M−1. These experimental results suggest that dynamic quenching rather than static quenching led to the fluorescence intensity decrease of af-GQDs in the presence of Se NPs. In addition, the fluorescence excitation and emission spectra of af-GQDs hardly overlapped with the absorption spectrum of Se NPs. It indicates that the fluorescence quenching was derived from neither inner filter effect nor fluorescence resonance energy transfer. It is known that carbon nanomaterials with delocalized π-electron system often act as the electron donor when they react with electron-withdrawing agents [37]. In the present work, photo-induced electron transfer (PET) from electron-rich af-GQDs to electron-deficient Se NPs produced non-radiative exciplex, (af-GQDs2+Se2-)*, and finally resulted in the fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs according to Eq. (2). (2) * af - GQDs + Se 0 → * af - GQDs ⋯ Se 0 → ( af - GQDs 2 + Se 2 - ) * → af - GQDs 2 + + Se 2 -
It is observed from curves b′, b, c′, c, d′ and d in Fig. 2 that the fluorescence-intensity changes of af-GQDs were negligible in the presence of Cu2+, Vc and their mixture. The fluorescence-quenching extent of af-GQDs after the introduction of the combination of Se NPs and Vc (curves f′ and f) and the mixture of Se NPs and Cu2+ (curves g′ and g) was close to that after the addition of Se NPs (curves e′ and e). The results suggest that either Vc or Cu2+ had little influence on the Se NPs-induced fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs. However, the fluorescence intensity of af-GQDs in the presence of Se NPs, Vc and Cu2+ were significantly recovered/turned on (curves h′ and h). Bai et al. has reported the reaction between Cu(II) and Se NPs in the presence of vitamin C [38]. Vc reduced Cu(II) to Cu(I) and the latter could be disproportionate to Cu(II) and Cu(0). (3) Cu II aq + reductive Vc → Cu I aq + oxidative Vc (4) 2 Cu I aq → Cu II aq + Cu 0 aq
The PET reaction of Cu(0) with Se NPs, which was similar to that between af-GQDs and Se0 (shown in Eq. (2)), was present. As a result, Cu2Se was finally generated on nanoSe0 according the following equation. (5) 2 Cu 0 aq + nano Se 0 sol → Cu 2 Se nanoSe 0 surface
In the present work, there was a competition between Cu(II) and af-GQDs. In the presence of Cu(II) and Vc, the amount of Se NPs that reacted with af-GQDs reduced. In other words, the PET between af-GQDs and Se NPs was inhibited once Cu2Se was produced. Therefore, the Se NPs-induced fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs was weakened.


### QUANTITATIVE DETECTION OF CU2+ ###

The fluorescence intensity of af-GQDs in the presence of the mixture composed of Se NPs and Vc (F 1) and that in the presence of the mixture comprising Se NPs, Vc and Cu2+ (F 2) were measured, respectively, and their difference, ΔF (ΔF = F 2 ⎻ F 1) was calculated. The ΔF values were recorded when pH and the mixing time of the Cu2+-Se NPs-Vc system changed and they are shown in Fig. 5. The fluorescence intensity change increased with increasing pH from 4.0 to 7.5. At a pH value greater than 7.5, the ΔF value decreased (panel A). Under the acidic condition (pH 4.0–7.0), protons could compete with Cu2+ for reacting with negatively charged nanoSe0, which caused the deficiency of Cu2Se on Se NPs surface. As a result, the Se NPs-induced fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs still appeared and the ΔF value was relatively small. Under the basic condition (pH > 7.5), it was easy for Vc to be oxidized by oxygen in air. In addition, Cu2+ might suffer from hydrolysis. These results were all unfavorable to the generation of Cu(I) and Cu2Se. Hence, pH 7.5 was employed in all the subsequent experiments, since the maximum ΔF value was achieved at this pH. The change in fluorescence intensity was gradually enhanced as the mixing time increased from 0.5 h to 2.5 h (panel B), and it was almost invariable when the latter was further increased. Apparently the reactions described by Eqs. (3)–(5) required some time in which the adequate amount of Cu2Se would be produced on nanoSe0 surface. Thus, the optimal mixing time for Se NPs, Vc and Cu2+ was chosen at 2.5 h.
Af-GQDs were mixed with Se NPs, Vc and Cu2+ with different concentrations in pH 7.5 PBS. The fluorescence intensity of the mixture increased with increasing Cu2+ content, as depicted in Fig. 6. As shown in the insert, a good linear relationship existed between the ΔF value, which is defined in Fig. 5, and the logarithm of the Cu2+ concentration in the range of 1 nM-10 μM with the linear regression equation of ΔF = 85.5 + 114 log c Cu 2 + (R = 0.991). The limit of detection was calculated to be 0.4 nM at the signal-to-noise ratio of 3. The result suggests that the developed fluorescence method using af-GQDs and Se NPs could be used for sensitive detection of Cu2+. The fluorescent probes, linear ranges and detection limits of several fluorescent Cu2+ sensors [39–44] were listed in Table 1. The developed sensor in this work was found to be competitive in comparison with others.
The reproducibility of this assay was estimated at the Cu2+ concentrations of 50 nm and 1 μM. The relative standard deviation (RSD) corresponding to the five independent fluorescence measurement results (the ΔF values) were calculated to be 8.6% and 7.6%, respectively, showing a good reproducibility. In this work, af-GQDs were stored at 4 °C after the preparation. The af-GQDs suspension was ultrasonically re-dispersed before its use. It was found that the fluorescence intensity of these probes remained nearly 98% of the initial value after one month. This result exhibited a satisfying long-term stability of the prepared af-GQDs. The different ΔF values were obtained by measuring the fluorescence intensity of af-GQDs in the presence of the mixture composed of Se NPs and Vc as well as that in the presence of the mixture comprising Se NPs, Vc and different metal ions, respectively, and the results are shown in Fig. 7. The ΔF value induced by Cu2+ was found to be much higher than that derived from other metal ions including Al3+, Co2+, Mn2+, Zn2+, Ni2+, Cd2+, Ag+, Hg2+, Fe3+, Fe2+, Ca2+, K+ and Na+. It means that these potential interfering substances had a little influence on the PET between af-GQDs and Se NPs. Therefore, the proposed fluorescence method displayed a high selectivity for the determination of Cu2+ in the presence of these metal ions.
The Cu2+ contents in tap water and lake water were measured by using the developed sensor. Table 2 shows the detection results from the samples before and after the addition of copper ions with known concentration. The concentrations of Cu2+ in the two water samples were estimated to be 1.12 and 1.63 μM, respectively, which were in good agreement with the results obtained from the atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) measurements, 1.09 and 1.69 μM. The recovery rates of Cu2+ added to these samples were in the range of 98.7–103%, indicating a satisfying accuracy.


### INVESTIGATION ON UPTAKE OF CU2+ INTO TUMOR CELLS ###

It has been reported that Cu transport at cell surface and Cu delivery to intracellular compartments are implicated in various biological processes [45]. In the present work, the uptake of copper ions into tumor cells and drug-resistant tumor cells was investigated based on the proposed fluorescence method. Fig. 8A and B show the microscope images of HeLa cells and HeLa/DDP cells in growth phase, separately. A monolayer of polygon-shaped HeLa cells, which were linked to each other, was observed on the substrate (panel A). It is interesting to be observed from panel B that the cisplatin-resistant cell line exhibited bigger spreading area than HeLa cells although the cell shape hardly altered. In this research, copper ions were added into pH 7.4 PBS, the HeLa cells-PBS suspension and the Hela/DDP cells-PBS suspension, respectively. The Cu2+-cells-PBS mixture suffered from centrifugal separation after different mixing time. The PBS containing copper ions (solution I), the PBS supernatant derived from the Cu2+-HeLa cells-PBS mixture (solutions II) and the PBS supernatant separated from the Cu2+-Hela/DDP cells-PBS mixture (solutions III) were used to inhibit the fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs, respectively. The fluorescence-intensity change induced by solution I at the initial time (0 h) is marked as ΔF 0. The fluorescence-intensity difference caused by solutions I, II and III at the different time (0.5, 1, 4 and 7 h) is marked as ΔF 1. The ratios of ΔF 1 to ΔF 0 were defined as ΔF re and they are shown in Fig. 8C–E. Panel C shows that the ΔF re values induced by solution I at the different time remained basically unchanged. It indicates that copper ions at low concentration were stable in pH 7.4 PBS. One can observe from panels D and E that the ΔF re values caused by solutions II and III gradually declined with the increasing time. The results demonstrate the slow uptake of Cu2+ into the two kinds of tumor cells. The longer the mixing time of Cu2+ with tumor cells was, the less metal ions were remained in the PBS supernatant. As a result, the influence of copper ions on the Se NPs-induced fluorescence quenching of af-GQDs was weakened in sequence. In addition, the ΔF re value induced by solution II was found to be less than that caused by solution III at the same time, which means that fewer copper ions were present in solution II. In other words, the uptake amount of Cu2+ into HeLa cells was more than that into HeLa/DDP cells. It has been reported that Cu(II) is reduced to Cu(I) in the presence of extracellular Fe(III)/Cu(II) reductases, Frelp and Fre2p, which are encoded by the FRE1 and FRE2 genes, respectively, prior to copper uptake [46]. High affinity Cu(I) uptake is mediated by Cu transporters encoded by the CTR1 and CTR3 genes [47]. Human copper transporter 1 (hCTR1) is located in a cytoplasmic vesicular compartment and it is concentrated around the nucleus in the cervical carcinoma cell line HeLa [48]. It has been found that hCTR1 can modulate the influx of Pt(II)-based anticancer drugs such as cisplatin and carboplatin [49]. Our experimental results derived from Fig. 8 suggest that hCTR1 was down-regulated in the cisplatin-resistant tumor cells, HeLa/DDP cells. This down regulation, which might be derived from the protein internalization from plasma membrane [50], had a negative effect on the influx of cisplatin. It should be one of important reasons for the drug-resistance of HeLa/DDP cells. At the same time, the uptake of copper ions into HeLa/DDP cells was inevitably weakened due to the decreased hCTR1 expression level.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

For the determination of copper ions, af-GQDs were prepared via CA pyrolysis, using a three-step method, and Se NPs were synthetized via the reduction of selenious acid, using sodium alginate as a soft template. Af-GQDs exhibited strong fluorescence intensity and it could be effectively quenched by the latter due to photo-induced electron transfer. The generation of Cu2Se on nanoSe0 in the presence of copper ions and Vc had an inhibition effect on the PET between af-GQDs and Se NPs. The fluorescence intensity change of af-GQDs in the presence of Se NPs and Vc depended upon the Cu2+ concentration. Therefore, these prepared nanomaterials show a great promise in the development of a sensitive fluorescence sensor for the determination of copper ions. The uptake amount of Cu2+ into HeLa/DDP cells was found to be less than that into HeLa cells, which might be derived from the down-regulated hCTR1 content in these cisplatin-resistant tumor cells.
