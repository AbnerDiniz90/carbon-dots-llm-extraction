### TITLE ###

Room temperature preparation of water-soluble polydopamine-polyethyleneimine copolymer dots for selective detection of copper ions


### AUTHOR GROUP ###

Zitao Zhong
Li Jia


### ABSTRACT ###

Polydopamine-polyethyleneimine (PDA-PEI) copolymer dots were prepared under mild conditions via the self-polymerization of dopamine and cross-linking with branched PEI (BPEI). The molecular weight of BPEI, mass ratio of dopamine to BPEI, and pH of reaction medium had significant influences on the quantum yield of PDA-PEI copolymer dots. Under the optimized conditions, the synthesized PDA-PEI copolymer dots have an average size of 9.4 ± 2.2 nm in diameter with good water dispersibility. The quantum yield is 12.5%. More importantly, they are very stable against extreme pH and high ionic strength. The PDA-PEI copolymer dots were employed as a fluorescent probe for sensitive and selective Cu2+ detection with a detection limit as low as 1.6 nM and a linear range from 0.0016 to 80 μM. The feasibility of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots-based method was verified by the successful determination of Cu2+ in water and oyster samples, demonstrating its potential for analysis of environmental water and food samples.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Fluorescent carbon nanoparticles or carbon dots (CDs) have inspired extensive studies on them since their accidental discovery during the separation and purification of single-walled carbon nanotubes by Xu et al. in 2004 [1]. They demonstrate specific characteristics, such as small size, bright emission, good water dispersibility and chemical stability. Most importantly, they are much safer for biological use in terms of their biocompatibility and low toxicity as compared to semiconductor quantum dots [2,3].
Polymer dots as one type of CDs possess excellent aqueous dispersibility and a wealth of functional moieties with high oxygen content, which makes them promising alternative fluorescent probes for bioimaging and bioassay [4,5]. They are usually prepared from sole small molecule precursor or polymer precursor (polymers with/without small molecules) through dehydration, condensation, entanglement and crosslinking. The accomplishment of these reactions is mainly dependent on high temperature and assistance of organic solvents. Thus, development of simple, mild and green preparation approaches is still in high demand in order to save energy and avoid the use of hazardous chemicals. Moreover, the stability of polymer dots under high ionic strength and extreme pH conditions is required in order to ensure good performance in bioimaging and bioassay applications.
Dopamine (DA), a kind of catecholamine, is found to undergo oxidative self-polymerization to form polydopamine (PDA) under mild alkaline conditions [6]. Benefitted from the unique properties of PDA, such as good aqueous dispersibility, excellent adhesion and biocompatibility, PDA has been used for a myriad of biological applications [7–13]. PDA is an intrinsic fluorescent polymer with weak fluorescence intensity. The aggregation of polymer chains, intra- and intermolecular coupling cause the extremely low quantum yield (QY) of PDA [14,15]. Therefore, enhancing the fluorescence intensity of PDA has become a meaningful research topic. In 2012, Zhang et al. first reported that using hydrogen peroxide to oxidize PDA nanoparticles can enhance the fluorescence intensity of PDA nanoparticles [16]. Since then, MnO2 and KMnO4 were used as oxidizing agents to prepare fluorescent PDA nanoparticles (PDA dots) [17,18]. Zhao et al. reported the preparation of PDA dots using ammonium persulfate as an oxidizing agent via a hydrothermal method under an acidic environment [19]. PDA dots were also synthesized through hydroxyl-radical-induced degradation of PDA nanoparticles [20]. Unfortunately, the poor QY (less than 1.5%) of PDA dots prepared using these methods still limits their wide applications in bioimaging and bioanalysis fields. Thus, it is desirable to develop a new methodology for preparation of PDA dots with ideal QY under mild conditions.
Branched polyethyleneimine (BPEI) is an amino-rich and water-soluble cationic polymer [21]. The abundance of amino groups can generate strong hydrogen bonding with organic compounds containing oxygen and nitrogen functional groups, and strongly coordinate with metal atoms. BPEI has been reported to be capable of enhancing the fluorescence intensity and photostability of CDs [22–24]. In 2015, Liu et al. reported a method for preparation of PDA-based polymer dots in water through the self-polymerization of DA in the presence of PEI (Mw 600) [25]. The polymer dots with an average diameter of 100 nm were successfully used as a fluorescent probe for biological imaging applications in a physiological environment. However, the fluorescence intensity of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots was pH-dependent. They were not stable beyond the pH range of 7–9. The pH-dependence of PDA-PEI copolymer dots restricts their wide applications. Zhao et al. also prepared PDA-PEI copolymer dots using DA and PEI (Mw 10,000) [26]. The dots had an average size of 200 nm in diameter. The strong fluorescence quenching was observed in the presence of Cu2+, Fe3+, and Ni2+. Thus preparation of PDA-PEI copolymer dots with high stability over a wide pH range and selectivity toward Cu2+ is still desirable.
In this contribution, we synthesized highly stable PDA-PEI copolymer dots with strongly green emission using a facile one-pot method. The preparation conditions were optimized in detail. The as-prepared PDA-PEI copolymer dots demonstrated small size, excellent water dispersibility, as well as stability over a wide pH range and high ionic strength. Furthermore, taking advantage of the specific interactions between amino groups and copper ions, the PDA-PEI copolymer dots were applied for sensitive and selective detection of copper ions with a detection limit (LOD) of 1.6 nM. The feasibility of the fluorescence probe in real samples was evaluated by detection of copper ions in water and oyster samples.

### SYNTHESIS OF PDA-PEI COPOLYMER DOTS ###

The procedure for preparation of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is schematically illustrated in Fig. 1. In a typical synthesis, 10 mg DA·HCl and 10 mg BPEI were added to a 25 mL flat bottom glass flask containing 10 mL Tris-HCl buffer (10 mM, pH 8.5). Subsequently, the mixture was stirred for 12 h at 500 rpm on a magnetic stirrer (M6728, Keezo Technology Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China) under 25 °C, which was controlled by air conditioner. Afterwards, the solution was filtered through a 0.22 µm cellulose ester membrane (Guangzhou F&H Trade Co., Ltd., Guangzhou, China), then dialyzed against distilled water through a dialysis bag (molecular weight cutoff 1000 Da, MYM Biological Technology Co., Ltd., Hyderabad, India) for 4 h. Finally, the PDA-PEI copolymer dots inside the dialysis bag were collected and stored at 4 °C for further use.


### QY MEASUREMENT OF PDA-PEI COPOLYMER DOTS ###

Quinine sulfate was selected as the standard reference material. The QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is calculated via the following equation: φ x = φ s k x k s n x 2 n s 2 where φ is the QY, k is the slope of calibration plot (obtained from linear fitting curve of integrated fluorescence intensity against absorbance of different sample concentrations), n is the refractive index of the solvent. The subscripts x and s refer to PDA-PEI copolymer dots and quinine sulfate, respectively. Quinine sulfate (literature φ = 55% at 313 nm) was dissolved in 0.1 M H2SO4 (n = 1.33) and PDA-PEI copolymer dots were dispersed in distilled water (n = 1.33).


### FLUORESCENCE DETERMINATION OF CU2+ ###

The fluorescence determination of Cu2+ was carried out as the following procedure. PDA-PEI polymer dots (10 μL) were added to the sample solutions (300 μL) containing different concentrations of Cu2+. The mixture was shaken for 5 s and reacted at ambient temperature for 4 min. Then, the emission spectra (430–650 nm) were measured with excitation at 380 nm by keeping the photomultiplier tube voltage at 700 V and the excitation and emission slit width at 10 nm. The fluorescence intensities of PDA-PEI copolymer dots with and without the addition of Cu2+ were represented as F and F 0, respectively.


### DETECTION OF CU2+ IN WATER AND OYSTER SAMPLES ###

The tap water from our laboratory and the river water from Pearl River (Guangzhou, China) were collected. The water samples were first filtered through 0.22 µm membrane to remove particulate matters. After that, the water samples were spiked with different concentrations of Cu2+ ranging from 0 to 50 μM. Subsequently, PDA-PEI copolymer dots were added into the water samples for detection.
The oyster samples were purchased from a local market. The edible part of the fresh oyster was washed several times with deionized water. Then, the oyster samples were cut into small pieces and dried under 40 °C in an oven (GC9890, Shanghai Linghua Instruments Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China). Subsequently, 1.0 g dried samples were added to 68% (w w−1) HNO3 solution (10 mL). The mixture was heated at 150 °C for 6 h in an autoclave. Finally, the samples were transferred to a flask and adjusted to a final volume of 500 mL with deionized water. The digested samples were directly used for ICP-OES analysis. For the determination of Cu2+ by the proposed method, the pH of samples was adjusted to 5.0 with 10 M NaOH.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### INVESTIGATION OF THE CONDITIONS FOR PREPARATION OF PDA-PEI COPOLYMER DOTS ###

The extremely low QY of PDA is caused by the aggregation of polymer chains, intra- and intermolecular coupling effect [14,15]. Thus, decreasing the PDA size and destroying the intra- and intermolecular coupling effect in PDA would enhance the QY. The abundance of amino groups in BPEI can generate strong hydrogen bonding with organic compounds containing oxygen and nitrogen functional groups. Hence, the hydrogen-bonding interactions between PEI and PDA would significantly reduce the size of PDA aggregates and weaken the intra- and intermolecular coupling effect, thereby enhancing the fluorescence intensity of PDA-PEI copolymer. In order to prepare PDA-PEI copolymer dots with high fluorescence intensity, several preparation conditions were investigated. The QYs were measured and used to estimate the influence of different factors on the prepared PDA-PEI copolymer dots.
Firstly, the influence of molecular weight of BPEI on the preparation of PDA-PEI copolymer dots was studied. As shown in Fig. 2a, the QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots decreases with the increase of molecular weight of BPEI. When the molecular weight of BPEI is 600, the QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is highest (12.5%). When the molecular weight of BPEI is beyond 1800, the obvious aggregation of copolymer is observed. The results demonstrate that low molecular weight of BPEI is in favor of its incorporation into the PDA aggregates through the Michael addition of PEI on the 5,6-dihydroxyindole unit of PDA [27]. The hydrogen bonding between the amino groups in BPEI and amino/phenolic hydroxyl groups in PDA effectively destroys the intra- and intermolecular coupling, thus significantly decreasing the size of PDA aggregates. The commercially available BPEI having the lowest molecular weight is BPEI 600. Thus, preparation of PDA-PEI dots using BPEI with molecular weight below 600 was not investigated. BPEI 600 was used for subsequent experiments.
Secondly, the effect of the mass ratio of DA·HCl to BPEI on the QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots was investigated. During the process for synthesis of PDA-PEI copolymer dots, the color of solutions changed from light yellow to deep red with the increase of PEI mass. As shown in Fig. 2b, when the mass ratio of DA·HCl to BPEI is from 1:0.8–1:1, the QY of products is highest. Beyond the ratio range, it is not favorable to enhance the QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots. Therefore, the mass ratio of 1:1 was used for subsequent experiments.
Thirdly, the effect of pH of reaction medium (10 mM Tris-HCl) on the QY of products was investigated. The pH of reaction medium plays an important role in the Michael-addition. It is well known that basic medium favors the Michael-addition. As shown in Fig. 2c, the QY of product prepared at pH 8.5 is highest. Beyond pH 8.5, the QY of products decreases. The strongly basic medium can speed up the intermolecular oxidative polymerization of DA and the coupling reaction (Michael-addition) between PDA and PEI. Nevertheless, the condition is not favorable to prepare the PDA-PEI copolymer with small size. The QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots prepared in Tris-HCl (10 mM, pH 8.5) with the mass ratio of DA·HCl to BPEI 600 at 1:1 is highest (12.5%). Thus, they were characterized and used for sensing copper ions. The optimal parameters for preparation of PDA-PEI dots were also investigated using orthogonal method [28]. The results are shown in Electronic Supplementary Material (Tables S1, S2, Fig. S1).


### CHARACTERIZATION OF PDA-PEI COPOLYMER DOTS ###

The particle size and morphology information of PDA-PEI copolymer dots were observed using TEM. As shown in Fig. 3a, the PDA-PEI copolymer dots are well dispersed with a uniform size of 9.4 ± 2.2 nm in diameter and an even distribution. The inset shows the representative image of an individual nanoparticle. The zeta potentials of PDA-PEI copolymer dots were measured in 10 mM phosphate solutions at different pH values. As shown in Fig. S2, there exists an inflection point (pH 6.8), which is the isoelectric point of PDA-PEI copolymer dots. The zeta potentials of PDA-PEI copolymer dots are positive below pH 6.8, indicating that PDA-PEI copolymer dots take positive charges due to the protonation of amino groups in PDA and PEI. Above pH 6.8, the zeta potentials of PDA-PEI copolymer dots are negative due to the ionization of phenolic hydroxyl groups in PDA component. In addition, Fig. S2 shows that the isoelectric point of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is higher than that of PDA nanoparticles (3.8), suggesting that BPEI indeed incorporates with PDA and PDA-PEI copolymer dots possess more amino groups.
FT-IR spectra were used to identify the functional groups of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots. As shown in Fig. S3, the peak at 3441 cm−1 is assigned to the stretching vibrations of O-H and N-H. The two peaks at 2923 and 2852 cm−1 are attributed to the stretching vibration of C-H bond. As compared to PDA, the intensities of the two peaks in PDA-PEI copolymer dots are significantly enhanced. The peak at 1645 cm−1 originates from C-N vibration existing in PEI. The results suggest that PDA-PEI copolymer dots contain a wealth of amino and hydroxyl groups, which endows the dots with good hydrophilicity and water dispersibility.
The optical properties of PDA-PEI copolymer dots were characterized by UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra. Compared to the UV–vis spectrum of PDA (Fig. S4), in the UV–vis absorption spectrum of PDA-PEI, a new characteristic peak appears at about 365 nm. Furthermore, the peak located at 280 nm in PDA has a slightly red shift in PDA-PEI copolymer dots. The results also demonstrate that PDA is successfully cross-linked with BPEI.
As shown in Fig. 3b, PDA-PEI copolymer dots can be excited by various excitation wavelengths ranging from 305 to 440 nm. The emission wavelength has a slight red shift with the increase of excitation wavelength in the range of 305–440 nm. The fluorescence intensity is excitation wavelength-dependent. The maximum emission wavelength and optimal excitation wavelength are 530 and 380 nm, respectively. In this case, the strongest fluorescence intensity is achieved. The insets of Fig. 3b show the aqueous solution of PDA-PEI copolymer dots under the irradiation of natural light and UV light at 365 nm. As shown from the left cell (inset of Fig. 3b), the aqueous solution of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is very limpid and clear, indicating that the PDA-PEI copolymer dots possess excellent water dispersibility. When the aqueous solution of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is irradiated with UV light at 365 nm, the strongly emitted green fluorescence is observed (right cell of inset of Fig. 3b).
The influences of pH and ionic strength on the fluorescence intensity of PDA-PEI copolymer dots were investigated. As shown in Fig. 4a, the fluorescence intensity of PDA-PEI copolymer dots exhibits about 20% variation in the pH range of 5–11, outside of which, the fluorescence intensity still remains greater than 67%, compared to that at pH 9.0. The QYs of PDA-PEI copolymer dots were respectively measured to be 4.8% (pH 3.0) and 3.4% (pH 12.0). The results suggest that the copolymer dots are suitable for the applications even under extreme pH conditions. As shown in Fig. 4b, the fluorescence intensities of PDA-PEI copolymer dots in 10 mM phosphate solutions (pH 7.0) containing different concentrations of NaCl have no obvious change. Compared to the solution without NaCl, the emission intensity remains greater than 88% even in the solution containing 1 M NaCl. The results suggest that the copolymer dots are highly stable toward highly ionic conditions. Furthermore, the fluorescence intensity remains unchanged after illumination by UV light (365 nm) for 1 h (Fig. 4c), suggesting the copolymer dots possess excellent photostability.
The PDA-PEI copolymer dots are compared with previously reported PDA-based polymer dots [25,26]. The polymer dots prepared by Liu et al. [25] have an average diameter of 100 nm and demonstrate stability in pH range of 7–9. Their QY is measured to be 3.2%. The polymer dots prepared by Zhao et al. [26] have an average diameter of 200 nm and QY of 2.6%. In this study, the size of PDA-PEI copolymer dots (9.4 nm) is significantly decreased. In addition, the PDA-PEI copolymer dots possess good stability in a wider pH range. More importantly, the QY of PDA-PEI copolymer dots (12.5%) demonstrates absolute superiority. These advantages of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots suggest their potential application prospect.


### FLUORESCENCE DETERMINATION OF CU2+ AND POSSIBLE MECHANISM ###

Inspired by the strongly chelating ability of BPEI with abundant amino groups to Cu2+ [29,30], the PDA-PEI copolymer dots are expected to serve as a fluorescent probe for selective and sensitive determination of Cu2+. As shown in the inset of Fig. 5a, the strong green-emission of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots is quenched obviously after addition of Cu2+. Addition of Cu2+ decreases the fluorescence intensity of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots, but has no effect on the fluorescence wavelength. It has been reported that the fluorescence of CDs containing amino groups could be quenched by Cu2+ due to the electron transfer effect of cupric amine complexes [31]. Therefore, the mechanism for the Cu2+-mediated fluorescence quenching of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots is speculated. Copper ions first chelate with the amine groups in PDA-PEI copolymer dots to form cupric amine complexes; then electron transfer process occurs from the PDA-PEI to Cu2+ in cupric amine complexes (Fig. 1). To confirm the binding capacity of amino groups in PDA-PEI for Cu2+ and the quenching activity of the formed cupric amine complexes for the copolymer dots, EDTA, a strong metal ion chelator, was added to the mixture containing PDA-PEI copolymer dots and Cu2+. The association constant of Cu2+ with EDTA (EDTA-Cu2+ complex as 6.3 × 1018) is larger than that with amino group (BPEI-Cu2+ complex as 1.1 × 1013) [32]. As displayed in Fig. 5a, the quenched fluorescence of PDA-PEI copolymer dots by Cu2+ is effectively recovered after adding EDTA since EDTA has a stronger chelation ability with Cu2+ than the amino groups in PDA-PEI copolymer groups. The results confirm the formation of cupric amine complexes and the quenching activity of the complexes for the copolymer dots. The Cu2+-induced fluorescence quenching of PDA-PEI copolymer dots was utilized to develop a fluorescence sensor for measurement of Cu2+ in aqueous media.
The influence of pH on the detection of Cu2+ was investigated. Fig. 5b shows the fluorescence intensities of PDA-PEI copolymer dots in the absence and presence of 10 μM Cu2+ at different pH values from 3.0 to 12.0. The addition of Cu2+ leads to the quenching of PDA-PEI copolymer dots fluorescence over a wide pH range from 3.0 to 12.0. However, the quenching degree (F 0-F) at different pH values is quite different. In strongly acidic media (pH 3.0), the addition of Cu2+ has slight fluorescence quenching effect for PDA-PEI copolymer dots, which may be attributed to the fact that the amino groups in PDA-PEI copolymer dots are well protonated and thus unable to complex with Cu2+ to form fluorescence-quenching cupric amine complexes. In the pH range of 5.0–11.0, the variation in the response of PDA-PEI dots for Cu2+ leads to the difference in the sensitivity of PDA-PEI-based assay for Cu2+ detection. At pH 9.0, Cu2+ demonstrates the strongest quenching ability for PDA-PEI dots. However, considering the hydrolysis of high concentration of Cu2+ in basic medium, pH 5.0 was used for investigation of the analytical performance of the PDA-PEI-based assay.
The response rate of fluorescence signal of PDA-PEI copolymer dots to Cu2+ at pH 5.0 was then investigated. As shown in Fig. 5c, the fluorescence intensity of PDA-PEI copolymer dots decreases rapidly upon the addition of 10 μM Cu2+ and reaches a plateau beyond 4 min. The fluorescence intensity has no distinct change in the following 1 h. This result indicates that the quenching of Cu2+ for the fluorescence of PDA-PEI copolymer dots is fast, implying a rapid and reliable sensing of Cu2+.
The sensitivity was evaluated by recording the emission spectra of the PDA-PEI copolymer dots solution after adding different concentrations of Cu2+. The corresponding results are depicted in Fig. 6a. It is observed that the fluorescence intensity at 530 nm (excited at 380 nm) gradually decreases with the increase of Cu2+ concentration. Beyond 80 µM, further increasing the concentration of Cu2+, the quenching intensity of Cu2+ for PDA-PEI dots reaches a plateau. The results demonstrate that the limitation of the upper detection limit to 80 µM may be due to the saturation of the active complexing centers for Cu2+ in the PDA-PEI. The quenching intensity (F 0-F) exhibits a good linear relationship against the logarithm of concentration of Cu2+ in the range from 0.0016 to 80 μM, which is expressed as an equation, F 0-F = (730.8 ± 13.5) log c – (1344 ± 58), with a determination coefficient (R2) of 0.9949, where F 0 and F are the fluorescence intensities of copolymer dots in the absence and presence of Cu2+ and c represents the concentration of Cu2+ (nM). The detection limit (LOD) of Cu2+ at a signal to noise ratio of 3 is calculated to be 1.6 nM (n = 10) [28]. The method for calculation of LOD is shown in Electronic Supplementary Material (Fig. S5).


### SELECTIVITY FOR CU2+ DETERMINATION ###

A series of metal ions (K+, Na+, Ag+, Co2+, Ni2+, Mn2+, Ba2+, Pb2+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Al3+, Fe3+, Cr3+) and anions (F−, Cl−, Br−, I−, N O 2 − , N O 3 − , CH3COO−, C O 3 2 − , S O 3 2 − , S O 4 2 − , S 2 O 3 2 − ) were used to evaluate the selectivity of PDA-PEI copolymer dots to Cu2+. First, the same concentrations of Cu2+ and other potential interfering species (10 μM) were used. As shown in Figs. 6b and 6c, the significant fluorescence quenching is observed for PDA-PEI copolymer dots in the presence of Cu2+. In contrast, the anions and other metal ions except for Fe3+ do not cause tremendous decrease in the fluorescence intensity of PDA-PEI copolymer dots. This is because the association constant of amines with Cu(II) (BPEI-Cu(II) complex as 1.1 × 1013) is higher than those of amines with most other metal ions (i.e., BPEI-Co(II) complex as 5.0 × 104) [32]. The amines in PDA-PEI copolymer dots preferentially chelate with Cu2+ to form BPEI-Cu(II) complexes.
Next, in order to check if the PDA-PEI copolymer dots are appropriate for determination of Cu2+ in complex samples, other potential interfering species whose concentrations (1 mM) are 100-fold more than that of Cu2+ were used to evaluate the selectivity of PDA-PEI dots toward Cu2+. As shown in Fig. S6, high concentrations of anions and metal ions except for Co2+ and Fe3+ do not induce the significant fluorescence quenching of PDA-PEI dots. The fluorescence quenching degree of PDA-PEI dots in the presence of Cu2+ (10 μM) is more than 1.5-times over those of Co2+ and Fe3+ (1 mM). The PDA-PEI dots with an average size of 200 nm were reported to have strong fluorescence quenching in the presence of the same concentrations of Cu2+, Fe3+, and Ni2+ [26]. The results demonstrate that smaller PDA-PEI dots have better selectivity toward Cu2+.


### APPLICATION IN WATER AND OYSTER SAMPLES ###

To evaluate the feasibility of PDA-PEI copolymer dots-based Cu2+ sensing system in real water samples, the tap water from laboratory and the river water from Pearl River (Guangzhou, China) were collected and analyzed. A standard addition method is applied in the assay. As shown in Fig. S7, the method demonstrates good linear response to Cu2+ in tap water and river water. The fitted equations for the linear relationships of F 0-F versus log c are respectively, F 0-F = (576.3 ± 13.9) log c – (1066 ± 53), R2 = 0.9954 (tap water), and F 0-F = (669.9 ± 17.5) log c – (1323 ± 55), R2 = 0.9946 (river water). The good linear responses of PDA-PEI copolymer dots to Cu2+ validate the feasibility of the method in the detection of Cu2+ in real water samples.
In order to further verify the validity of the method for complex samples, oyster samples were chosen. The results were also compared with those by the ICP-OES method. As shown in Table 1, the contents of Cu2+ determined by the proposed method are consistent with those by the ICP-OES method. Furthermore, statistical analysis of the results by the two methods was performed using the Student's t-test and Fisher's F-test through the Excel software [28]. P (probability) values are greater than 0.05, indicating no significant difference in their accuracy and precision.
The approach for the determination of Cu2+ is compared with previously reported fluorescent sensors based on the fluorescence quenching [33–36], as shown in Table 2. The approach demonstrates absolute superiority in terms of linear range and detection limit. Furthermore, different from other fluorescent probes, PDA-PEI copolymer dots can be facilely synthesized under room temperature (25 °C). In addition to the water samples, PDA-PEI dots are also applied for detection of Cu2+ in oyster samples.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

The PDA-PEI copolymer dots were prepared under mild conditions using a facile and one-pot method. They possess good water dispersibility and high QY. In addition, they demonstrate good stability toward extreme pH and high ionic strength. They are found to be excellent fluorescence probe for Cu2+ detection. Cu2+ can react with the amino groups on the surface of PDA-PEI copolymer dots to form a chelate complex, subsequently resulting in the quenching of the fluorescence of PDA-PEI copolymer dots via electron transfer mechanism. The PDA-PEI copolymer dots provided rapid, selective, sensitive, and reliable fluorometric probe for Cu2+ sensing in aqueous media. The feasibility of the probe in real samples was validated by successful detection of Cu2+ in water and oyster samples. Furthermore, owing to the easy preparation, strong fluorescence, as well as good stability, the PDA-PEI copolymer dots are expected to have great potential in chemo/biosensors.
