### TITLE ###

Genotoxic response and damage recovery of macrophages to graphene quantum dots


### AUTHOR GROUP ###

Lina Xu
Jian Zhao
Zhenyu Wang


### ABSTRACT ###

The potential adverse effects of graphene quantum dots (GQDs) have increasingly attracted attention. Our present study revealed the genotoxic responses of rat alveolar macrophages (NR8383) to aminated graphene QDs (AG-QDs) and detected the cellular recovery after removing AG-QDs. Global gene expression analysis from RNA-sequencing showed that AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) caused significant alterations in expression of 2898 genes after exposure for 24 h. Among these, 1335 and 1563 genes were up-regulated and down-regulated, respectively. Based on the Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Gene and Genomes (KEGG) analysis, we found that most of the down-regulated genes were responsive to “cell cycle”, which correlated well with the cell cycle arrest data that AG-QDs triggered cell cycle arrest at S (synthesis) and G2/M (second gap/mitosis) phase. The percentages of cells in S and G2/M phase were increased by 4.5%, and 29.0%, respectively. In addition, the up-regulated genes related with “endocytosis” and “phagocytosis” were identified, which could regulate the internalization of AG-QDs by endocytosis and phagocytosis. After removing exposed AG-QDs and re-incubating the cells in fresh medium, the arrest of S and G2/M phase in NR8383 cells was reduced, and the cell cycle gradually recovered. This cellular recovery could be attributed to the cellular excretion of AG-QDs and the up-regulation of the DNA-repair-related genes (Rad51, Brca2, and Atm). The current work provides insights into the potential hazards of AG-QDs in transcriptional level and presented the long-term effects of AG-QDs on organisms in environment.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Graphene quantum dots (GQDs), with exceptional physical and chemical properties, have attracted tremendous attention in environmental, biological, and energy-related applications (Zheng et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017). With such promising potential in these areas, GQDs would probably be released into the environment and may trigger uncertain ecological effects. Recently, the toxicity of GQDs on mammalian cells has been investigated, and a number of studies have found that GQDs exhibited low cytotoxicity (Chandra et al., 2014; Qin et al., 2015). However, Qin et al. (2015) found that GQDs slightly affected the cell viability, whereas significantly activated apoptosis, autophagy, and inflammatory signal pathways in THP-1 cells. It has also been shown that GQDs induced DNA damage in NIH-3T3 cells via increasing the expression of DNA damage response proteins (p53, Rad 51, and OGG1), although changes in cytotoxicity by GQDs were not obvious (Wang et al., 2015). In addition, our previous study shown that GQDs could interact with DNA and induce the cleavage of DNA chains (Xu et al., 2018). These findings indicated that GQDs might be potentially genotoxic, which could be observed more sensitively than apparent toxicity (e.g., growth inhibition) (Tang et al., 2016). However, the genotoxicity of GQDs at noncytotoxic dose and the relevant cellular responses remain unclear. Amine-modified GQDs (AG-QDs) as one class of functional GQDs could significantly promote linkage between graphene-materials and DNA by amine groups (Junkermeier et al., 2013). Therefore, we comprehensively investigated the genotoxicity of AG-QDs at noncytotoxic dose, including global gene expression and toxicogenomic responses.
Many studies regarding nanotoxicity focused on the effects of exposure to nanoparticles (NPs) over time. However, nonstop exposure to NPs seems unrealistic in natural environment. Thus, it is of high importance to investigate the cell recoverability and potential long-term consequences once NPs have been removed. It has been shown that cell viability, cell cycle, and cell functions could gradually recover after removing exposed NPs (e.g. CuO, poly (amidoamine) (PAMAM) dendrimers, and Au) (Wang et al., 2012; Feliu et al., 2014; Mironava et al., 2014). Excretion of NPs from cellular systems and organelles has been considered as an important pathway for cellular detoxification (Mironava et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2012). AG-QDs with small lateral size were expected to be excreted from cells to make the injured cells return to normal status. On the other hand, DNA repair is essential to defend against genotoxicants, maintaining genetic integrity and cell survival (Jeggo et al., 2016; Singh et al., 2009). Although current investigations on nano-genotoxicology suggested that the genes related to DNA repair were down-regulated after exposure to NPs (e.g. ZnO, Ag, and Au) (Pati et al., 2016; Asharani et al., 2012; Li et al., 2008), the DNA-damage recovery after removal of NPs is still unknown. It is reported that a large array of DNA damage was likely to be repairable (Kienzler et al., 2013). We therefore hypothesize that the toxicogenomic response induced by AG-QDs could be eliminated after removing AG-QDs, and this recovery could be attributed to the cellular excretion of AG-QDs and DNA damage repair.
In the current work, macrophages, the “first responder” against exogenous particles/agents (Vasconcelos et al., 2013), were used as a model cell line. The objectives of this work were to investigate (1) the genotoxicity of AG-QDs at noncytotoxic dose, and the regulation of differentially expressed genes on cell cycle and AG-QDs internalization; and (2) the cell cycle recoverability, cellular excretion, and DNA damage repair after removing AG-QDs. The findings from this work will provide critical data for better assessment of the potential hazards and long-time effects of AG-QDs on organisms in environment.


### MATERIALS AND METHODS ###



### CHARACTERIZATION OF AG-QDS ###

AG-QDs suspension (prepared in deionized water, 1000 μg/mL, pH 7.0) were purchased from Nanjing XFNANO Materials Tech Co., Ltd (China). One drop of AG-QDs was evaporated on a copper grid to observe particle morphology using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (H-7650, Hitachi, Japan). The TEM images were recorded at an acceleration voltage of 100 kV. The thickness of AG-QDs was determined by atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Agilent-5400, USA). The AG-QDs suspension was evaporated on freshly cleaved mica (approx 1 cm × 1 cm), and then the topographic images of AG-QDs were obtained by AFM in tapping mode. Freeze-dried AG-QDs were used to analyze the surface functional property using Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) (Nicolet 6700, USA).


### RNA SEQUENCING (RNA-SEQ) ###

The rat alveolar macrophages cell line (NR8383) (purchased from Shanghai Institute for Biological Sciences, Chinese Academy of Sciences) was cultured in F12K medium (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) supplemented with 15% FBS (PAA Laboratories GmbH, Austria), 2 mM l-glutamine (Amresco Inc., USA), and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Haoyang Biological Manufacture Co., Tianjin, China) in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2 at 37 °C. Cell viability of NR8383 cells after AG-QDs (0, 100, 500, and 750 μg/mL) exposure for 24 h was determined by Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8, Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology, China) (Xu et al., 2018).
After exposure to AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) for 24 h, NR8383 cells (1 × 107 cells/mL) were collected to analyze the genes expression via RNA-Seq technology. Total RNA of the cells was extracted by the mirVana miRNA Isolation Kit (Ambion) following the manufacturer's protocol, and then purified by Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). The integrated RNA with RNA Integrity Number (RIN) ≥ 7 was reverse-transcribed to cDNA. The cDNA library was constructed by Shanghai OE Biotech Company (Shanghai, China) using TruSeq Stranded mRNA LTSample Prep Kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer's instructions, and sequenced on the Illumina sequencing platform (HiSeqTM 2500). The obtained high-quality reads from sequencing were mapped to reference Rattus norvegicus genome (Langmead and Salzberg, 2012). After comparing with the sequences of reference genes, the differentially expressed genes induced by AG-QDs were identified using the DESeq (2012) functions estimateSizeFactors and nbinomTest. Fold Change > 2 or <0.5, p value < 0.05 were set as the threshold for defining significant up or down expression, respectively. The functional enrichment analysis of the differentially expressed genes was based on Gene Ontology (GO). The minimum number of genes in the GO terms was set at 3 for the up-regulated genes and at 4 for the down-regulated genes. The pathway enrichment analysis of the differentially expressed genes was performed using Kyoto Encyclopedia of Gene and Genomes (KEGG) (http://www.genome.jp/kegg/). Top 20 enrichment KEGG pathways analysis was implemented with a cut off p value < 0.05 and the overlap of at least 10 differentially expressed genes in each pathway.


### QUANTITATIVE REAL-TIME PCR ANALYSIS ###

The expression levels of the differentially expressed genes in RNA-Seq sequencing were verified using quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR). Briefly, NR8383 cells (1 × 107 cells/mL) were exposed to AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) for 24 h. Total RNA in the NR8383 cells was extracted by Trizol plus RNA purification kit (Invitrogen, USA). The Oligo-dT-primed cDNA was synthesized from RNA via an M-MLV Reverse-Transcriptase Kit (Invitrogen). The qRT-PCR reaction was performed with a Takara SYBR Premix DimerEraserTM (Perfect Real Time, Japan). Actin2 was included as a reference gene. The primers of Actin2 and 6 genes (Cdkn2b, Gadd45b, Cdc14a, Cdc45, Ccne2, and Rbl1) are shown in Table S1.


### CELL CYCLE ASSAYS ###

NR8383 cells (1 × 107 cells/mL) were exposed to AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) for 24 h, and then were harvested and washed with cold phosphate buffered saline (PBS) (4 °C) for three times. The cells were fixed in 70% ice-cold ethanol and incubated at 4 °C for 12 h. After fixation, the cells were re-suspended in clod PBS containing 0.2% triton X 100 (v/v) at 4 °C for 30 min to permeabilize the cells. The cells were stained with propidium iodide (PI) (1 mg/mL) containing 10 mg/mL RNAse for 30 min at 37 °C. The cell cycle was assessed by flow cytometry (FCM) (Becton Dickinson, Mountain View, USA) and analyzed using the Flowjo software (Tree Star, Ashland, USA).


### AG-QDS INTERNALIZATION ###

NR8383 cells (1 × 107 cells/mL) were exposed to AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) for 24 h, and then washed three times with PBS to remove free AG-QDs. Cellular uptake of AG-QDs was investigated using FCM. A profile of the sample could be obtained by examining both the forward scatter (FSC) and side scatter (SSC) in FCM. SSC intensity which represented the cellular uptake of NPs was analyzed by FACSDiva 6.1.2 software (BD Biosciences) (Zhao and Ibuki, 2015). The uptake pathways were analyzed via endocytosis and phagocytosis inhibitors (NaN3, 3 mM and cytochalasin D, 10 μM). NR8383 cells were pre-incubated with inhibitors for 30 min, subsequently exposed to AG-QDs for 24 h. The internalization of AG-QDs in cells was analyzed by FCM.


### CELLULAR RECOVERY EXPERIMENTS ###

After exposure with AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) for 24 h, NR8383 cells were washed with PBS for three times and re-incubated with fresh culture medium without AG-QDs for 48 h and 96 h. Cell cycle of the re-incubated cells was detected by FCM as described in Section 2.4, and analyzed by Flowjo software. After 48-h and 96-h re-incubation in the fresh medium, the internalized AG-QDs in NR8383 cells were quantified by the SSC intensity in FCM. The fluorescence intensity of AG-QDs in re-incubation medium was detected by fluorescence spectrophotometer (Hitach F-4500, Japan). In addition, the expression levels of 6 DNA repair genes (Rad51, Brca2, Atm, Msh2, Rpa2, and Neil1) after removing AG-QDs (0, 48 and 96 h) were detected by qRT-PCR following the above method (Section 2.3). Actin2 was included as a reference gene. The primers of these 6 DNA repair genes (Rad51, Brca2, Atm, Msh2, Rpa2, and Neil1) were shown in Table S2.


### REACTIVE OXYGEN SPECIES (ROS) GENERATION ###

NR8383 cells were exposed to 100 μg/mL AG-QDs for 24 h, and then the intracellular ROS was detected using a fluorescent probe 2′, 7′-dichlorofluorescin diacetate (DCFH-DA, Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology, China). In addition, the ROS levels of NR8383 cells after re-incubation with fresh culture medium for 48 h and 96 h were also detected.


### STATISTICAL ANALYSIS ###

All experiments were conducted at least in triplicate. The data were expressed with mean ± standard deviation. The statistical significance was analyzed by One-way ANOVA with LSD and t-test using SPSS Statistics 20.0 (p < 0.05).


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### GENE EXPRESSION AND GO CATEGORIES ANALYSIS ###

AG-QDs used in this work were well dispersed in water. TEM image showed that the size of individual AG-QDs was 4–6 nm (Fig. S1A). The thickness of AG-QDs from AFM imaging was ~0.7 nm (Fig. S1B), corresponding to a single layer of oxidized graphene (Zhao et al., 2015). FTIR detected that the main functional groups of AG-QDs were CC/CC, COH, CO, OCOH, and CN (Fig. S1C). Although AG-QDs had a typical D and G bank with the peak intensity ratio (ID/IG) of 0.86 indicating the rich defects, the 2D peak, which could determine the number of layers of AG-QDs was not observed (Xu et al., 2018).
After incubation for 24 h, AG-QDs at 100 μg/mL insignificantly inhibited the growth of NR8383 cells (Fig. S2), which was consistent with our previous study (Xu et al., 2018); however, significant inhibition on cell growth was observed after AG-QDs exposure at 500 and 750 μg/mL. Therefore, the noncytotoxic dose of AG-QDs at 100 μg/mL was selected to further investigate the genotoxicity by RNA-Seq. After AG-QDs exposure for 24 h, RNA-Seq generated over 46 million 125 bp clean reads per sample, yielding a very high sequencing depth (Table S3). More than 83% of the obtained reads could map to the annotated Rattus norvegicus genome. The clean reads mapped in proper pairs were >65% (Table S3). The hierarchy cluster analysis for the mapped reads revealed that exposure to AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) caused 2898 differentially expressed genes in NR8383 cells (fold changes > 2 or <0.5, p value < 0.05) (Fig. S3). Among these, 1335 genes were up-regulated and 1563 genes were down-regulated, which were obviously reflected in the MA plot (Fig. S3). To confirm RNA-Seq results, 6 genes (up-regulation: Cdkn2b, Gadd45b, Cdc14a; down-regulation: Cdc45, Ccne2, and Rbl1) were selected for qRT-PCR validation. The expression tendency of tested genes by sequencing analysis and qRT-PCR experiments showed a similar pattern (Fig. S4), suggesting that the expression results generated by RNA-Seq were reliable. Therefore, RNA-Seq results revealed that AG-QDs had a significant impact on the global gene expression of NR8383 cells. These findings were consistent with that of Li et al. (Li et al., 2018), in which hydroxyl-modified GQDs were reported to cause 8151 differentially-expressed genes in HET-1A cells. In addition, GQDs could also stimulate the expression of DNA damage response proteins (p53, Rad 51, and OGG1) in NIH-3T3 cells (Wang et al., 2015). But, the protein damage of AG-QDs needs further investigation.
To better understand and classify the functions of differentially expressed genes induced by AG-QDs, the enrichment of differentially expressed genes in each GO category including biological process, cellular component, and molecular function was calculated by hypergeometric distribution method. The top 10 significantly enriched terms of up-regulated and down-regulated genes in GO categories of biological process, cellular component, and molecular function were shown in Fig. 1 . For up-regulated genes in 24-h AG-QDs exposed cells, most significant enrichment terms of “response to virus” (GO:009615), “MHC class I protein complex” (GO:0042612) and “protein binding” (GO:0005515) were revealed in biological processes, cellular components, and molecular functions, respectively (Fig. 1A, Table S4). In addition, “endosome” (GO:0005768) and “early endosome” (GO:0005769) were the other enrichment terms in cellular components category. The up-regulation of the genes related “endosome” and “early endosome” suggested the activation of endocytosis, implying that AG-QDs could be taken into cells by endocytosis.
For the down-regulated genes, the top 10 enriched GO terms in the biological process were mainly involved DNA replication, chromosome segregation, cell division, and mitotic cell cycle (Fig. 1B, Table S5-1), which were related to cell-cycle regulation. Therein, the GO terms “DNA replication initiation” (GO:0006270) and “DNA replication” (GO:0006260) were involved in the G1/S (first gap/synthesis) phase of cell cycle. “Microtubule-based movement” (GO: 0007018), “mitotic chromosome condensation” (GO: 0007076), “chromosome segregation” (GO:0007059), “mitotic sister chromatid segregation” (GO: 0000070), “mitotic cell cycle” (GO: GO:0000278), and “cell division” (GO: 0051301) were involved in the G2/M (second gap/mitosis) phase of cell cycle. In top 10 cellular component terms of down-regulated genes, 7 terms were related to “cell-cycle” regulation, including “chromosome, centromeric region” (GO: 0000775),“kinetochore” (GO: 0000776), “kinesin complex” (GO: 0005871), “spindle” (GO: 0005871), “MCM complex” (GO: 0005871), “condensed chromosome kinetochore” (GO: 0000777), and “spindle pole” (GO: 0000922) (Fig. 1B, Table S5-2). These results confirmed that noncytotoxic dose of AG-QDs inhibited the expression of genes involved in the “cell cycle”-regulation in NR8383 cells.


### ANALYSIS OF “CELL-CYCLE” AND “PHAGOSOME” PATHWAYS BY KEGG ###



### “CELL CYCLE” PATHWAY ###

In the top 20 enriched KEGG pathways, “cell cycle” (rno04110) containing 44 differentially expressed genes was the representative pathway (Fig. S5, Table S6). In the “cell cycle” pathway, 7 genes were up-regulated, and 37 genes were down-regulated (Table S7). It was clear that down-regulated genes played a major role in the “cell cycle” pathway, which was accorded with the results of GO enrichment analysis that most of the down-regulated genes were involved in cell-cycle-related GO terms (Fig. 1B, Tables S5-1, S5-2). The mainly different-expressed genes in “cell cycle” pathway were then mapped into a regulatory network (Fig. 2 ) to analyze the regulative variation in response to AG-QDs exposure.
As cells proceed through the cell cycle, cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor genes (Cdkn1a, 2b) in G1 phase were up-regulated in NR8383 cells after AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) exposure for 24 h, which inhibited the expression of Cyclin D, E, A (Ccnd3, Ccne1, 2, and Ccna2) and cyclin dependent kinase 2 (Cdk2). The down-regulated Ccnd3, Ccne1, 2/Cdk2, and Ccna2/Cdk2 decreased the phosphorylation of retinoblastoma protein transcriptional repressor (Rb11) (Fig. 2). It was reported that the phosphorylated Rb by Cyclin/Cdk complexes could release E2f transcription factors which regulated the expression of numerous genes needed for DNA synthesis in S phase (Giacinti and Giordano, 2006; Ye et al., 2016). In NR8383 cells, the decrease of phosphorylated Rb11 and the down-regulated E2f1, 2, 3 indicated the inhibition of the E2f transcription factors upon AG-QDs exposure. Consequently, DNA synthesis and cell-cycle progression from G1 to S phase were suppressed.
To initiate DNA replication in S phase, mini-chromosome maintenance (MCM) complex combined with origin recognition complex (ORC) and cell division cycle 6 (Cdc6) are firstly loaded at replication origins of DNA during G1 phase (Fernández-Cid et al., 2013), and then been activated upon binding to cell division cycle 45 (Cdc45) in S phase (Yeeles et al., 2015). The activated MCM complex could separate the DNA double-strand into single-strand to prepare for DNA replication (Masai et al., 2006). In our study, the expression of genes (Mcm2-7, Orc1, 6, Cdc6, and Cdc45) were down-regulated after AG-QDs exposure, implying the inhibition on MCM activation and DNA despiralization in S phase progression. In good agreement with our findings, it was reported that the down-regulation of MCMs and Cdc6 induced the loss of proliferative capacity in NIH/3T3 cells (Kingsbury et al., 2005); additionally, the lack of Cdc45 blocked initiation of DNA replication (Takisawa et al., 2000).
In S/G2 phase, the up-regulated Gadd45, encoding growth arrest and DNA-damage-inducible protein, inhibited the expression of Cdk1 which could disturb the progression of cells at S-phase (Fig. 2) (Wu et al., 2013). Cell cycle entry into G2/M phase was precisely signaled by Cdc25B,C, a phosphatase that removed inhibitory phosphates from Cdk1 (Nilsson and Hoffmann, 2000). The down-regulation of Cdc25B,C decreased the phosphatase activity, suggesting the delay of entering into M phase. This was consistent with the previous studies that the down-regulation of Cdk1 and Cdc25C blocked the transition of the cell cycle from G2 phase to M phase (Giono et al., 2017; Gong et al., 2015).
The gene families of Bub (mitotic checkpoint kinase), Mad (mitotic arrest deficient), and Mps (monopolar spindle) in M phase play key roles in ensuring correct chromosome segregation (Medina-Redondo and Meraldi, 2009; Kops et al., 2005). The expression of Bub1, BubR1, Mad2, and Mps1 in NR8383 cells were inhibited after AG-QDs exposure (Fig. 2), which could result in chromosomal instability and proliferative inhibition. Kops et al. (2005) also reported that BubR1 and Mad2 silencing caused a decrease in cell proliferation in cervical tumor cells and glioblastoma multiform cells. The down-regulated gene Cdc20 in NR8383 cells could disturb sister-chromatid separation and induce M phase arrest (Manchado et al., 2010; Li et al., 2007).


### “PHAGOSOME” PATHWAY ###

“Phagosome” pathway (rno04145) was also significant enrichment in KEGG analysis, which contained 26 up-regulated genes and 15 down-regulated genes (Table 1 ). Cd14, encoding the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) receptor CD14, was shown to mediate phagocytosis of macrophages (Liu et al., 2005). After AG-QDs exposure for 24 h, the expression of Cd14 was up-regulated, which could promote the phagocytic activity of macrophages. Toll-like receptors (TLRs), as transmembrane receptors, are predominantly expressed in macrophages (Fang et al., 2014). TLRs often interact with co-receptors such as CD14 to transduce phagocytic signals in response to solid particles (Reed-Geaghan et al., 2009). In NR8383 cells, Tlr2 and Tlr4 were both up-regulated expression upon AG-QDs exposure, suggesting the stimulation of phagocytic activity. These results were in good agreement with that TiO2 NPs caused an increase expression of Tlr genes which mediated the uptake of TiO2 NPs via phagocytosis in sea urchin immune cell (Pinsino et al., 2015).


### CELLULAR RESPONSES: CELL CYCLE ARREST AND AG-QDS INTERNALIZATION ###



### CELL CYCLE ARREST ###

Since RNA-Seq analysis suggested that the genes affected by AG-QDs primarily participated in cell cycle regulation, we therefore evaluated the cell cycle in NR8383 cells after AG-QDs exposure (Fig. 3 ). The proportion of cells in G1 phase was significantly decreased from 70.5% to 36.0% upon AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) exposure for 24 h. The percentages of cells in S and G2/M phase were increased from 18.5% to 22.9%, and 11.0% to 40.0%, respectively. Remarkably, noncytotoxic dose of AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) specifically impaired cell cycle progression, with retention of the cells in S and G2/M phase. With this functional assay, we thus were able to corroborate that the down-regulation of cell-cycle-related genes in NR8383 cells could result in the cell cycle arrest (Figs. 1, 2). This finding was in good agreement with the study of Feliu et al. (2014), in which the cell-cycle-related genes were down-regulated and cell cycle was arrested when primary human bronchial epithelial cells were exposed to PAMAM dendrimers.


### AG-QDS INTERNALIZATION ###

The internalization of AG-QDs in NR8383 cells was examined using FCM (Fig. 4 ). After AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) exposure for 24 h, the SSC signals of cells were enhanced, and the intensity of SSC was significantly higher than that in unexposed cells. The intensity of SSC in FCM is a parameter reflecting the inner density of each cell (Park et al., 2017). When NPs entered the cells, the intracellular density increased, resulting in the increase of the SSC intensity (Kumar et al., 2011). Therefore, the increase of the SSC intensity in NR8383 cells indicated that AG-QDs were effectively taken into NR8383 cells. These results were supported by Zhao and Ibuki (2015) that the increased SSC intensity indicated the internalization of Ag NPs in HaCaT and A549 cells. Endocytosis, especially phagocytosis was the primarily process by macrophages to engulf solid particles (Seo et al., 2017). After pre-treated with endocytosis and phagocytosis inhibitors (NaN3 and cytochalasin D), the SSC signals and intensity in AG-QDs exposed cells were significantly decreased, suggesting that most of AG-QDs were internalized in cells by energy-dependent endocytosis and phagocytosis. The internalization pathways of AG-QDs could be regulated by the increased expression of the genes which related with “endosome” and “early endosome” in GO analysis and “phagosome” in KEGG pathways (Fig. 1A and Fig. S5).


### CELLULAR RECOVERY FROM AG-QDS-INDUCED STRESS ###

To determine whether AG-QDs could cause permanent damage to NR8383 cells, the recovery of cell cycle was examined when AG-QDs exposed cells were re-incubated with fresh cell medium without AG-QDs (Fig. S6). After re-incubation in fresh medium for 48 and 96 h, the proportion of NR8383 cells in G1 phase increased significantly as compared to the cells after AG-QDs exposure. However, this proportion was still lower than that of non-exposed cells, indicating that DNA pre-synthesis phase gradually recovered but did not return to the normal level. For S phase, the proportion of cells after re-incubation in fresh medium was significantly increased. Asharani et al. (2010) reported that IMR-90 cells and U251 cells accumulated in the S phase could be a result of successful DNA repair and subsequent recovery from Pt NP-induced stress. For G2/M phase, the proportion of cells decreased significantly with increasing re-incubation time. In addition, the proportions of cells in G2/M phase after re-incubation for 96 h has insignificant change with non-exposure cells, indicating that G2/M arrest was recovered and the cellular mitosis returned to normal level after removing AG-QDs for 96 h. Cosentino et al. (2013) found that the decrease of cell number in G2/M arrest could increase the regenerative capacity of actively cycling cells, and promoted the recovery of kidney injury in mice. Taken together, the cell cycle arrest induced by AG-QDs could gradually recover after removing AG-QDs and re-incubation in fresh medium.
Cellular excretion which was deemed to be responsible for cellular detoxification was further detected by FCM (Fig. S7A, S7B). After re-incubation in fresh medium for 48 h, the SSC intensity in cells was reduced by 25.3%, suggesting that AG-QDs were exported by NR8383 cells. There was no significant difference in the SSC intensity of cells between 48- and 96-h re-incubation (29.3%), suggesting that cellular excretion mainly occurred in <48 h. Moreover, the fluorescence of AG-QDs in the re-incubation medium was clearly detected (Fig. S7C), confirming the excretion of internalization AG-QDs. The observed excretion phenomenon could result in the recovery of NPs-induced injured cells (Wang et al., 2012; Mironava et al., 2014).
To identify whether the recovery of cell cycle was associated with the expression of DNA repair-related genes, 6 genes (Rad51, Brca2, Atm, Msh2, Rpa2, and Neil1) related to DNA repair were investigated using qRT-PCR. As shown in Fig. 5 , AG-QDs significantly decreased the expression of these 6 genes in NR8383 cells after 24-h exposure. However, after re-incubation in the fresh medium without AG-QDs, the expression of Rad51 was up-regulated (Fig. 5A). It was reported that Rad51 played a key role in mediating DNA strand–exchange, leading to homologous recombination (Godin et al., 2016). The up-regulation of Rad51 in the present work could promote DNA recombination and increase the resistance to the disruption of cell cycle (Klein, 2008). Brca2, is known to mediate Rad51 protein binding to double-strand break sites and promote DNA recombination (Godin et al., 2016). The up-regulation of Brca2 in recovered cells (Fig. 5B), indicated that the AG-QDs-induced DNA damage could be repaired via homologous recombination to promote DNA replication in S phase. Atm, an important DNA damage signal, was also up-regulated in the recovered cells (Fig. 5C). It was reported that active Atm could delay G1 phase to allow more time for DNA repair (Khoronenkova and Dianov, 2015), this supports our finding that the percentage of G1 increased as a function of re-incubation time (Fig. S6). Msh2, encoding DNA mismatch repair protein, could correct base-base mismatches and insertion/deletion loops which generated during DNA replication and recombination (Mjelle et al., 2015). In the recovered cells, Msh2 was not activated after re-incubation in the fresh medium (Fig. 5D), suggesting that DNA mismatch repair regulated by Msh2 was independent on the recovery of cell cycle. For Rpa2 and Neil1, the expressions after re-incubation in the fresh medium were both down-regulated (Fig. 5E, F). The base-excision repair proteins Rpa2 and Neil1 were required for DNA replication initiation, as well as for damage repair and recombination (Olson et al., 2006). It was speculated that the promoted DNA replication in S phase (Fig. S6) was not stimulated by the base-excision repair proteins such as Rpa2 and Neil1.


### EFFECT OF ROS ON GENOTOXICITY ###

Oxidative stress is one of the most common mechanisms leading to the change of genes expression via the transcription factors (Tabish et al., 2017; Tabish et al., 2018; and Wang et al., 2015), and modulation of inflammation/apoptosis via signal transduction (Li et al., 2018). In the present work, we observed that AG-QDs at 100 μg/mL caused an increase in ROS generation in NR8383 cells (Fig. S8). ROS generation could cause DNA-chains breaks (Hashemi et al., 2016). In addition, the genes relate to “double-strand break repair via homologous recombination” (GO:0000724) were down-regulated expression (Table S5-1) after AG-QDs exposure, which could inhibit the repair of DNA double-strand breaks (Thyme and Schier, 2016). After re-incubation with fresh culture medium without AG-QDs, the ROS level in NR8383 cells was decreased, with insignificant change compared to unexposed cells (Fig. S8). The reduced ROS level may contribute to the cellular recovery from AG-QDs-induced stress. Furthermore, ROS generated by macrophages could also cause toxicity to co-cultured cells, such as monocytes. Ponath and Kaina (Ponath and Kaina, 2017) found that in macrophages-monocytes co-cultivation system, ROS generated by macrophages caused the oxidative DNA damage, thus accumulating DNA single-strand breaks, increasing apoptosis and even death to monocytes.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

In this study, AG-QDs at 100 μg/mL were capable of triggering genotoxicity in cells. 2898 differentially expressed genes were identified, including 1335 up-regulated and 1563 down-regulated genes. Among these, the cell-cycle-related genes were significantly down-regulated expression. Further results showed that AG-QDs (100 μg/mL) exposure induced cell cycle arrest, thus corroborating the GO and KEGG analysis–based predictions. In addition, endocytosis and phagocytosis were identified the main pathways for the AG-QDs internalization, which correlated well with the activation of the genes related with “endocytosis” and “phagocytosis”. After removing AG-QDs and re-incubating the cells in fresh medium, cell cycle arrest was relieved in NR8383 cells. Internalized AG-QDs were exported from NR8383 cells after a 96-h re-incubation period, and DNA-repair-related genes (Rad51, Brca2, and Atm) were activated, both mechanisms could contribute to the cellular recovery. These findings were helpful to better understanding the transcriptional response and the long-term consequences of organisms upon AG-QDs exposure in the environment.
