### ABSTRACT ###

Typically, sialic acids (SA) with a nine-carbon backbone are found at the glycan chain termini on the cell membranes, which play crucial roles in various physiological and pathological processes. The expression level of SA in the blood serum has been reported to correlate with various disease states among cancer. In this study, a novel approach for preparing fluorescent boronic-acid-modified carbon dots (C-dots) for the detection of SA was developed. The functionalized C-dots were synthesized by a facile, one-step hydrothermal method using 3-pyridineboronic acid as the sole carbon source. The added SA selectively recognized the C-dots, leading to the fluorescence quenching of the C-dots in a linear range of 80–4000 μM with a detection limit of 54 μM. The as-developed boronic-acid nanoprobe was successfully applied for the detection of SA in human serum samples with satisfactory results. In addition, this method afforded results within 4 min. Compared to other methods, this new proposed approach was simpler and exhibited excellent sensitivity and selectivity, demonstrating immense potential as an alternative for SA detection.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Glycans, which constitute the external cell surface, play key roles in various biological processes including immune response, cell–cell communication, and microbial infection [1,2]. As one of the key constituents in these glycans, sialic acids (SA) are typically found at the termini of several classes of cell surfaces and secreted glycan molecules. SA have attracted immense attention due to their crucial roles in various physiological and pathological processes [3,4]. Increasing evidence has revealed that the abnormal expression and distribution of SA on the cell surface or in body fluids are closely associated with various diseases, including cancer and diabetes, as well as cardiovascular and neurological diseases [4–6]. In addition, SA have been regarded as a tumor-associated biomarker as their overexpression in blood serum can reveal a malfunction of several organs of the human body and also indicate an early stage of various cancers or cardiovascular disease [6,7]. Therefore, the detection of SA is of significance in clinical diagnostics and therapeutics.
By the combination of excellent optical properties and versatile surface functionalization, conjugated polymers, noble metal nanoparticles (NPs), or semiconductor-quantum-dot-based colorimetric or fluorescent assays have been designed and successfully applied for SA detection [8–12]. Typically, these nanosensors include at least two steps, i.e., NP synthesis and particle surface functionalization, for the introduction of recognition sites, respectively. Conjunction is often indispensable for effective functionalization. However, conjunction processes are time-consuming, often tending to lead to the aggregation of the reacted NPs [13] as colloidal NPs are a thermodynamically unstable dispersed system. A considerably simpler, more rapid technique involves the use of a biosensor [14,15]. Although a biosensor exhibits high selectivity and does not require tedious NP modification, two enzymes are required, which incurs additional cost. In addition, enzymes activities are easily affected by physical and chemical changes [16]. Thus, it is crucial to develop new methods that simultaneously prevent surface modification and enzymes towards the development of SA detection.
Boronic acids can form reversible covalent interactions with diol-moiety-bearing compounds via reversible esterification [17,18]. Thus, boronic acids can bind to the triol side chains of SA; this impressive result endows boronic acid with immense potential as a novel receptor for SA detection.
Interest in C-dots has increased in various fields due to their chemical inertness, low toxicity, simple synthesis, high water solubility, inexpensive raw materials, and high photostability [19,20]. Several studies have reported analytical applications of C-dots similar to semiconductor quantum dots. However, typically, the prepared C-dots exhibit low toxicity and more stability toward the chemical and physical environment changes such as pH, ionic strength, or even the presence of several chemical species. In this study, a simple, sensitive nonenzymatic strategy for SA detection with carbon dots was described using 3-pyridylboronic acid as the sole carbon source to prepare C-dots by a one-step hydrothermal method ( Scheme 1). The as-prepared C-dots were directly functionalized by boronic acids during particle synthesis, with no requirement for additional surface modification for the introduction of binding sites. The functionalized C-dots specifically recognized SA, and its fluorescence intensity was quenched by SA. The developed sensor was extremely selective for SA rather than its analogs and samples matrixes.


### METHODS AND MATERIALS ###



### INSTRUMENTS ###

UV–vis and fluorescence spectra were recorded on a Cary 60 UV–vis spectrometer (Agilent Technologies Inc., USA) and an F-2700 spectrofluorophotometer (HITACHI Co., Ltd., Japan), respectively. Zeta potentials were measured on a Zetasizer (Malvern Co., UK) at room temperature. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were recorded on a Hitachi H8100 electron microscope at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV without staining. FTIR spectra were recorded using KBr pellets on a Nicolet Avatar 360 FTIR spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., USA) at 4000–400 cm−1. XPS profiles were recorded on an ESCALAB 250 spectrometer by using a monochromatic X-ray source with Al Kα excitation (1486.6 eV). All pH values were recorded on a PHS-3C pH-Meter (INESA Scientific Inc., China).


### CHEMICALS ###

N-Acetylneuraminic acid and 3-pyridylboronic acid were purchased from Aladdin Chemistry Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Glucose, galactose, and mannose were purchased from Tianjin Guangfu Chemical Company (China). Analytical-grade chemicals were used without further purification. Deionized (DI) water obtained from a Millipore purification device (18.2 MΩ cm) was used for syntheses and analyses.


### SYNTHESIS OF C-DOTS FROM 3-PYRIDYLBORONIC ACID ###

C-dots were synthesized by a hydrothermal method according to a previous reported study [20]. First, 0.2 g of 3-pyridylboronic acid was dissolved in 20 mL of DI water, followed by bubbling N2 for 1 h to remove dissolved O2. Second, the solution was transferred into a Teflon-lined reactor (20 mL) and heated at 160 °C for 8 h. C-dots were collected by the removal of large dots via centrifugation (10,000 rpm), followed by dialysis against ultrapure water using a dialysis membrane (molecular weight cut-off = 1000) for 24 h to remove the excess precursors and resulting small molecules. The as-prepared C-dots solution was maintained at 4 °C for further characterization and use.


### DETERMINATION OF SA ###

Typical detection measurements were carried out as follows: First, 40 μL of a C-dot solution and 160 μL of PBS buffer (pH = 5.5, 0.01 M) were added into a 1-mL centrifuge tube. Second, different amounts of SA were added, and the mixtures were diluted to 400 μL with water. After the mixtures were incubated for 4 min at room temperature, their fluorescence emission spectra were recorded at an excitation wavelength of 320 nm.


### REAL SAMPLE ASSAY ###

The application of SA detection in actual samples was carried out using human serum as the samples. In brief, human serum samples were collected after filtration through a 0.22-μm membrane to remove the large particles before use. Next, the purified human serum samples (994.44 μL) were mixed with 5.56 μL of concentrated sulfuric acid to release SA from the glycoproteins. Next, the mixture was stored at 80 °C for 60 min before being neutralized with 200 μL of a 0.50 M NaOH solution prior to analysis [21]. In consideration of the dilution by digestion, the samples were further diluted to 20-fold with water before analysis. For the recovery experiment, the serum samples were diluted 20-fold with water for detection.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### SYNTHESIS AND CHARACTERIZATION OF BORONIC-ACID-FUNCTIONALIZED C-DOTS ###

In this study, 3-pyridylboronic acid was used as the sole carbon source for preparing water-soluble C-dots by a green, one-step hydrothermal method (Scheme 1). The synthesis conditions including hydrothermal temperature and reaction time were changed to examine their effects on the optical property of the C-dots. Carbonization conditions seriously affected the fluorescence intensity of C-dots (Fig. S1). In summary, 0.2 g of 3-pyridylboronic acid in 20 mL of DI water at a reaction temperature of 160 °C for 8 h is selected as the optimum condition for synthesizing C-dots.
TEM images of the as-prepared C-dots were recorded to observe their morphology. Fig. 1a shows the TEM image of the C-dots: Monodisperse, uniform C-dots with an average size of 3.5 nm were observed. To confirm the presence of various functional groups in C-dots, FTIR spectra were recorded (Fig. 1b). Distinct characteristic peaks were observed at 3218 cm−1, 1468 cm−1, 1384 cm−1, and 1196 cm−1 corresponded to the O–H stretching vibration, C–N stretching vibration, B–O stretching vibration, and B–O–H bending vibration, respectively [20,22,23]. Results revealed that C-dots are successfully functionalized boronic acid groups that are transferred from 3-pyridylboronic acid molecules to the C-dot surfaces during particle formation. XPS analysis of C-dots was carried out to further identify the elemental composition, chemical bonds, and surface functional groups. The XPS spectrum revealed the presence of four elements in the C-dots, 37.59% of C; 8.52% of N; 31.79% of O; and 22.08% of B, respectively ( Fig. 2a). The content of oxygen (31.79%) in the C-dots was greater than that in the 3-pyridylboronic acid molecules (27.37%), indicating that water molecules might participate in the C-dot synthesis [24]. Three distinct peaks were observed in the high-resolution XPS spectrum of C 1s (Fig. 2b). The positions of the peaks at 286.2 eV and 284.8 eV confirmed C-O/C-N and C˭C [25]. The peak observed at 283.9 eV corresponded to the C–B functional groups [22]. Pyridinic N (398.9 eV), pyrrolic N (400.9 eV), amino N (400.1 eV), and graphitic N (402.1 eV) (Fig. 2c) were observed in the N 1 s spectrum, which was consistent with that reported previously [26]. The B 1 s spectrum of C-dots (Fig. 2d) decomposed into three peaks from B–C (191.7 eV), B–O (192.8 eV), and B2O3 (193.7 eV), respectively. These results clearly revealed that the as-prepared C-dots bear surface boronic acid groups.
UV–vis absorption and fluorescence spectra were recorded to further examine the optical properties of the as-prepared C-dots. Absorption bands characteristic of C-dots were observed at 255 nm, corresponding to π–π* transition of the aromatic C˭C bond (Fig. S2) [26]. Fig. S3 shows the fluorescence spectra of the C-dot dispersion at various excitation wavelengths from 280 nm to 330 nm. With the increase in the excitation wavelength from 280 to 330 nm, the emission peak position shifted to higher wavelengths accompanied by the decrease in the fluorescence intensity, which is a phenomenon typically observed in carbon-based quantum dots [24]. Furthermore, its fluorescence stability under different conditions was investigated (Fig. S4). First, the fluorescence intensities of C-dots did not exhibit clear changes from pH 5.5–8.0. Second, the fluorescence intensity was constant up to an ionic strength of 1.0 mol L−1 NaCl, indicative of the good salt tolerance of the C-dots. All of the results manifested that C-dots demonstrate potential applications in sophisticated samples.


### SENSING PRINCIPLE ###

In the presence of SA, the fluorescence of the C-dots gradually decreased, and ~80% of the fluorescence at 390 nm was quenched. Theoretically, the photoluminescence of the C-dots corresponds to the energy trapping of the surface organic groups [27]. For the C-dots, by functionalization with boronic acid molecules, their surface sites facilitate the trapping of photoinduced electrons and holes. The introduced SA recombined with the ground state of the boronic acid molecules on the C-dot surface, affording five-membered cyclic complexes; hence, the fluorescence intensity of the C-dots decreases because of the exciton-energy-transfer-based fluorescence responses [9]. A reasonably good fit can be achieved using two-exponential decays, and the lifetimes of the C-dots do not exhibit any discernible change with the addition of SA ( Fig. 3). Moreover, the absorption spectra apparently changed with the addition of SA (Fig. S2). Thus, the above analysis revealed that the system exhibits static quenching [28]. The Stern–Volmer [29] plots of In(F0/F) was nearly linear ( Fig. 4b), and the Stern–Volmer constant (K sv) derived from the plot was ~2.96 × 102 M−1.


### OPTIMIZATION FOR SA DETECTION ###

Before quantitative analysis of SA using the proposed method, the detection conditions, including system pH, NaCl concentration, and reaction time, were optimized.
pH is a key factor in this system due to its effect on the boronic acid functional groups. The glycerol tail of SA can be recognized by the uncharged trigonal form of boronic acid [30,31]. For boronic acid to be in this form, the pH was required to be less than 6.0. With the decrease in the pH from 8.0 to 6.0, the quenching efficiencies gradually increased and reached a plateau at pH < 6.0 (Fig. S5a); this result is in agreement with that reported previously [8]. Thus, a pH of 5.5 is selected as the optimum pH for the SA assay.
The zeta potential of C-dots (Fig. S6) was − 8.53 mV, indicative of the formation of negatively charged C-dots. The SA carboxyl group was deprotonated at a pH greater than 2.6 (pKa of SA). The C-dots should be repelled by the negatively charged SA due to electrostatic repulsion. On the other hand, the boronic-acid-group-containing C-dots can bind to SA through reversible esterification [10,32]. Therefore, the addition of salts can screen the electrostatic repulsion and increase the proximity between SA and the C-dot surface, leading to the enhanced signal-to-noise ratio of this sensor. In the presence of 0.05 M Na+, the quenching efficiency was 2.1 (Fig. S5b). At higher Na+ concentrations, the quenching efficiencies were progressively improved. The maximum quenching efficiency was 2.5 with a 0.2 M NaCl solution. Hence, a 0.2 M NaCl solution is used in subsequent experiments.
In addition, the effect of reaction time was examined: With the increase in the reaction time to until 4 min, the fluorescence intensity of the C-dots decreased, which subsequently remained nearly constant within 10 min (Fig. S5c). Thus, a reaction time of 4 min is used for subsequent experiments.


### DETECTION OF SA ###

Under the optimal conditions, the sensing system for the quantitative detection of SA was examined. With the increase in the SA concentration, the fluorescence intensity of C-dots was quenched (Fig. 4a). A good linear relationship of 80–4000 μM, with a limit of detection of 54 μM (S/N = 3, Fig. 4b), was observed. Compared to previously reported boronic-acid-based colorimetric or fluorescent methods, the method developed herein afforded a wide linear range and LOD (Table S1). In addition, the results obtained for the developed sensor were obtained within 4 min; this time was at least 10-fold shorter than the above-mentioned fluorescent and colorimetric sensors. To evaluate the selectivity of developed sensor to SA, the effects of different monosaccharides, including glucose (Glu), mannose (Man), and galactose (Gal) on the fluorescence emission of the C-dots were examined. Under the same conditions, in sharp contrast to SA, other three sugars exhibited almost no effect on the spectra of the C-dots ( Fig. 5). This result indicated that the proposed sensor exhibits high selectivity for SA, probably because this sensor was operated at pH 5.5 at which trigonal boronic acid was present [30], which was available for binding with the triol side chains of SA, whereas the binding with other sugars required tetrahedral boronate, which was only present at a higher pH.


### DETECTION OF SA IN HUMAN SERUM SAMPLES ###

To investigate the analytical reliability and potential applications of the proposed method, the boronic acid C-dot nanoprobe was applied to the detection of SA in human serum samples. According to the reported assays, the total levels of SA in human serum ranged from 1.6 to 2.9 mM. The result obtained from detection was ~2.04 mM, which coincided with that obtained from the assay [33]. The recoveries were calculated from three concentrations (i.e., final concentrations of 0.40, 0.80, 2.0 mM) of the three spiked human serum samples (diluted 20-fold with PBS). Table 1 summarizes the results. The recovery ranged from 92.5 to 102, indicative of the practicability of the proposed sensing platform.


### CONCLUSIONS ###

In summary, a new boronic-acid-functionalized C-dot-based fluorescence sensor for the detection of SA was described. Compared to the existing method, the proposed strategy did not require any additional surface modification, which is simpler, more immediate, and effective. In addition, quantitative results were obtained within 4 min; this time was more rapid than other fluorescent SA sensors. In addition, this sensor was successfully for the detection of SA in human serum samples with satisfactory results. Thus, our newly developed strategy demonstrates potential to be an alternative analytical tool for the examination of SA-related diseases with no requirement for any special instrument.
