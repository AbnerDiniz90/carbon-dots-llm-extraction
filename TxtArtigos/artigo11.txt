### ABSTRACT ###

Graphene and derivatives have been considered as useful additives to improve the electrochemical property of the cathodes in lithium ion batteries. To get insight into the effects of graphene sheets on the electrochemical properties of LiCoO2, in the work, LiCoO2 coated with graphene sheets with micrometer sized lateral sizes, porous graphene sheets, and graphene quantum dots are prepared, and their electrochemical properties are studied systematically. It is demonstrated that the capacity, rate capability and charge/discharge cycling stability of LiCoO2 can be improved, but depends on the lateral size and the basal plane morphology of the graphene sheets. In contrast, the graphene sheets with lateral sizes in micrometer can more efficiently improve the overall electrochemical performances.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Since it was recognized as a cathode material by Goodenough in 1980, the layered-structure LiCoO2 (LCO) has been widely used in the commercialized lithium ion batteries (LIBs) [1,2]. To date, in comparing to other cathode materials such as LiFePO4 [3,4] and Li(M1-x-yNixCoy)O2 (M: Co, Mn, Al) [5–7] which are seen also significant developments, LiCoO2 is still a dominant cathode material in the LIBs used for portable electronic devices, benefitting from its superior volume energy density (1.7–2.9 g cm−3), high working potential (∼3.8 V vs. Li+/Li) and simple production process [8–10]. However, the poor Li+ diffusion coefficient, which lead to relatively poor rate capability, remain to be improved [10–12]. Moreover, LiCoO2 suffers from irreversible phase transformation from hexagonal to monoclinic phase and the structure collapse due to the extraction of Li-ions from LiCoO2 (LiCoO2⇄Li1-xCoO2+xLi+, x ≥ 0.5) during charge process, which decrease its practical capacity to only half of the theoretical value (∼275 mAh g−1) [11,13]. Therefore, intensive efforts have been devoted to enhancing the Li+ diffusion rate and the structure stability of LiCoO2 in recent years including the metal elements doping [14–18], and surface coating [19–24]. For surface coating, the carbonaceous materials, such as carbon-black, carbon nanotubes, are widely used due to its excellent electrical conductivity, superior chemical, electrochemical stability [25] and structural flexibility [26–29]. Actually, the conductive carbonaceous material coating on cathode materials cannot only improve the electrochemical performance, but can also protect electrodes from the electrolyte [30].
Graphene is one of the most promising carbonaceous materials to improve the overall properties of the electrode materials used in LIBs owing to its unique single atomic layered morphology, and the effective electron and ion conductivities [31,32]. In addition, graphene can promote the formation of the well-defined and stable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer on the electrodes [33], which may afford long term lithium-ion battery stability and the morphology of SEI is related to the shape of carbon materials [34].It was reported that by coating graphene on LiCoO2 its electrochemical performances including cycling stability, rate capability can be improved somehow [11,35]. In this work, to get insight the effects of the lateral sizes and basal plane structure of graphene sheets on the electrochemical properties of LiCoO2, the LiCoO2 coated by chemically reduced graphene oxide sheets with micrometer lateral sizes (mRGO), porous chemically reduced graphene oxide sheets (pRGO), and graphene quantum dots (GQDs) are prepared. It is demonstrated that the LiCoO2/mRGO composite show better electrochemical performances as cathode than bare LiCoO2, LiCoO2/pRGO, and LiCoO2/GQDs, and the underneath mechanisms are discussed.


### EXPERIMENTAL SECTION ###



### MATERIALS ###

The pure LiCoO2 powder was provided by Zhongxing Telecommunication Equipment Corporation (ZTE), China. mRGO used in this work was prepared through modified Hummers method that was described in our previous work [36]. pRGO and GQDs were fabricated through Photo-Fenton reactions of GO under different illumination times [37].


### SYNTHESIS OF LICOO2/MRGO, LICOO2/PRGO, LICOO2/GQDS ###

In a typical experiment, LiCoO2 particles, 2 g, was dispersed into 200 mL, the 0.5 wt%, of mRGO, pRGO or GQDs aqueous suspension, respectively. The mixture was stirred vigorously for 20 min at 23 °C. The solid product was separated through filtration, and dried at 110 °C for 12 h in vacuum oven. The final products were named as LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, LiCoO2/GQDs.


### CHARACTERIZATIONS OF THE MATERIALS ###

AFM images of mRGO, pRGO and GQDs were acquired on a Multimode nanoscope V AFM (Veeco, USA). To prepare the sample for AFM measurements, the corresponding aqueous suspensions of mRGO, pRGO, and GQDs were dispersed homogeneously by ultrasonic machine for 30 min, and then a droplet of them was deposited on a clean mica and evaporated at 60 °C in a drying oven. Raman spectra recorded using a Senterra R200-L dispersive Raman microscope (Bruker, Germany). Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were acquired on an Equinox 55 FT-IR spectrometer (Bruker, Germany). The morphology of composite materials was examined using Ultra 55 field-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM) (Zeiss, Germany). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images and high resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images were obtained on a Talos F200X field emission transmission electron microscope (FEI, USA) operated at 200 kV.


### ELECTROCHEMICAL TEST ###

The electrochemical properties and kinetic properties of LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs were tested in coin cells. Briefly, the active materials, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) binder were mixed with a ratio of 8:1:1 in weight, and the mixture was dispersed into N-methylpyrrolidone (NMP) solvent to form homogeneous slurry. The slurry was coated uniformly on an Al foil and then dried at 110 °C for 12 h in the vacuum oven. The coin cells (2025) were assembled in a glove box using a lithium foil as anode, a Celgard 2325 membrane as a separator, and the 1 M LiPF6 dissolved in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate/ethyl methyl carbonate (4:3:3, in volume) as the electrolyte.
Galvanostatic charge–discharge cycles were tested on LAND-CT2001A electrochemical workstation (Wuhan, China) at various C-rates between 2.5 and 4.6 V versus Li+/Li at 23 °C. The potential static step charge measurement was performed by charging at +500 mV potential steps for 1000 s, and the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was obtained on the coin cell by applying a perturbation voltage of 5 mV in a frequency range of 0.1 Hz–100 KHz using an electrochemical working station (PGSTAT 302N) (Metrohm, Switzerland) at 23 °C.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###

The size and thickness of the mRGO sheets, pRGO, and GQDs used in the work were characterized by AFM imaging. As shown in Fig. 1 a, the lateral dimensions of mRGO sheets range in micrometer and the thickness is about 0.85 nm, revealing the single atomic layer motif [38]. In contrast, the pRGO, obtained through photo-Fenton reaction of GO, assume similar thickness, but smaller lateral sizes, and more pronouncedly there are a number of the nanopores can be observed clearly on their basal planes (Fig. 1b). The average size of GQDs is less than 50 nm in diameter (Fig. 1c) that is similar to the one we reported previously [39].
The morphologies of the LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, LiCoO2/GQDs were further characterized by FE-SEM, TEM and HRTEM, and the results are shown in Fig. 2 . As depicted in Fig. 2a and e, the raw LiCoO2 particles show even clean smooth surface and their sizes of 1–2 μm. In comparison, in LiCoO2/mRGO, the mRGO not only clad on LiCoO2, but also bridge LiCoO2 particles together to form 3D conductive network, Fig. 2b and f. The surface morphology of LiCoO2/pRGO is displayed in Fig. 2c and g, in which the LiCoO2 surfaces are covered with pRGO sheets, nanopores can be observed. The HRTEM images of the LiCoO2/mRGO and LiCoO2/pRGO (Fig. 2j and k) show clear the lattice fringes of (110) plane for mRGO with spacing values of 2.12 Å. From Fig. 2d and h, it can be seen that GQDs adhere to the LiCoO2 surface and some of them perhaps aggregate together. The HRTEM image of Fig. 2l shows the lattice fringes with spacing values of 2.12 Å, which correspond to the (110) plane of the GQDs in LiCoO2/GQDs.
Fig. 3 depicts the FT-IR spectra of LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs after being dried at 110 °C in vacuum oven for 12 h. The C–O (alkoxy) stretching peak at 1052 cm−1, the CO stretching vibration peak at 1725 cm−1, the CC/C-C conjugated system peak at 1620 cm−1, and the vibration and deformation peaks of O–H groups at 3398 cm−1 and 1410 cm−1, respectively [36,38],can be observed from mRGO, pRGO, GQDs and their corresponding composites LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs which indicate dramatically, and some of them disappeared entirely. These observations demonstrated that most oxygen functionalities in the GO were reduced, which also confirmed unambiguously the formation of those composites.
Fig. 4 a shows the discharge performances of coin cells with LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, LiCoO2/GQDs as cathodes measured at various discharge rates. Obviously, all three composites exhibit higher specific capacity and better rate capability than the bare LiCoO2, which demonstrate that the graphene with different lateral size and basal plane morphologies could enhance the electrochemical properties of LiCoO2. The initial discharge capacities of the cells with LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, LiCoO2/GQDs cathodes can reach to 228, 225.1, 184 mAh g−1 at 0.2 C, and decrease to 106, 84.8, 43.3 mAh g−1 at 5 C, but get back to 208.7, 185.5, 140.5 mAh g−1 once the discharge rate return to 0.2 C. The reason is that once the charge/discharge rate is of 5 C, the charge/discharge time is quite short, and the inherent electron/ion conductivities of the LiCoO2 cathode are both relatively low, thus, there is no enough Li ions can be insert into/extract from the LiCoO2, and its apparent capacity is close to zero. When the charge/discharge rate is decreased to 0.2 C, the capacity can be recovered partially due might to the structure of LiCoO2 has been destroyed somehow. These data reveal in general that even the lateral sizes of mRGO and GQDs, and the basal plane morphologies of mRGO and pRGO are different, but all they can improve somehow the electrochemical properties of LiCoO2. More specifically, it can be found that although both cells with LiCoO2/mRGO and LiCoO2/pRGO as cathodes have almost the same specific capacity at the beginning, the one of the LiCoO2/pRGO decrease quicker than that of LiCoO2/mRGO with the discharge rate increasing, which may raise from the basal plane difference between the mRGO and pRGO, precisely, the nanopores on basal planes of pRGO most probably decrease the electron and/or ion conductivity. The reason might be that the nanopores in pRGO are defects intrinsically and could destroy the π-π structure of mRGO, which affect the overall physical/chemical properties of pRGO, especially the electron and ion conductivity [40–42]. Notably, LiCoO2/mRGO shows better electrochemical performance over the LiCoO2/GQDs demonstrating the lateral size the graphene is also crucial. The reason is that the mRGO can cover larger portion of the surfaces of LiCoO2 particles and also form network bridges among them that can enhance the electron and Li+ diffusion on the LiCoO2 surface and also among the particles. This also affects their cycling stability. As shown in Fig. 4b, after 500 charge/discharge cycles, the cell using LiCoO2/mRGO cathode retains higher specific capacitance than those of LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs (Table 1 ). By comparing the SEM images before and after the charge/discharge cycle, a thin homogeneous CEI (cathode electrolyte interphase) film seems formed on the LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO [34,43]. Clearly, both mRGO and pRGO can promote the CEI film formation on LiCoO2 that may protect its pulverization, and thereby enhance the cycling stability of LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO cathodes [34] (see Fig. 5).
To investigate the effects mRGO, pRGO and GQDs on the electrochemical kinetic properties of LiCoO2 as cathode, the electrochemical impedance spectra of LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, and LiCoO2/GQDs were acquired and are shown in Fig. 4c. The high-frequency arc is corresponding to the solid electrolyte interface resistance (R f), The semicircle in the high and middle frequency range is referred to the charge-transfer resistance (R ct) on electrode–electrolyte interface, and the inclined line in the lower frequency range represents the Warburg impedance (W O) associated with the diffusion process of lithium ions in the solid phase of LiCoO2 and mRGO, pRGO, or GQDs. Interpretively, a simplified equivalent circuit model, in Fig. 4c, was constructed to analyze the impedance spectra. Table 1 shows the parameters of the R ct for the LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, and LiCoO2/GQDs cathodes obtained through commutating simulations using the Nova software. It can be noted that the R ct values of three composites cathodes are smaller than that of the bare LiCoO2 cathode, implying that mRGO, pRGO, and GQDs can reduce the surface charge transfer resistance. In contrast, the LiCoO2/mRGO shows smallest surface charge transfer resistance than others. These results can be attributed to its large lateral sizes than those of the GQDs which provides larger conductive contact area [6], and the more completed electron conjugate state of the basal plane than pRGO that may affect inherent electron and Li+ diffusions of the RGO sheets.
The Li+ diffusion coefficients (D) within the LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO LiCoO2/GQDs, and LiCoO2 cathodes are determined using a potential-step method (chronoamperometry method). Fig. 4d shows the relation of current density versus charging time for LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO, LiCoO2/GQDs, and LiCoO2 cathodes at +500 mV potential steps. It can be seen that the logarithmic values of the cathodic currents (ln i) decreased rapidly at the beginning. However, ln i is linearly dependent on the charging time after about 800 seconds, which should be closely related to the Li+ diffusion within the cathode. Approximately, it can be considered that the Li+ concentration on the surface of the cathode closes to zero, and the electrochemical reactions within the cathode is controlled by the Li+ diffusion inside the cathode material [44]. The LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs particles can be treated as the spheres with a radius of r, thus, based on Eq. (1) [45], the D values can be calculated. (1) ln i ( t ) = ln [ ( 2 n F A D ) / r ] ( C ∞ − C 0 ) − ( π 2 D t ) / ( 4 r 2 ) where i is the cathode current density, t is the charging time, F is the Faraday constant, A is the area of the electrode, and C∞ and C0 are the surface concentration and initial concentration of Li+ in the bulk of the cathode, respectively, the average r of LiCoO2, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs are almost the same and are about 1 μm (see Fig. 2a–d). The as-derived D values are listed in Table 1. The D values of the three composites cathodes increased obviously than that of the bare LCO cathode owing to the increased Li+ diffusion channels brought by graphene. Comparably, the LiCoO2/GQDs assume relatively larger D value than those of the other two composites, however the reason is not clear for us at moment. The electrochemical performances of LIBs are in dependence of its kinetic properties including Li+ diffusion rate, electron conductivity and so on [46–48]. LCO/GQDs has the largest Li+ diffusion rate but also relative larger charge transfer resistance on electrode-electrolyte interface, which leads to the poorer electrochemical performances.


### CONCLUSION ###

In summary, to get insight the effects of the lateral sizes and the basal plane morphology of the RGO on the electrochemical properties of the LiCoO2/RGO, LiCoO2/mRGO, LiCoO2/pRGO and LiCoO2/GQDs were prepared, and their electrochemical performances as cathodes were evaluated and compared with that of bare LiCoO2. It is demonstrated that the specific capacity, charge/discharge rate capability, and cycling stability of LiCoO2 can be enhanced by coating mRGO, pRGO or GQDs, but the mRGO affects more pronouncedly. The results reveal that nanopores on the basal plane of pRGO disrupt its electron and Li+ conductivity and thus limit its enhancement to the electrochemical performances of LiCoO2 in comparison with the mRGO which assume more completed basal plane electron conjugate state. The GQDs, owing to its shrunken lateral dimensions, cannot cover the surfaces of LiCoO2 as efficient as mRGO that weakens its effect on electrochemical properties of LiCoO2. Consequently, we envisage that the mRGO with relatively large lateral sizes and less basal plane defects should be practically useful as additive to enhance the overall electrochemical properties of LiCoO2 and other cathode materials.
