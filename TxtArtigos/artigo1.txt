### TITLE ###

A novel fluorescence “turn off-on” sensor based on N-doped graphene quantum dots in amino acid ionic liquid medium and its application


### AUTHOR GROUP ###

Wei Liu
Xiashi Zhu


### ABSTRACT ###

The spectrum behavior of N-doped graphene quantum dots (N-GQD) in 1-hexyl-3-methylimidazolium tryptophan ionic liquid (TryIL) was studied. It was showed that TryIL could self-aggregate into water/oil micellar in aqueous solution and N-GQD was assembled on hydrophilic part of TryIL micelle to construct a novel fluorescence “turn on-off” probe (N-GQD-TryIL). the fluorescence intensity was quenched through the formation of N-GQD-TryIL-Cu(II) complex due to the strong binding ability between Cu2+ and N-GQD-TryIL. In the following fluorescence “turn-on” step, the chiral ligand exchange between levodopa (LD) and Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL to partly recovery the fluorescence intensity. Corresponding influencing factors and mechanisms in fluorescence “turn-off” and “turn-on” process were discussed. Thus, a sensitive and selective fluorescence “turn off” and “turn on” platform for assay Cu(II) and LD was designed. Under the optimal conditions, Cu(II) and LD exhibited good linear relationship in the range of 0.051–16.00 μg/mL and 0.55–5.00 μg/mL. The LOD of them was 0.017 μg/mL and 0.16 μg/mL respectively. This method was successfully applied to the determination of Cu(II) and LD in real samples. Moreover, N-GQD-TryIL performed application prospects in bio-imaging due to protein affinity of the hydrophilic part.

### INTRODUCTION ###

Fluorescent “turn-off-on” materials, as an important subject in fluorescence sensing, their fluorescence could be reversibly quenched and recovered under environmental stimulation [1], and they are generally regulated by light, electricity, temperature, machinery and chemistry [2]. Recent evidences indicate that MOFs, poly-acid compounds, organic dyes and quantum dots are the most popular materials for fluorescent “turn-off-on” [3–6]. Graphene quantum dots (GQD) are zero-dimensional (0 D) nanoparticle with a diameter less than 20 nm. Compared with conventional semiconductor quantum dots, GQDs are more environmentally friendly, biocompatible and photo-stable, which widely applied in fluorescence and electrochemical sensing and bio-imaging [4–7]. However, GQDs usually exhibit polycrystalline or highly defective structures with poor optical properties [8]. It has been paid marvelous attention to modify GQD's spectral properties. Wherein, the surface functionalization and element doping were generally employed [9]. Doping GQDs with heteroatoms, provides an attractive mean that effectively tune their intrinsic properties and improve luminous efficiency [10,11].
Recent evidences showed that external medium (such as surfactants, supramolecular, ionic liquids) posed significant influence on quantum dots’ size, composite behavior or electron/energy transfer mechanism [12–15]. Ionic liquids surfactants, which possess properties comparable to amphiphies, including micelle formation and surface activity when the carbon atoms in their hydrophobic tail reached a certain value, could dramatically change the polarity and dielectric constant of the system, which made them widely used in fluorescence sensitization research [16]. Amino acid ionic liquids (AAILs) were serials novel ionic liquids that derived from natural amino acid. Compared with conventional ionic liquids, AAILs had strong polarities, hydrogen bonding basicity, stable chiral center and better biocompatibility [17–19]. However, few researches focused on the ionic liquids (ionic liquids surfactants, amino acid ionic liquids) to improve fluorescence behavior of quantum dots.
In this paper, 1-hexyl-3-methylimidazolium tryptophan ionic liquid (TryIL) was synthesized and utilized as medium to disperse N-doped graphene quantum dots (N-GQD) for the first time. The spectrum behavior of N-GQD in TryIL medium (N-GQD-TryIL) was studied. It could be considered as a fluorescence “turn off” and “turn on” probe for the determination of Cu(II) and Levodopa (LD). Moreover, the application of N-GQD-TryIL in protein imaging was preliminarily studied.


### EXPERIMENT ###



### REAGENTS AND INSTRUMENTS ###

Copper standard solution (1.000 μg/mL): 1.0 mL standard solution (100.0 μg/mL，China) was accurately pipetted and 5.0 mL 10% nitric acid was added into 100.0 mL Volumetric flask. LD solution (200.0 μg/mL): 0.020 g levodopa were accurately weighed, fixed volume of 50.0 mL and stored in 4 °C. Citric acid, ammonia, 1-methylimidazole, bromo-n-hexane, anhydrous ethanol was all analytical purity and provided by Sinopharm Group Regent Co. Ltd., China. TryIL was synthesized by reference method [17,20]. The buffer solution of this experiment was configured by dipotassium hydrogen phosphate and potassium dihydrogen phosphate (Sinopharm Group Regent Co. Ltd, China). The water used throughout this experiment was grade 3 water (resistance >20 MΩ).
Fluorescence spectrum was performed on F-7000 Fluorescence Spectrophotometer (Japan). Thremo-1780 UV Spectrophotometer (USA) was used for UV test. Shimadzu LC-10A High Performance Liquid Chromatography (Japan) was used for Pharmacopoeia method. DK-S22 Constant Temperature Water Bath (Shanghai Precision Instruments Corporation), JEM-2100 TEM (Germany) and Bruker Tensor 27 Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer (Germany) were used for synthesis and characterization.


### N-GQD PREPARATION ###

N-GQDs were synthesized by carbonization of citric acid with ammonia according to the previous reports [21]. 4.20 g of citric acid, 40.0 mL ultrapure water, 4.50 mL of ammonia were transferred into a 100.0 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 8 h. The resultant green mixture was filtered through a 0.22 µm filter and dialyzed against deionized water for 24 h. Then the obtained liquid was diluted with water acquiring N-GQDs stock solution with adsorption 0.50 at 275 nm. Finally, the as-prepared N-GQDs were stored at 4 °C for further characterization and dispersion.


### PREPARATION OF N-GQD-TRYIL ###

50.0 mL N-GQDs stock solution, 0.050 g NaOH, and 0.050 g TryIL was mixed and stirred to clarification, then this mixture was heated under 40 °C in the water bath. After 1 h, the solution changed from green to bright yellow. Then this solution was freeze drying at −86 °C.


### DETERMINATION OF CU(II) BY FLUORESCENCE “TURN-OFF” METHOD ###

0.050 g N-GQD-TryIL was accurately weighed, dissolved and diluted to 100 mL. For fluorescent “turn-off” sensing of Cu(II), 2.00 mL N-GQD-TryIL, 5.00 mL pH = 6.0 buffer solutions, 2.50 mL15% NaF solutions and different volumes of Cu(II) were mixed into a 25.0 mL volumetric flask. The fluorescence spectrum of the solution under excitation at 318 nm was measured at 0 °C, and the peak intensity was recorded (both excitation and emission slits were 5.0 nm, scanning speed was 1200 nm/min).


### DETERMINATION OF LD BY FLUORESCENCE TURN ON METHOD ###

2.00 mL of N-GQD-TryIL solution, 3.00 mL Cu(II) standard solution (5.00 μg/mL), and 1.00 mL test solution was transferred into 25.00 mL volumetric flasks. After sufficient shaking, this solution was kept at 60 °C. The fluorescence spectrum was excited at 318 nm and the peak intensity was recorded (both excitation and emission slits were 5.0 nm and scan speed was 1200 nm/min).


### PREPARATION OF REAL SAMPLES ###



### ORE, SOIL SAMPLES ###

0.20 g ore/soil sample was accurately weighed and transferred into microwave digestion tank, then 1.00 mL HNO3, 3.00 mL HCl and 0.50 mL 30% hydrogen peroxide solution was added. This solution was microwave digestion at 150 °C (temperature rise time 15 min) for 30 min. Then the solution was drained of excess acid at 110 °C and the remaining solution was diluted with water to 25.0 mL.


### LEVODOPA TABLETS ###

10 pieces of levodopa(LD) tables were grinded and mixed, 0.020 g powder was weighed and placed in a 100.0 mL volumetric flask. Then 50.0 mL dilute hydrochloric acid solution was added, shook to dissolve LD.


### VALIDATION PROTOCOL ###

The calibration techniques were evaluated for Cu(II) (0.051–16.00 μg/mL) and LD(0.55–5.00 μg/mL) in aqueous solution. The linearity of the analytical curves was determined applying a linear regression analysis [22].
The limits of detection (LOD) and quantification (LOQ) were evaluated with three and ten times baseline noise (n = 11) divided by the slope of the linear equation from the analytical curve.
The precision was evaluated with repeatability tests and reproducibility tests. The repeatability tests were estimated through of relative standard deviation (RSD), which was detected at cCu = 1.0 μg/mL−1 and cLD = 2.0 μg/mL−1 for 5 times. The reproducibility tests were performed by RSD determined at cCu = 1.0 μg/mL−1 and cLD = 2.0 μg/mL−1 for 5 days [22].
The interference experiment was to analysis for Cu(II) in presence of different concentration of Fe3+, Cd2+, Pb2+, Cr3+, Mg2+, Al3+, Ba2+, Co2+, Na+ and K+, and LD coexisting with dopamine, aspartic acid, glutathione and phenylalanine. When the relative error caused by the interferer reached ± 5.0% [20], the ratio (cinterferer/canalyte) was recorded as interference factor (IF). The larger IF meant better anti-interference ability of this method.
Accuracy of the method was checked using recovery tests with different concentration of the Cu(II) and LD standard solution added into sample solution. And t-test at 95% confidence level was applied to verify the statistical significance of the result obtained by this method and standard certified value or values by standard method.


### RESULTS AND DISCUSSION ###



### DISPERSION BEHAVIOR OF N-GQD IN TRYIL ###

The critical micelle concentration (CMC), zeta potentials and TEM were discussed in the current experiment to characterize the formation of TryIL micelles and the dispersion behavior of N-GQD in TryIL medium.


### CMC, ZETA POTENTIALS ###

CMC is the lowest concentration at which ionic liquids associate in water to form micelles [23]. In this work, CMC values of TryIL and N-GQD-TryIL were determined by conductivity method. As is shown in Fig. 1, the CMC of TryIL is 0.32%, namely TryIL was self-aggregated into micellar when the concentration of TryIL is greater than 0.32%. The CMC of TryIL was decreased to 0.28% when N-GQD was added. It could be speculated that there might be an interaction between TryIL and N-GQD
The zeta potentials of TryIL and N-GQD-TryIL was determined in this experiment. the zeta potential of TryIL moved toward positive from −45.3 mV to −28.7 mV by the addition of N-GQD. The reason may be that N-GQD interacted with TryIL due to the surface charge of TryIL was partly neutralized by N-GQD and N-GQD would be attached onto the surface of TryIL through electrostatic interaction according to the results revealed above [14].


### TEM CHARACTERIZATION ###

TEM and HR-TEM were used to character the morphology of N-GQD and N-GQD-TryIL ( Fig. 2). Fig. 2A, B were TEM, HR-TEM images of N-GQD and C, D of N-GQD-TryIL. Following conclusions could be drawn: 1) N-GQD has been successfully synthesized by their shapes and crystal lattice spacing (0.215 nm); 2) N-GQD were successfully attached to the surface of TryIL micelles.


### THEORETICAL CALCULATION OF N-GQD-TRYIL ###

According to the energy minimum principle, the interaction between N-GQD and TryIL could be simulated by theoretical calculation [20]. The simulation N-GQD and TryIL was displayed in Fig. S1A and B. They were merged and calculated under PM 6 level (scrf = PCM, solvent = water). The resultant morphology was shown in Fig. S1C. It could be inferred that N-GQD could interact with TryIL though π-π interactions and hydrogen bond interactions. This ordered combination of molecules might give N-GQD-TryIL different spectral properties.


### SPECTRAL PROPERTIES OF N-GQD-TRYIL ###

In this section, three-dimensional fluorescence spectroscopy, fluorescence emission spectra, ultraviolet absorption spectroscopy and fluorescence lifetime of N-GQD and N-GQD-TryIL were compared to invest the newly spectral behavior.

This phenomenon might be owing to the reduction of N-GQD aggregation in the micellar environment, causing the red-shift emission wavelength [15].
Fluorescence emission spectra of N-GQD（C）and N-GQD-TryIL(D) at different excitation wavelengths were presented in Fig. 3. The results stated that: (1) N-GQD was excitation-dependent, while N-GQD-TryIL was excitation-independent. N-GQD-TryIL was suitable acted as a fluorescence probe at a certain wavelength [24]; (2) The optimum excitation wavelength for N-GQD-TryIL was 318 nm, and the emission wavelength was 421 nm; (3) The fluorescence intensity could be affected by N-GQD-TryIL concentration. ### UV ABSORPTION SPECTRUM ###

The UV absorption spectrum of N-GQD (H2O as a reference, curve 1) and N-GQD-TryIL (TryIL at the same concentration as a reference, curve 2) were shown in Fig. S2. The maximum absorption of N-GQD peak appeared at 276 nm and 315 nm owing to π-π* and n-π* transition. The maximum absorption of N-GQD-TryIL was significantly differed and red shifted to 282 nm and 337 nm. It might be owing to electrostatic interactions between N-GQD and TryIL [25]. And the results was consistent with 3.1.3.


### FLUORESCENCE QUANTUM YIELDS (ΦF), TIME STABILITY ###

The Φf of N-GQD and N-GQD-TryIL were detected according to reference method [18] (Quinine sulfate was used as a reference with a standard Φf = 55%). The Φf of N-GQD-TryIL (37%) was significant higher than N-GQD (26%). This phenomenon might be owing to the reduction of polycrystalline structure in TryIL medium. The time stability of N-GQD-TryIL was represented by fluorescence intensity within seven days, and the fluorescence intensity were kept steady (RSD = 0.17%), which illustrated that N-GQD-TryIL was stable enough as a fluorescence probe.


### FLUORESCENCE LIFETIME ###

The fluorescence decay curves of were N-GQD (curve 1) and N-GQD-TryIL (curve 2) performed in Fig. S3. Lifetimes (τ1) were calculated by fitting the curves with multiexponential equations described as follows [15]: I ( t ) = A 1 e − t / τ 1 + A 2 e − t / τ 2 τ = A 1 τ 1 2 + A 2 τ 2 2 A 1 τ 1 + A 2 τ 2
As a consequence, the fluorescence decay curves of N-GQD and N-GQD-TryIL were single exponential decay and similar fluorescence lifetime (N-GQD: 3.12 ns, N-GQD-TryIL: 3.40 ns)
In summary, the spectral behavior of N-GQD-TryIL was significantly differed. The N-GQD in TryIL medium had non-excitation wavelength-dependent, higher luminous efficiency, and stability. These advantages made N-GQD-TryIL suitable as a probe for fluorescence sensing and protein imaging.


### N-GQD-TRYIL FLUORESCENCE “TURN OFF/ON PROPERTIES” ###

In this experiment, different concentrations of Cu(II) standard solution were added to the N-GQD-TryIL solution, and the N-GQD-TryIL fluorescence spectrum was recorded under excited wavelength 318 nm. The results were shown in Fig. 4A. The fluorescence intensity continuously “turned off” with the addition of Cu(II). As the LD was added into quenched solution, the fluorescence spectrum of N-GQD-TryIL was partly turned on under the same wavelength (Fig. 4B). Moreover the value of fluorescence “turn-on” accounted for 1/3 of total “turn-off” value. Therefore, N-GQD-TryIL could be employed as a fluorescent “turn off” and “turn on” probe for the quantitative detection of Cu(II) and LD. (Fig. 4C).


### INFLUENCING FACTORS OF FLUORESCENCE “TURN-OFF” PROCESSES ###

The pH, temperature, and ionic strength was the main factors affecting the fluorescence “turn-off” process. The pH (4.0–9.0), temperature (0–60 °C) and ionic strength (0–2.5%) were invested in current experiment respectively. Corresponding fluorescence “turn-off” intensity (ΔF1) was recorded in Fig. S4. The following conclusions could be drawn: 1) As the pH increased, ΔF1 increased firstly, then decreased, and tended to be stable between pH 6.0 and 8.0 (Fig. S4 A). This might result from that the surface functional group of N-GQD-TryIL was deprotonated and more likely to interact with Cu(II) at pH ranging from 6.0 to 8.0. When the pH was larger than 8.0, the hydroxyl side reaction coefficient Cu(II) increased, making it difficult interact with N-GQD-TryIL and leading a decreasing of ΔF1. 2) As the system temperature increased, ΔF1 decreased continuously (Fig. S4 B). It might be explained that the stability of Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL was declined along with the temperature arising. 3) When the solution ionic strength (NaF concentration) was greater than 2.0%, ΔF1 decreased, which was due to the enhancement in NaF concentration causing colloidal coagulation. ### INFLUENCING FACTORS OF FLUORESCENCE TURN ON PROCESS ###

In order to investigate the influence factors of fluorescence turn on process. The intensity of fluorescence “turn-on” (ΔF2) in the range of 5.0–25.0 μg/mL Cu(II) and 0–60 °C were studied. The result was shown in Fig. S5. It could be drawn that the ΔF2 was gradually decreased as the Cu(II) content increasing (Fig. S5A). This phenomenon might be owing to the increasing of Cu(II) concentration, which leads to LD preferentially binding to Cu(II) instead of ligand-exchanging with Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL. When the experimental temperature increased, ΔF2 gradually increased (Fig. S5 B). It could be explained by the decreasing in the stability of Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL.


### EFFECT OF INTERFERING SUBSTANCES ON FLUORESCENCE “TURN-OFF” AND “TURN-ON” ###

The IF of multiple metal ion were performed in Table S1. As the concentration of Fe3+, Cd2+, Pb2+, Cr3+ and Mg2+ reached 5, 8, 12, 20 and 35 times the concentration of Cu(II), they posed influence on Cu(II) detection with 5% relative error [26]. Therefor this method was still practical in most real samples. The same method was employed to invest the interference on fluorescence “turn-on” process. However, there were scarcely substance had influence on the “turn on” process.


### MECHANISM EXPLANATIONS OF FLUORESCENCE “TURN-OFF” AND “TURN-ON” PROCESS ###

The mechanism of N-GQD-TryIL fluorescence “turn off” and “turn on” was studied by thermodynamic parameters, infrared spectroscopy, particle size distribution and theoretical calculations.


### THERMODYNAMIC PARAMETER CALCULATION ###

The quenching/recovery constant Ksv was brought into the Van't Hoff equation at different temperatures, and the ΔH, ΔS, and ΔG during fluorescence turn off/on process were calculated by fitting formulas as follow: F 0 △ F = 1 f a K a [ Q ] + 1 f 1 In K = − ( △ H 0 RT ) + ( △ S 0 R ) △ G 0 = △ H 0 − T △ S 0
The results were shown in Table S2 The following conclusions could be drawn: 1) ΔH ≈ 0, ΔS > 0 indicated that electrostatic interaction might exist during the rurn off/on process [27]; 2) ΔG < 0 indicated that the fluorescence turn off/on was spontaneous.


### INFRARED SPECTRUM ###

The shifting of absorption in the infrared spectrum could reflect the interaction between two species [28]. The infrared spectrum of N-GQD-TryIL and LD with the present or absent of Cu(II) was shown in Fig. S6 A and B. In the presence of Cu2+, the stretching peak of the N-H bond red-shifted, and the C=O stretching peak blue-shifted. It indicated that Cu2+ interacted with the N, O atoms on the surface amino acid of N-GQD-TryIL and LD.


### PARTICLE SIZE DISTRIBUTION ###

Particle size distribution of N-GQD-TryIL, Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL, Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL+LD could be used to determine the microstructure. The particle size analysis results were displayed in Fig. S7. The N-GQD-TryIL was 139.8 nm, but significantly increased to 588.1 nm resulting from the formation of Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL complex through aggregation kinetics mechanism. In the presence of LD, the particle size of the system was reduced (539.4 nm), and a new particle (152 nm), which was close to the size of the N-GQD-TryIL, appeared. Thus the mechanism of turn on could be explained as a ligand exchange process between LD and Cu (II)-N-GQD-TryIL complex to dissociate the complex and partly “turn-on” fluorescence. According to the literature reports [29,30], it could be inferred that Cu(II) might form distorted octahedral complex Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL with three N-GQD-TryIL. The LD molecule underwent a ligand exchange process with Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL to displace an N-GQD-TryIL to turn on the fluorescent of the system partly.


### THEORETICAL CALCULATION ###

In order to verify the formation of Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL and ligand exchange process, DFT theory was used to molecularly simulate the possible morphology at the PM6 level through Gaussian 09 [20]. Under the lowest energy conformation ( Fig. 5), the empty orbit of Cu(II) could interact with N and O in N-GQD-TryIL. This phenomenon was consistent with the increasing size of the Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL in fluorescence turn-off. With the presence of LD，Cu(II)-N-GQD-TryIL was partly dissociated through ligand exchange and the fluorescence of the system was turned on. This conclusion was basically consistent with experiment results in 3.3 and 3.7.3. As a consequence, the chemical reaction between N-GQD-TryIL, Cu(II) and LD might be described as Fig. 6.

### REAL SAMPLES ###

The results of Cu(II) and LD in real samples were shown in Tables 3, 4. The recovery rates of Cu(II) in water samples, ore and soil samples were ranged in 90.8–102.1%, 95.7–104.4%, and 88.5–115.4% respectively. The Cu(II) assay result in the standard water sample (BWZ6616-2016, standard value 0.999 μg/mL) was 0.987 ± 0.02 μg/mL, and there was no significant difference between this result and the standard value. The ore standard sample (GBW07164, standard value 2.80%) was 2.89 ± 0.09% by this method and there was no significant difference between this result and the standard value (t-test).
The recovery rates of LD in the drug and urine samples was 93.7–102.2%. Wherein the drug sample had a consequence of 13.9 mg/g, which was consisted with the results of the pharmacopoeia method (14.1 mg/g, t-test). The results showed that the method could be employed to determine the contents of Cu(II) and LD in various samples with satisfactory analytical results.


### PROTEIN IMAGING ###

The hydrophilic part of N-GQD-TryIL was constructed by amino acid, N-GQD-TryIL has a good application prospect in the field of protein imaging. In this experiment, bovine serum albumin was invoked as a template protein and the imaging results was shown in Fig. 7. Wherein, 1a-1d was bovine serum albumin without N-GQD-TryIL. 2a-2d was pictures of bovine serum albumin after addition of N-GQD only. The bovine serum albumin was difficult to observe under different wavelengths of illumination. 3a-3d and 4a-4d was the bovine serum albumin after adding N-GQD-TryIL. The template protein could be clearly observed under different objective magnification and N-GQD-TryIL could meet the requirements of simultaneous multicolor fluorescent protein imaging. Hence, N-GQD-TryIL had potential application in the field of protein imaging comparing with N-GQD owing to its protein affinity.


### CONCLUSION ###

In summary, N-GQD were firstly dispersed in TryIL medium, and the spectral properties was changed greatly. N-GQD-TryIL could be used as a fluorescence “turn off-on” probe modulated by Cu(II) and LD. Wherein Cu(II) was quencher and LD was fluorescence recovery agent. The method was successfully applied to quantitative analysis of Cu(II) and LD in multiple samples and achieved satisfactory results. Moreover, N-GQD-TryIL had useful application prospects in the field of protein imaging.
